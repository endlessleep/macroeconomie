Chapter 1: The IS-PC-MPR Model

Understanding the structure of Aggregate Supply, Demand, and Monetary Policy

Model Introduction

The macroeconomic model we are studying is built upon three fundamental equations, earning it the name IS-PC-MPR model. This structure allows us to understand how different macroeconomic variables interact with each other.

  • 1. Demand side: IS curve. This explains how output reacts to interest rates.
  • 2. Supply side: Phillips curve. This connects the output level to inflation.
  • 3. Interest rate side: Taylor rule (monetary policy rule). This describes how central banks set interest rates.

Exam Tip: The Big Picture

Always remember that these three blocks do not exist in isolation. A shock to the demand side will affect output, which feeds into the supply side (Phillips curve) to change inflation, which in turn causes the central bank to react via the monetary policy rule.

The Demand Side: The IS Curve

The demand side of the model should tell us why output ($y$) reacts to other variables—specifically, interest rates and inflation. The intuitive proposition is simple but powerful: Output is higher when interest rates are lower. We need to build a model that captures this relationship.

Demand Side Microfoundations

To rigorously prove this intuitive proposition, we look at the decisions of individual actors in the economy:

Consumption Microfoundations

The modern model of consumption links current consumption directly to current interest rates. The key idea here is that a higher interest rate motivates postponing consumption from today into the future (since the reward for saving is higher). Therefore, higher interest rates dampen current demand and output.

Investment Microfoundations

An alternative microfoundation comes from investment. Why would investment depend on interest rates?

  • Saving channel: Analogical to consumption, firms might prefer saving to investing.
  • Borrowing channel: Investment is often financed through external borrowing. The price of this borrowing is the interest rate. Higher interest rates make borrowing expensive, reducing investment.
Nominal vs. Real Interest Rate

What matters for consumption: nominal or real interest rate?

While the visible reward for saving is the nominal interest rate, what truly matters to people is their consumption, not money itself. For future consumption, we need to pay future prices.

Therefore, what matters is the increase in real purchasing power thanks to saving. This means the real interest rate ($r$) is the true driver of demand.

The IS Curve Equation

Assuming that demand for goods and services is given by the behavior described above, we formalize the IS curve:

$y_t = \bar{y}_t - \alpha(r_t - \rho) + \epsilon_t$
$y_t$
Log of total output.
$\bar{y}_t$
Log of the natural level of output (also known as potential output).
$r_t$
The real interest rate.
$\rho$
A natural rate of interest ($\rho > 0$). At this level, interest rates neither stimulate nor dampen consumption.
$\alpha$
Sensitivity parameter ($\alpha > 0$). It implies output is lower when real interest rate is higher. If the interest rate rises by 1 percentage point, output declines by $\alpha$.
$\epsilon_t$
A demand shock. These are movements in output unexplained by changes in natural output or interest rates. Sources include: monetary policy shocks, risk appetite, confidence ("animal spirits"), or external shocks (e.g. recession abroad).

Interpretation and Equilibrium

Consider an equilibrium state featuring:

  • Interest rate $r_t$ equal to its equilibrium (natural) value $\rho$.
  • No shocks ($\epsilon_t = 0$).

Under these conditions, output $y_t$ should be exactly equal to its potential (natural) value $\bar{y}_t$.

When the real interest rate diverges from the natural rate:

  • If $r_t > \rho$: Consumers are incentivized to postpone consumption. Demand falls below the natural level.
  • If $r_t < \rho$: Consumers are incentivized to advance consumption. Demand rises above the natural level.

The Supply Side: The Phillips Curve

The supply side of our model takes the form of the Phillips curve (PC). This equation models how production levels feed into price dynamics.

$\pi_t = \pi^e_t + \phi(y_t - \bar{y}_t) + \nu_t$

The core message of the supply equation is that inflation is positively related to output. The microfoundations for this relationship include price stickiness, wage stickiness, or imperfect information.

  • $\pi^e_t$ (Expected Inflation): Inflation is driven in part by what people expect it to be in the future.
  • $(y_t - \bar{y}_t)$ (Output Gap): This is the difference between actual and potential output.
  • $\phi$ (Sensitivity): ($\phi > 0$). It represents the sensitivity of inflation to output fluctuations.
  • $\nu_t$ (Supply Shock): Exogenous shocks to prices (e.g., sudden oil price spikes).

Interest Rate Side: Monetary Policy Rule

Our IS curve links output to the real interest rate. We need to understand how real interest rates are actually determined. This is a two-step process:

  1. Determine nominal interest rates.
  2. Determine real interest rates as a function of nominal rates.

Determination of Nominal Interest Rates

In modern economies, nominal interest rates are set by the central bank. The central bank decides what nominal interest rates it wishes to have. Through various operating procedures (implementation via repo rates or adjustment of supply of bank reserves), it ensures these specific interest rates prevail.

Historical Note: Old Keynesian Approach

The alternative, older view was that the interest rate is a "price" of money determined by a market with a demand and supply of money. The modern approach we use assumes the central bank simply dictates the rate.

The Monetary Policy Rule (Taylor Rule)

We need a systematic description of central bank behavior. We assume monetary policy reacts to excess inflation and excess output:

$i_t = \pi_t + \rho + \theta_\pi (\pi_t - \pi^*_t) + \theta_y (y_t - \bar{y}_t)$

This rule captures the idea that the central bank:

  • Has an inflation target ($\pi^*_t$) and acts to ensure inflation stays close to it. (Inflation targeting is standard since the 1990s).
  • Dislikes recessions and output volatility.

When current inflation $\pi_t > \pi^*_t$ or when $y_t > \bar{y}_t$, the central bank increases interest rates.

Parametrization and The Taylor Principle

Monetary policy rate depends on 4 variables (actual/target inflation, actual/potential output) and 2 key parameters ($\theta_\pi$ and $\theta_y$).

The Taylor Principle: The assumption that $\theta_\pi > 0$ is crucial. It ensures that nominal interest rates move more than one-for-one with inflation. Thus, an increase in inflation leads to an increase in the real interest rate (which cools down the economy via the IS curve). This gives the rule its stabilization property.

The Rule with Smoothing

The standard rule suggests the central bank reacts instantly to any fluctuation. This is unreasonable. An alternative specification includes smoothing:

$i_t = \delta[\pi_t + \rho + \theta_\pi (\pi_t - \pi^*_t) + \theta_y (y_t - \bar{y}_t)] + (1-\delta)i_{t-1}$

The interest rate is a weighted average of the past interest rate and the rate implied by the Taylor rule. The central bank slowly adjusts towards the target rate (though during crises like recessions, they might react very quickly in reality).

The Fisher Equation and Expected Inflation

The Monetary policy rule determines the nominal interest rate. To get the real rate (which accounts for change in prices and measures purchasing power), we use the ex-ante Fisher equation:

$r_t = i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1}$

It depends on expected inflation. How do we determine expectations? We assume adaptive (backward-looking) expectations:

$E_t \pi_{t+1} = \pi_t$

Alternative theories exist, such as anchored expectations ($E_t \pi_{t+1} - \pi^* = \psi(\pi_t - \pi^*)$) or rational/model-consistent expectations.

Control of Rates: Short-term vs Long-term

Central banks typically focus on short-term rates (daily/weekly). However, for firms and households, long-term rates matter more. Based on arbitrage (expectations hypothesis), long-term rates should be equal to the expected average of future short-term rates. Thus, the central bank steers long-term rates through short-term ones. Sometimes they intervene directly via Quantitative Easing.

Control of Rates: Nominal vs Real

The central bank controls nominal rates. If expected inflation is pre-defined due to adaptive expectations, controlling nominal rates equals controlling real rates. In reality, expected inflation also depends on the central bank's actions, making things complicated.

Model Summary & Simulation

The purpose of macroeconomic models is to determine current and future values of model variables. To understand the machinery, we must distinguish between variables and parameters.

Types of Variables

  • Endogenous variables: Determined by the model.
  • Exogenous variables: Enter the model, but are not determined by it.
  • Pre-determined endogenous: Endogenous in principle, but from current perspective are already determined.

Variables vs. Parameters

A parameter is essentially a number that is not fixed, but can be changed. Variables change as a result of shocks; changes in parameters are shocks.

Setting Model Parameters

To use the model for simulations, we need to specify parameter values. We can either:

  • Estimate them: Finding values that lead to the best model fit (often via Bayesian methods since mid-2000s).
  • Calibrate them: Finding values that allow the model to match salient features of the data (common in the 80s/90s).

Model Parameter Values for Simulations

We will use the following default values:

  • $\alpha = 1$: Real rate moves output 1-for-1.
  • $\rho = 2$: Natural rate is 2.
  • $\phi = 0.25$: Inflation increases 0.25 percentage points for a 1 percentage point increase in the output gap.
  • $\theta_y = 0.5$ and $\theta_\pi = 0.5$: Real rates react equally to inflation and the output gap.
  • Normalized potential output $\bar{y} = 100$ and common inflation target $\pi^*_t = 2$.
