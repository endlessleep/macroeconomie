1. The Demand Side: The IS Curve

The macroeconomic model is built on three equations: the Demand side (IS curve), the Supply side (Phillips curve), and the Interest rate side (Monetary Policy Rule). The demand side of the model tells us why output reacts to other variables, mainly interest rates and inflation.

The intuitive proposition is that output is higher when interest rates are lower. We want to formulate a model of this exact relationship.

Microfoundations of Demand

Consumption Microfoundations

Modern models tightly link current consumption decisions to current interest rates. A higher interest rate motivates postponing consumption from today into the future. This acts as our model of the relationship between aggregate demand and the interest rate.

Investment Microfoundations

Alternatively, investment also logically depends on interest rates. Through a saving channel, it functions fundamentally like consumption. Through a borrowing channel, it acts as the external financing price. A higher interest rate increases the cost of external borrowing, dropping overall investment.

Nominal vs. Real Interest Rate

What specifically matters for consumption—the nominal or the real interest rate?

While the reward for saving is technically the nominal interest rate, what consumers ultimately care about is actual consumption, not money. For future consumption, consumers must pay future prices. Therefore, what practically matters is the true increase in real purchasing power achieved through saving.

Conclusively, what drives demand is the real interest rate.

The IS Curve Equation

Assuming that the total demand for goods and services determines short-term output, the model dictates:

$y_t = \bar{y}_t - \alpha(r_t - \rho) + \epsilon_t$

The equation uses log variables, where $y_t$ is total output, $\bar{y}_t$ is the natural level of output, $r_t$ is the real interest rate, $\rho > 0$ is the natural rate of interest, and $\alpha > 0$ strictly implies output declines when the interest rate rises. $\epsilon_t$ encompasses all demand shocks (e.g., sentiment, government spending).

Interpretation: Consider the equilibrium, which naturally features no shocks ($\epsilon_t = 0$), and an interest rate equal to its natural value $\rho$. Consequently, output $y_t$ sits precisely at its natural potential $\bar{y}_t$. When the interest rate rises by 1 percentage point, consumers are incentivized to postpone consumption, so that demand is critically below the natural level, causing output to decline by $\alpha$.

Visualizations & Shocks

A change in interest rates manifests as movement strictly along the IS curve. Conversely, positive or negative demand shocks rigidly shift the entire curve. Demand shocks operate as a residual category, encompassing unknown factors such as the risk appetite of households, their generalized confidence ("animal spirits"), or completely external demand shocks (e.g., a recession abroad).

2. The Supply Side: The Phillips Curve

The supply side dictates how inflation behaves, taking the physical form of the Phillips Curve (PC).

$\pi_t = \pi^e_t + \phi(y_t - \bar{y}_t) + \nu_t$

The core logic of the supply equation fundamentally dictates that inflation ($\pi_t$) is positively related to output. Within the equation, $\pi^e_t$ represents expected inflation, and the term $(y_t - \bar{y}_t)$ establishes the 'output gap'. The parameter $\phi > 0$ captures precisely the absolute sensitivity of inflation to output fluctuations. Finally, $\nu_t$ encapsulates any supply shock.

Microfoundations

The structural microeconomic foundations causing this rigid relationship include: price stickiness, wage stickiness, or systemic imperfect information.

3. Interest Rates & Model Synthesis

Our IS curve directly links output exclusively to the real interest rate. Therefore, we rigidly need to determine how nominal interest rates are fundamentally established, and how they convert predictably into real interest rates.

The Monetary Policy Rule

In modern economies, nominal interest rates are entirely set by the central bank. The central bank decisively dictates what nominal rates it wishes to project, then ensures and implements these rates through various tight operating procedures (via repo rates or rigid supply adjustments of bank reserves).

We assume strictly that monetary policy behaves entirely systematically, which is perfectly understood by all economic agents. We utilize the Monetary Policy Rule (Taylor Rule):

$i_t = \pi_t + \rho + \theta_\pi (\pi_t - \pi^*_t) + \theta_y (y_t - \bar{y}_t)$

The Taylor rule formally captures the ideas that the central bank: (1) enforces a strict inflation target $\pi^*_t$, (2) acts resolutely to guarantee inflation stays close exclusively to that target, and (3) completely dislikes severe recessions. When observing $\pi_t > \pi^*_t$ or calculating $y_t > \bar{y}_t$, the central bank automatically increases rates mechanically.

The Taylor Principle & Smoothing Logic

The mandatory assumption $\theta_\pi > 0$ strictly guarantees that interest rates move faster and more aggressively than inflation. This forces the true real interest rate functionally to increase in response to an inflation shock, triggering a push back firmly towards the target. This is categorized formally as the Taylor principle.

Smoothing: The base formula technically implies the bank rigidly reacts immediately to arbitrary small fluctuations identically, which is practically unreasonable. Modern smoothing parameters dictating the central bank slowly weight current targets alongside historical past rates.

The Fisher Equation & Expectations

To acquire the real interest rate acting as the linchpin for the IS framework, the model utilizes firmly the Fisher equation:

$r_t = i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1}$

This ex-ante rate depends strictly on Expected Inflation ($E_t\pi_{t+1}$). This requires assuming behavior, typically adaptive (purely backward-looking) expectations ($E_t\pi_{t+1} = \pi_t$). Alternative structures imply tightly anchored expectations based upon the rigorous central bank target, or entirely rational model-consistent expectations where intelligent agents utilize complex mathematical information completely.

Control of Rates

Central Banks target exclusively short-term active rates. However, long-term interest rates impact fundamentally the majority of firms and households. Relying on strict structural arbitrage, foundational long-term rates equate strictly to completely expected averages of all successive short-term interest rates cumulatively (expectations hypothesis). Therefore, the central bank purposefully steers everything through the short-term foundation.
