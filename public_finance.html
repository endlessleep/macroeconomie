Public Finance & Government Debt

Government Role, Budget Constraints, Debt Sustainability & Borrowing Limits

1. The Role of Government

Three Core Functions
Provision

Providing public goods and services (education, defence, health) that the market would underprovide.

Redistribution

Redistributing income and wealth across households through transfers, taxes and social programmes.

Stabilization

Macroeconomic stabilization — smoothing the business cycle through fiscal and monetary policy.

The size of government is most commonly measured by the ratio of general government expenditure to GDP.

35–55%
of GDP — Typical range in developed economies
~43%

⚠️ Distinction importante : The general government is broader than the state budget. It includes central government, regional governments, municipalities, and social security funds. In the Czech Republic, general government is ~43% of GDP vs. ~30% for the state budget alone.

2. Government Revenues & Expenditures

Types of Government Revenues

  • Taxes on income & wealth: personal income tax, corporate tax, property taxes.
  • Taxes on goods & services: VAT, excise taxes, customs duties.
  • Social contributions: financing pensions, healthcare and social insurance.
  • Other revenues: fees, fines, dividends from state-owned companies, EU funds.

Types of Government Expenditures

  • Purchases of goods & services: public sector wages, education, health, defence.
  • Transfer payments: pensions, unemployment benefits, social assistance.
  • Public investment: infrastructure, schools, hospitals and long-term assets.
  • Subsidies & grants: financial support to firms, households or local governments.

3. Budget Deficits

A budget deficit occurs when government expenditures exceed government revenues in a given period. When revenues are insufficient, governments finance deficits by borrowing — typically by issuing government bonds.

Budget Deficit Formula
$$\text{Budget Deficit} = G + TR + iB - T$$
$G$ = government purchases  |  $TR$ = transfer payments  |  $iB$ = interest payments on existing debt  |  $T$ = government revenues

The accumulation of past deficits increases the stock of public debt. It is important to distinguish between different types of deficit:

Primary Deficit

Excludes interest payments on existing debt ($iB$). Measures the underlying fiscal imbalance before debt service costs.

Total Deficit

Includes interest payments ($iB$). This is the complete measure of how much the government must borrow in a given year.

Structural Deficit

Reflects the underlying fiscal position after adjusting for the business cycle — the deficit that would persist at full employment.

Cyclical Deficit

Caused by temporary fluctuations in output, employment and tax revenues. It disappears when the economy returns to potential.

4. Public Debt

Public debt is the stock of accumulated past government deficits. The most widely used indicator is the debt-to-GDP ratio:

Key Indicator
$$\frac{B_t}{Y_t} = \text{Debt-to-GDP Ratio}$$
A higher ratio may increase interest costs and reduce the government's fiscal space (room to manoeuvre with policy).

Debt sustainability depends on four interconnected factors:

Economic growth — fast GDP growth increases the tax base and makes a given stock of debt easier to service.

Interest rates — if $i > g$ (interest exceeds growth), the debt-to-GDP ratio tends to grow automatically.

Inflation — can erode the real value of debt, but also triggers higher nominal yields if investors anticipate it.

Future primary balances — the government's ability and credibility in generating future surpluses.

5. Budget Constraints

Flow Version (Period-by-Period)

Sources of government funds must equal uses of government funds at every point in time:

Government Flow Budget Constraint
$$\underbrace{G_t + TR_t + iB_t}_{\text{Uses of funds}} = \underbrace{T_t + \Delta B_{t+1} + \Delta M_{t+1}}_{\text{Sources of funds}}$$
$G_t$ Purchases $TR_t$ Transfers $iB_t$ Interest payments $T_t$ Tax revenues $\Delta B_{t+1}$ New borrowing $\Delta M_{t+1}$ Money creation

In other words: government expenditures must be covered by taxes, borrowing, or money creation.

Intertemporal Budget Constraint

Assume: (1) no money creation, (2) no transfers for simplicity, (3) interest payments separated from the primary deficit. Then the government's period-by-period constraint is:

Period-by-period debt accumulation
$$B_{t+1} = (1+i)B_t + \underbrace{G_t - T_t}_{\text{primary deficit}}$$

For a two-period economy (periods 1 and 2), starting from initial debt $B_1$:

Period 2 debt
$$B_2 = (1+i)B_1 + G_1 - T_1$$

Combining both periods and imposing no debt at the end ($B_3 = 0$, the government cannot borrow forever), we obtain the intertemporal budget constraint:

★ Intertemporal Budget Constraint (2-period economy)
$$\underbrace{G_1 + \frac{G_2}{1+i}}_{\text{PDV of spending}} + \underbrace{(1+i)B_1}_{\text{initial debt}} = \underbrace{T_1 + \frac{T_2}{1+i}}_{\text{PDV of taxes}}$$
PDV Present Discounted Value $B_1$ Initial debt stock $i$ Interest rate

Key insight from the intertemporal constraint

  • The government must eventually raise enough taxes (in present value) to cover all spending and repay its initial debt.
  • Today's deficit means higher future taxes or lower future spending — the burden is shifted across time, not eliminated.
  • There is no free lunch: if $G_1 > T_1$, then we need $T_2 > G_2$ to satisfy the constraint.

6. How Much Can the Government Actually Borrow?

There is no fixed debt-to-GDP ratio at which a crisis must occur. Sustainability is multidimensional and context-dependent.

Growth rate: When GDP grows faster than debt, the debt-to-GDP ratio falls even if nominal debt rises.

Credibility: Investors must trust the government's future fiscal commitments and institutional quality.

Taxing capacity: The ability to generate future primary surpluses is crucial for long-run solvency.

Interest vs. growth ($i$ vs $g$): If $g > i$, the debt ratio tends to stabilise or fall automatically.

Economic Growth & the Debt-to-GDP Ratio

What matters is not the absolute level of debt, but debt relative to GDP:

The Crucial Ratio
$$\frac{B_t}{Y_t}$$
This ratio can fall even when nominal debt $B_t$ rises — provided that $Y_t$ (GDP) grows faster than debt. Economic growth expands the tax base, making a given stock of debt easier to service.

Generational Accounting

Intergenerational Burden

Government debt shifts part of the cost of current spending into the future. Future generations may have to pay higher taxes or receive lower benefits.

Borrowing is not necessarily bad if it finances productive public investment (infrastructure, education) — future generations will inherit both the liabilities and the valuable assets.

The key normative question: Do future generations inherit only debt, or also the public capital that justifies it?

Deficits and Investment (Crowding Out)

From national income accounting, investment must be financed by the sum of private saving, government saving, and foreign saving:

$(Y - T - C) + (T - G) + (IM - EX) = I$
$Y-T-C$ = Private saving $T-G$ = Government saving $IM-EX$ = Foreign saving $I$ = Investment
Standard View: Crowding Out

A higher budget deficit reduces government saving $(T - G)$. If private or foreign saving does not rise to compensate, investment falls.

Ricardian Equivalence

Under Ricardian equivalence, forward-looking households anticipate higher future taxes and increase private saving today, so investment need not decline.

7. Troubles on the Horizon — Can Countries Go Bankrupt?

If debt becomes too large, investors may doubt the government's ability or willingness to repay. This leads to a feedback loop:

The debt spiral: Rising investor doubt → higher risk premia on government bonds → higher borrowing costs → worse debt dynamics → more doubt. Governments under severe fiscal pressure may need to: cut spending, raise taxes, generate inflation, restructure debt, or — in extreme cases — default.

Inflation as an Implicit Tax

Inflation & Debt Dynamics

Inflation can act as an implicit tax on debt holders: it lowers the real value of outstanding nominal debt and reduces the real burden of fixed interest payments.

Bracket creep (taxflation): Inflation pushes nominal incomes into higher tax brackets even when real incomes are unchanged, raising tax revenues without explicit tax hikes.

But — important caveats:

  • This only works for debt issued in domestic currency and not indexed to inflation.
  • If investors expect higher inflation, they demand higher nominal yields, which can offset the benefit and damage credibility.

Sovereign Default — Key Factors

Sovereign states do not go bankrupt like private firms, but they can lose market access, restructure debt, or default. What matters for sustainability:

Credibility of fiscal policy

Economic growth relative to interest rates ($g$ vs $i$)

Currency denomination of debt (own currency = more flexibility)

Investor confidence and institutional quality

Ability to generate future primary surpluses

Case Study: Argentina 2001–2002

Argentina 2001–2002 — A Debt Crisis Blueprint

  • Argentina had a fixed exchange-rate regime: the peso was pegged to the USD (currency board).
  • A prolonged recession reduced tax revenues, making debt servicing increasingly difficult.
  • Investors lost confidence, risk premia rose, and Argentina lost market access.
  • The government could no longer refinance its debt and defaulted in December 2001 — the largest sovereign default in history at the time (~$100bn).
  • The crisis triggered: devaluation of the peso (end of convertibility), a severe banking crisis, hyperinflation, and a deep fall in output.
  • Costs of default: difficult debt restructuring, restricted future borrowing, very high risk premia, and strict IMF conditionality.
Key lesson: Default is not caused by debt alone, but by debt interacting with weak growth, credibility problems, currency mismatches, and financing constraints.

8. What Have We Learnt?

Final Lecture Summary — Key Takeaways

  • Governments affect the economy through public spending, taxation, redistribution and stabilization policy.
  • Budget deficits arise when government spending exceeds revenues ($G + TR + iB > T$).
  • Public debt is the accumulation of past total deficits.
  • The total deficit includes interest payments; the primary deficit excludes them.
  • Debt sustainability depends on interest rates, economic growth and future primary balances.
  • A high debt-to-GDP ratio is not automatically a crisis — context and credibility matter enormously.
  • Government borrowing can crowd out investment, unless private or foreign saving adjusts (Ricardian equivalence).
  • The intertemporal constraint means current deficits imply higher future taxes or lower future spending — there is no free lunch.
