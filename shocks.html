Shock Response Simulations

1. Why Simulate Shocks?

While the DAD-DAS diagram is an elegant geometric tool for mapping out basic shock movements, it suffers from severe limitations when we try to analyze complex dynamic behavior across time. Specifically, it only maps output and inflation, rendering the critical underlying movements of interest rates (both nominal and real) completely invisible.

Furthermore, a static piece of paper cannot naturally capture the source of dynamic adaptations over multiple periods. Therefore, to truly master the macroeconomic machine, we must utilize Shock Response Simulations (Impulse Response Functions). These allow us to trace the chronological chain reaction of every single variable simultaneously.

Part I: Supply Shocks

A supply shock ($\nu_t$) is mathematically defined as anything that abruptly increases domestic inflation without directly increasing physical output. Real-world examples primarily include commodity price spikes (like an oil crisis), massive sudden wage renegotiations across the board, or skyrocketing financial intermediation costs during a banking crash.

The Mechanics of a Supply Shock Reaction

When a supply shock forcefully hits, our foundational equations trigger a relentless chronological chain reaction. Here is the step-by-step unrolling of the mechanism, explicitly detailing exactly why variables shift:

  1. Initial Strike: The supply shock fiercely raises inflation physically on the ground. However, DAD-DAS graphing shows us immediately that output simultaneously falls. Why?
  2. The CB Strikes Back: The central bank (CB) witnesses inflation brutally exceeding their target line ($\pi_t > \pi^*_t$). Following their strict mandated Taylor mapping, they instantly aggressively hike the nominal interest rate ($i_t$).
  3. The Real Rate Spikes: Because the CB is hawkish (following the Taylor principle), they increase the nominal rate strictly more than the sheer increase in inflation itself. Mathematically, this forcefully drives the real interest rate ($r_t$) upward above its natural neutral resting anchor ($\rho$).
  4. Demand Collapses: Confronted with incredibly expensive real borrowing costs, businesses scrap expansion plans, and households stop making major purchases. Aggregate demand plummets. Output $y_t$ is choked down severely below potential $\bar{y}_t$.

The Crucial Stabilization Paradox

Notice carefully: The sheer initial inflation spike was actually smaller than the raw mathematical size of the supply shock itself. Why? Because the very instant the shock hit, the Central Bank violently pulled the emergency brake on aggregate demand. This immediate, engineered drop in demand immediately applied a massive counter-weight (downward pressure) on prices, partially muting the total shock impact.

Dynamic Adjustments: After the Initial Impact

What mathematically occurs in the periods following the initial strike ($t+1$, $t+2$)?

Even though the physical external shock ($\nu_t$) eventually zeroes out and dissipates, inflation stubbornly remains hovering above the target. Why? Because of Expected Inflation. People vividly remember recent high inflation ($\pi_{t-1}$) and bake it into their current pricing requests. Thus, inflation is kept aloft purely by mathematical societal memory.

Consequently, because inflation memory keeps inflation artificially high, the Central Bank is mandated to forcefully keep the real interest rate uncomfortably high. Output remains painfully suppressed.

The Central Bank's Agonizing Trade-Off

Herein lies the ultimate macroeconomic tragedy of supply shocks. To force inflation firmly back to the target, the Central Bank functionally must deliberately engineer a recession. They deliberately orchestrate weak demand simply to coerce firms to stop hiking prices.

The Ultra-Aggressive Bank

Could the CB theoretically ensure inflation never spikes at all? Mathematically, yes! They could unleash interest rates to catastrophic levels instantly. The outcome? Perfect zero inflation, but an apocalypse-level depression resulting in immediate massive societal unemployment. Their $\theta_\pi$ parameter would inherently be gigantic.

The Navigational Trade-Off

Real Central Banks constantly navigate a delicate ratio map. The strictness configured in their Taylor Rule (parameters $\theta_\pi$ vs $\theta_y$) entirely governs the Sacrifice Ratio: "How enormous of a recession are we politically willing to induce to kill inflation quickly?"

What if the Central Bank is too weak? (The Great Inflation)

Imagine a scenario where the parameter is inverted: $\theta_\pi < 0$. When inflation hits, the CB only nominally raises interest rates, but less than the magnitude of the inflation spike. This triggers a total economic catastrophe:

  • The absolutely crucial real interest rate fundamentally declines.
  • Experiencing dropping real borrowing costs, households and businesses borrow furiously, radically increasing Output.
  • Overstimulated output triggers immense demand-pull forces, adding massive new inflation right on top of the original supply shock.
  • The DAD curve fundamentally snaps and becomes upward sloping. The model loses its mathematical anchor, spiraling into total systemic hyperinflation. (Similar to phenomena witnessed in the 1970s).

The Role of Societal Expectations

Why is the CB forced into orchestrating a recession? Exclusively because they are terrified of inflation expectations de-anchoring. The entire recession is just a tool to crush the societal assumption that prices will keep rising (breaking the $E_t\pi_{t+1}$ loop).

If societal expectations are firmly "anchored" (people fundamentally trust the CB to fix it, so they mentally ignore the temporary spike), inflation converges completely organically back to the baseline without the Central Bank even having to inflict a recession! This perfectly explains the Post-Pandemic era where inflation somewhat normalized despite deeply negative real interest rates.

Lesson: Pure backward-looking (adaptive) expectations inherently create severe instability requiring brutal CB intervention. Forward-looking, rational, anchored expectations synthesize stability naturally.

Part II: Demand Shocks

A demand shock ($\epsilon_t$) is starkly defined as anything that intrinsically forces output to climb without explicitly originating from prices. Examples include huge government fiscal spending programs, sudden surges in consumer confidence, massive stock-market wealth generation, or exploding export requests from foreign nations.

The Mechanics of a Demand Shock Reaction

Unlike a supply shock that initially hurts output, a demand shock looks fantastic on the surface—but still triggers the Central Bank's wrath.

  1. Initial Strike: The demand shock massively amps up output. Instantly, factories overheat and labor gets scarce, triggering the demand-pull mechanism, which mathematically forces inflation upwards.
  2. The CB Overrides the Party: The central bank witnesses BOTH output overheating ($y_t > \bar{y}_t$) AND inflation exceeding target ($\pi_t > \pi^*_t$). Both variables scream "Danger!". The CB aggressively hikes rates to cool the economy.
  3. Muted Boom: Because the CB actively fights the boom from day one, the total absolute final increase in societal output is actually far smaller than the raw mathematical size of the initial demand shock itself.

The Morning After: Post-Shock Dynamics

What happens when the massive confidence surge or government spending spree abruptly ends? Output should simply drop exactly back to normal. However, the system is now heavily scarred.

Because inflation was previously pulled high during the boom, societal inflation expectations are now deeply elevated. Although physical demand has vanished, inflation memory forces actual inflation to continue resting uncomfortably above target (DAS shifted up).

The Hangover Recession

Because society is currently expecting high inflation, the Central Bank is mandated to continue enforcing a deeply restrictive policy (keeping the real interest rate brutally high above the neutral rate) long after the boom has ended. By doing this, they physically crush an initially normal economy into a calculated recession ($y_t < \bar{y}_t$) purely to starve out the lingering inflation memory generated by yesterday's party.

If the demand shock lasts for multiple continuous periods, inflation doesn't just bump—it relentlessly compounds. The CB reacts by jacking nominal rates successively higher and higher. Paradoxically, during a sustained demand shock, sheer output will actively start declining mid-boom, purely because the compounding interest rates finally suffocate the expansion completely.

Part III: Monetary Policy Shocks

Finally, we analyze the Central Bank sabotaging itself. A Monetary Policy shock ($\eta_t$) is rigorously defined as an exogenous deviation completely unjustified by the standard rigid formulas. Examples: Incompetently misjudging the physical neutral rate, an arbitrary political decision to change the target goalpost, or erratic, panicked rate tweaking.

1. The Policy Rate Mistake (The "Commission" Error)

Imagine the CB inexplicably drops the nominal interest rate without justification ($\eta_t < 0$). This sudden plunge physically crushes the real interest rate framework. Cheap money furiously floods the system.

Output violently skyrockets, dragging inflation right up behind it (demand-pull). The moment this occurs, the CB realizes its catastrophic error and instantly scrambles to jack rates incredibly high above baseline to slaughter the very monster it just birthed.

Crucial Insight: Geometrically and mathematically, a pure arbitrary policy rate shock is technically completely identical to a standard Demand Shock across almost all variables!

2. The Natural Rate Shock (The "Omission" Error)

This is extremely subtle. Assume a massive underlying demographic shift or a tech slowdown permanently drops the unobservable "Natural Interest Rate" ($\rho$) globally. However, the rigid Central Bank doesn't realize it and keeps fighting at the old baseline.

Because the true natural rate collapsed, the CB's "normal" rates are now suddenly, accidentally massively restrictive. Without doing anything, the CB inherently causes output to plummet and inflation to crater. Once they finally notice and catch up, they slash nominal rates severely, eventually finding the new lower baseline. Empirical evidence heavily suggests the global natural rate has been steadily collapsing for the past decade globally due to aging populations and a "global savings glut".

3. The Disinflation Shock (Changing the Target)

What if the CB deliberately lowers the mathematical $\pi^*_t$ target? To force society down to the new target, they explicitly engineer a severe recession mapping, hiking rates intensely while inflation is technically "normal" according to the old scale. This is precisely Paul Volcker's historical playbook. The absolute cost (length of the recession) depends entirely on how quickly society visually 'believes' and maps to the new target psychologically (Expectations Anchoring).

Conclusion: The Grand Overview

This Dynamic Macro Model introduces fundamental systemic upgrades compared to elementary AS-AD models. The inclusion of an explicit, proactive Monetary Policy Rule explicitly reveals that the trajectory of a modern economy fundamentally depends on the psychological aggressiveness (Taylor parameters) of the Central Bank mapping the system.

The Eternal Tradeoffs

Following a supply wound, the CB faces a political nightmare: Crashing the economy physically to coerce price stabilization mathematically. Following demand fevers, they proactively engineer icy recessions simply to kill psychological momentum gaps.

Expectations Rule All

The severity of every single mapped trauma is entirely predicated on societal memory. Pure backward-looking (adaptive) memory causes profound geometric lagging crises. Advanced models shifting towards strictly forward-looking "rational" expectations drastically dilute these traumatic geometric phases.
