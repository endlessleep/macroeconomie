Seminar 2: Other Equations

DMM — Phillips Curve, Taylor Rule, Fisher Equation & Equilibrium

Exercise 1

The Phillips Curve

Key Formula

$\pi_t = \pi_t^e + \phi(y_t - \bar{y}_t) + \nu_t$

where $\pi_t$ is inflation, $\pi_t^e$ is expected inflation, $(y_t - \bar{y}_t)$ is the output gap, $\phi > 0$ measures how sensitive inflation is to the output gap, and $\nu_t$ is a supply shock.

  1. Why is inflation related to the output gap?
  2. If the economy is at potential output and there are no supply shocks, what determines inflation?
Show Solution
Question 1 — Inflation and the Output Gap

The output gap reflects the balance between demand and the economy's productive capacity.

When output is above potential ($y_t > \bar{y}_t$), the economy is overheating. Firms face strong demand and may run into capacity limits (not enough workers, machines running at full speed). Because demand is high relative to supply, firms can raise their prices → inflation increases.

At the same time, tight labor markets push wages up, which raises production costs and further increases prices.

When output is below potential ($y_t < \bar{y}_t$), there is unused capacity. Demand is weak, and firms struggle to sell their products. They are less able to raise prices and may even lower them → inflation decreases.

$y_t > \bar{y}_t \implies \pi_t \uparrow \qquad \text{and} \qquad y_t < \bar{y}_t \implies \pi_t \downarrow$
Question 2 — At Potential, No Shocks

If $y_t = \bar{y}_t$ (output gap = 0) and $\nu_t = 0$ (no supply shock), the Phillips curve simplifies to:

$\pi_t = \pi_t^e$

Inflation is entirely determined by inflation expectations. If people expect high inflation, inflation will be high. If they expect low inflation, inflation will be low.

This is why central banks work so hard to keep expectations "anchored" near their target.

Exercise 2

Taylor Monetary Policy Rule

Key Formula

$i_t = \pi_t + \rho + \theta_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*_t) + \theta_y(y_t - \bar{y}_t)$

  1. Why must $\theta_\pi > 0$ for the economy to be stable? (Taylor Principle)
  2. Explain what happens if $\theta_\pi < 0$.
  3. If $\theta_y = 0$, how does the CB respond to recessions?
  4. Compare two central banks: Bank A has $\theta_\pi = 1.5$, $\theta_y = 0.5$ while Bank B has $\theta_\pi = 0.5$, $\theta_y = 1.5$.
Show Solution
Q1 — The Taylor Principle ($\theta_\pi > 0$)

The Taylor principle says the central bank must raise interest rates when inflation rises above the target.

If inflation increases and the CB raises the nominal rate enough, the real interest rate also increases. A higher real rate reduces consumption and investment → aggregate demand falls → output drops → inflation decreases through the Phillips curve.

$\pi_t \uparrow \implies i_t \uparrow\uparrow \implies r_t \uparrow \implies y_t \downarrow \implies \pi_t \downarrow$

This mechanism brings inflation back toward the target. The economy is stable.

Q2 — What if $\theta_\pi < 0$?

If $\theta_\pi < 0$, the CB lowers interest rates when inflation increases. This creates an inflationary spiral:

$\pi_t \uparrow \implies i_t \downarrow \implies r_t \downarrow \implies y_t \uparrow \implies \pi_t \uparrow\uparrow$

Inflation keeps growing instead of stabilizing. The economy becomes unstable.

Q3 — $\theta_y = 0$ (No Output Reaction)

If $\theta_y = 0$, the CB only reacts to inflation, not to the output gap.

During a recession ($y_t < \bar{y}_t$), the CB will lower rates only if inflation also falls below target. If inflation stays near target, the CB does nothing — even if output is low.

This is a pure inflation-targeting central bank.

Q4 — Comparing Bank A vs. Bank B

Bank A: $\theta_\pi = 1.5$, $\theta_y = 0.5$

Reacts strongly to inflation. Will raise rates aggressively when inflation rises. Prioritizes price stability over output.

→ Inflation-focused policy

Bank B: $\theta_\pi = 0.5$, $\theta_y = 1.5$

Reacts strongly to the output gap. Focuses on reducing recessions and stabilizing economic activity, even if inflation moves more slowly.

→ Output stabilization policy

Exercise 3

Fisher Equation & Expectations

Key Formulas

$r_t = i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1}$ | Adaptive: $E_t\pi_{t+1} = \pi_t$ | Anchored: $\pi_t^e - \pi^* = \psi(\pi_{t-1} - \pi^*)$

Imagine that an unexpected shock causes an increase in inflation, while nominal interest rates remain unchanged.

  1. What is the difference between ex-ante and ex-post real interest rate?
  2. What happens to the real interest rate under adaptive expectations?
  3. How is it different with anchored expectations?
Show Solution
Q1 — Ex-Ante vs. Ex-Post Real Interest Rate

Ex-Ante (Before)

Calculated using expected inflation:

$r_t^{ex\,ante} = i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1}$

This is the real return households and firms expect when making decisions about saving, borrowing, or investing.

Ex-Post (After)

Calculated using actual realized inflation:

$r_t^{ex\,post} = i_t - \pi_{t+1}$

This is the real return that actually occurs after observing inflation. It may differ from ex-ante because inflation is uncertain.

Q2 — Adaptive Expectations ($E_t\pi_{t+1} = \pi_t$)

With adaptive expectations, expected inflation equals current inflation. If an unexpected shock raises $\pi_t$ while $i_t$ stays unchanged:

$\pi_t \uparrow \implies E_t\pi_{t+1} \uparrow \implies r_t = i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1} \downarrow$

The real interest rate falls. Cheaper borrowing stimulates consumption and investment → aggregate demand rises → inflation may increase even more.

This is a destabilizing mechanism.

Q3 — Anchored Expectations

With anchored expectations, expected inflation does not fully follow current inflation:

$\pi_t^e - \pi^* = \psi(\pi_{t-1} - \pi^*) \qquad \text{where } 0 < \psi < 1$

When inflation increases unexpectedly, expected inflation rises only partially. So the decrease in the real interest rate is smaller than under adaptive expectations.

Key Comparison

Adaptive: inflation shock → expectations rise strongly → $r_t$ falls a lot → destabilizing
Anchored: inflation shock → expectations rise only slightly → $r_t$ falls less → more stable

Exercise 4

Long-Run Equilibrium (Steady State)

Long-Run Conditions

$y_t = \bar{y}$, $\;\pi_t = \pi^*$, $\;r_t = \rho$, $\;i_t = \rho + \pi^*$

Assume the model parameters: $\alpha = 1$, $\rho = 2$, $\phi = 0.25$, $\theta_\pi = 0.5$, $\theta_y = 0.5$, $\bar{y} = 100$, $\pi^* = 2$.

  1. What are the long-run equilibrium values of $y_t$, $\pi_t$, $r_t$, and $i_t$?
  2. If the inflation target changes to $\pi^* = 3$, what happens to the long-run equilibrium?
Show Solution
Q1 — Baseline Long-Run Values

In the long run, all shocks have disappeared and variables are stable:

$y_t = \bar{y} = 100 \qquad \pi_t = \pi^* = 2 \qquad r_t = \rho = 2 \qquad i_t = \rho + \pi^* = 2 + 2 = 4$
Q2 — New Target $\pi^* = 3$

If the inflation target changes, only the nominal variables adjust. Real variables stay the same:

$y_t = 100 \qquad \pi_t = 3 \qquad r_t = 2 \qquad i_t = 2 + 3 = 5$

Key insight: A higher inflation target raises nominal interest rates and inflation in the long run, but it does not affect real variables (output or real interest rate). This is the concept of long-run monetary neutrality.

Exercise 5

Slope of the DAD Curve

DAD Curve

$y_t = \bar{y} - \frac{\alpha\theta_\pi}{1 + \alpha\theta_y}(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \frac{1}{1+\alpha\theta_y}\epsilon_t$

  1. What is the slope of the DAD with respect to inflation?
  2. How does the slope depend on $\theta_\pi$ and $\theta_y$?
  3. Calculate and interpret the numerical slope using baseline parameters $\alpha = 1$, $\theta_\pi = 0.5$, $\theta_y = 0.5$.
Show Solution
Q1 — The Slope Formula

The slope is the coefficient in front of $(\pi_t - \pi^*)$:

$\text{slope of DAD} = -\frac{\alpha\theta_\pi}{1 + \alpha\theta_y}$

The slope is negative: when inflation increases, the CB raises rates → real rate rises → demand and output fall.

Q2 — Effect of $\theta_\pi$ and $\theta_y$

Higher $\theta_\pi$

CB reacts more to inflation → the DAD curve becomes flatter. A small increase in inflation causes a large drop in output.

Higher $\theta_y$

CB cares more about stabilizing output → the DAD curve becomes steeper. A small increase in inflation causes a smaller drop in output, partially offsetting the inflation effect.

Q3 — Numerical Calculation

With $\alpha = 1$, $\theta_\pi = 0.5$, $\theta_y = 0.5$:

$\text{slope} = -\frac{1 \times 0.5}{1 + 1 \times 0.5} = -\frac{0.5}{1.5} \approx -0.33$

Interpretation: A 1 percentage point increase in inflation reduces output by about 0.33 units, given the baseline parameters.

Exercise 6

Short-Run Equilibrium after a Demand Shock

The economy starts in long-run equilibrium ($y = 100$, $\pi = 2$, $r = 2$, $i = 4$). Then a positive demand shock hits: $\epsilon_t = 2$.

Parameters: $\alpha = 1$, $\phi = 0.25$, $\theta_\pi = 0.5$, $\theta_y = 0.5$, $\bar{y} = 100$, $\pi^* = 2$.

  1. Calculate the new short-run equilibrium values of $y_t$ and $\pi_t$.
  2. Calculate the corresponding values of $r_t$ and $i_t$.
  3. Show your solution graphically in the DAD–DAS diagram.
Show Solution
Step 1 — Set Up the DAD Equation
DAD

$y_t = \bar{y} - \frac{\alpha\theta_\pi}{1+\alpha\theta_y}(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \frac{1}{1+\alpha\theta_y}\epsilon_t$

Computing the coefficients:

$\frac{\alpha\theta_\pi}{1+\alpha\theta_y} = \frac{0.5}{1.5} = 0.33 \qquad \frac{1}{1+\alpha\theta_y} = \frac{1}{1.5} = 0.67$

So:

$y_t = 100 - 0.33(\pi_t - 2) + 0.67 \times 2 = 101.33 - 0.33(\pi_t - 2)$
Step 2 — Set Up the DAS Equation
DAS (Phillips Curve)

$\pi_t = \pi_{t-1} + \phi(y_t - \bar{y})$

Starting from equilibrium, $\pi_{t-1} = 2$:

$\pi_t = 2 + 0.25(y_t - 100)$
Step 3 — Solve the System

From the DAD: $y_t - 100 = 1.33 - 0.33(\pi_t - 2)$

Substituting into the DAS:

$\pi_t = 2 + 0.25[1.33 - 0.33(\pi_t - 2)]$

$\pi_t = 2 + 0.33 - 0.083(\pi_t - 2)$

$\pi_t = 2.33 - 0.083\pi_t + 0.166$

$1.083\pi_t = 2.496$

$\boxed{\pi_t \approx 2.30}$

Plugging back into the DAD:

$y_t = 101.33 - 0.33(2.30 - 2) = 101.33 - 0.10$

$\boxed{y_t \approx 101.23}$

Output rises above potential and inflation increases.

Step 4 — Calculate $i_t$ and $r_t$

Nominal interest rate (Taylor Rule):

$i_t = 2.30 + 2 + 0.5(2.30 - 2) + 0.5(101.23 - 100)$

$i_t = 4.30 + 0.15 + 0.62$

$\boxed{i_t \approx 5.07}$

Real interest rate (Fisher, adaptive: $E_t\pi_{t+1} = \pi_t$):

$r_t = i_t - \pi_t = 5.07 - 2.30$

$\boxed{r_t \approx 2.77}$
Step 5 — Graphical Interpretation

A positive demand shock shifts the DAD curve to the right.

The new short-run equilibrium is at the intersection of the new DAD and the original DAS:

  • Output increases: $100 \to 101.23$
  • Inflation increases: $2 \to 2.30$

In following periods, the rise in inflation shifts the DAS upward, gradually bringing the economy back to long-run equilibrium.

Exercise 7

DAD–DAS Diagram: Movements vs. Shifts

For each of the following changes, determine whether it causes a movement along or a shift of the DAD and DAS curves:

  1. Negative supply shock $\nu_t > 0$.
  2. Positive demand shock $\epsilon_t > 0$.
  3. Increase in potential output $\bar{y}$.
  4. Increase in real interest rate due to policy tightening.
Show Solution
1 — Negative Supply Shock ($\nu_t > 0$)
DAS

$\pi_t = \pi_{t-1} + \phi(y_t - \bar{y}) + \nu_t$

A positive $\nu_t$ increases inflation at every output level (e.g., higher energy prices or production costs).

Result: DAS shifts upward. DAD does not move.

2 — Positive Demand Shock ($\epsilon_t > 0$)

In the DAD equation, a positive $\epsilon_t$ increases output at every inflation level (e.g., higher government spending, stronger consumer confidence).

Result: DAD shifts right. DAS does not move.

3 — Increase in Potential Output ($\bar{y} \uparrow$)

$\bar{y}$ appears in both equations:

  • DAS: higher $\bar{y}$ → smaller output gap → lower inflation → DAS shifts downward.
  • DAD: higher $\bar{y}$ → more output at every inflation level → DAD shifts right.

Result: Both curves shift. This is like a positive productivity or technology shock.

4 — Policy Tightening ($r_t \uparrow$)

Higher real interest rates reduce consumption and investment → aggregate demand falls at every inflation level.

Result: DAD shifts left. DAS does not move.

Summary Rule

Supply shocks ($\nu_t$) → shift DAS
Demand shocks ($\epsilon_t$) → shift DAD
Structural changes ($\bar{y}$) → shift both
Policy changes → shift DAD
