The Open Economy

Trade Balance, Capital Mobility, and Exchange Rates

1. The Small Open Economy (SOE) Model

Until now, we have reasoned as if our country were alone in the world (a closed economy). But globalization changes the rules of the game. A country can borrow from abroad to consume more than it produces, or conversely, save and lend its wealth to its neighbors.

Crucial Assumptions of the SOE Model

1. Long Run Horizon We are in a long-run framework. Prices are perfectly flexible. Consequently, output ($Y$) is always fixed at its maximum potential level ($\bar{Y}$), dictated by the available capital and labor.
2. Small Size The country is "small". This means that its actions do not affect the global economy. If Switzerland decides to double its interest rates, the rest of the world will not even notice.
3. Perfect Capital Mobility Money travels instantaneously across borders. Because of this, the local interest rate ($r$) will always be strictly equal to the global interest rate ($r^*$). If the local rate falls, investors flee immediately to another country until the rate returns to $r^*$. Conclusion: $r = r^*$.

The Golden Identity: $NX = S - I$

This is the most important equation of the chapter. In an open economy, GDP is broken down as follows: $Y = C + I + G + NX$. By rearranging this equation, we discover a profound truth about international trade.

$Y - C - G = I + NX$

The left side ($Y - C - G$) represents exactly National Saving ($S$), that is, what remains of the national income once households and the government have consumed. Thus:

$\boxed{NX = S - I}$
The story of the farmer and the apple

Why is the trade balance ($NX$) identical to the difference between saving and investment ($S - I$)? Let's take a simple example.

Imagine a country composed of a single farmer. He produces 100 apples ($Y$). He eats 60 apples and the government takes 10 to feed its soldiers. It remains $100 - 60 - 10 = 30$ apples. These 30 apples represent his Saving ($S$).

With these 30 apples, he decides to plant 20 of them in the ground to grow new apple trees next year. This is his Investment ($I$). There are exactly 10 apples left over.

What happens to these 10 leftover apples? He has no choice but to sell them to a neighboring country! These 10 apples therefore constitute his Net Exports ($NX$). We thus verify perfectly that $NX = S - I$ (10 = 30 - 20).

Economic conclusion: A country that exports a lot (trade surplus) is simply a country that saves more than it invests locally. It lends its excess savings to the rest of the world. Conversely, a country running a trade deficit is a country that invests or consumes more than it can afford, and must borrow from abroad to finance the difference.

2. The Impact of Economic Policies

Let's use our new identity $NX = S - I$ to understand the effects of public policies. Remember that investment $I$ depends on the world interest rate $r^*$, and saving $S$ depends on the fiscal policy.

Fiscal Expansion (Increase in $G$ or decrease in $T$)

Suppose the government decides to cut taxes or massively build infrastructure. What is the impact?

1. Public saving falls drastically, which reduces total national saving ($S \downarrow$).
2. Since $r^*$ is fixed internationally, our local investment ($I$) does not change.
3. Because $NX = S - I$, if $S$ falls and $I$ is constant, then $NX$ must necessarily fall.

Conclusion: A domestic fiscal expansion mechanically causes a trade deficit. This is called the phenomenon of "Twin Deficits" (a public deficit creates a trade deficit).

Global Shock (The world interest rate $r^*$ rises)

Imagine the United States (a massive economy) decides to raise its interest rates, driving up the global rate $r^*$.

1. With higher rates, borrowing becomes very expensive for our local businesses.
2. Consequently, local investment plummets ($I \downarrow$).
3. Saving ($S$) remains unchanged.
4. According to $NX = S - I$, if we subtract a smaller $I$ from a constant $S$, $NX$ explodes upward!

Conclusion: When the global rate rises, local investment collapses, freeing up capital that will be lent abroad, which translates into a massive trade surplus.

3. The Real Exchange Rate ($\epsilon$)

How exactly does $NX$ manage to increase or decrease? Through the exchange rate. We must distinguish between the nominal and the real exchange rate.

Nominal Rate ($e$) The price of our currency in terms of another. Example: 1 Euro = 1.10 Dollars. If $e$ rises, our currency appreciates.
Real Rate ($\epsilon$) The relative price of our goods compared to foreign goods. It measures true competitiveness. The formula is $\epsilon = e \times \frac{P}{P^*}$ (where $P$ is our domestic price and $P^*$ is the foreign price).

Crucial mechanism: Net Exports ($NX$) are a decreasing function of the real exchange rate $\epsilon$. If our goods become relatively very expensive ($\epsilon$ is high), foreigners stop buying our products, and our citizens prefer to buy cheap foreign imports. Therefore, $NX$ collapses.

How the market adjusts automatically

Let's return to our fiscal expansion scenario. We said that $S$ decreases, so $NX$ must decrease. But how does this happen in reality on the financial markets?

When national saving $S$ falls, there are fewer local dollars available to be lent abroad. The supply of local currency on the foreign exchange market dries up.

Because it becomes rarer, the value of our currency skyrockets! The real exchange rate appreciates ($\epsilon \uparrow$).

Because of this strong currency, our exporters are severely penalized, while imported goods become very cheap for our citizens. Thus, exports fall and imports explode. This causes the trade deficit ($NX \downarrow$). The loop is closed!

4. Purchasing Power Parity & Balassa-Samuelson

There is an extreme theory of international trade called Purchasing Power Parity (PPP). It is based on the "Law of One Price": an identical good must sell for exactly the same price everywhere in the world, once converted into the same currency. Otherwise, arbitrageurs would buy in the cheap country and resell in the expensive one until prices equalize.

Under pure PPP, the real exchange rate is always equal to 1 ($\epsilon = 1$), meaning the cost of living is strictly identical from one country to another. But anyone who has ever traveled to India or Switzerland knows this is false. Why?

The Balassa-Samuelson Effect (The Hairdresser Story)

This effect elegantly explains why the cost of living is always higher in rich countries.

Step 1: The Tradable Sector. Imagine a German engineer making high-tech cars. Thanks to technology, his productivity is huge. His salary is therefore very high. Cars are exported, so their price is fixed globally (PPP applies here).

Step 2: The Non-Tradable Sector. Think of a hairdresser in Berlin. Her productivity hasn't changed in 50 years: it takes the same amount of time to cut hair. The service cannot be exported (you don't go to India just to get a haircut).

Step 3: Wage Contagion. Even though the hairdresser's productivity is low, she has to pay her rent in the same city as the wealthy engineer. To prevent all hairdressers from quitting to go work in the car factories, hair salons are forced to pay high salaries. To afford these high salaries, they must raise the price of a haircut!

Conclusion: Because of the high productivity in the tradable sector, wages rise everywhere, driving up prices in the non-tradable sector (services, housing). This makes the general price level ($P$) higher in rich countries.
