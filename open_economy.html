Open Economy — SOE Model

National Accounting, Trade Balance, Exchange Rates, and Policy Shocks in the Small Open Economy

 Three Levels of Openness

Closed Economy No trade, no capital flows. $Y = C + I + G$. Domestic savings must equal domestic investment ($S = I$). Interest rate adjusts to clear the domestic market.
Small Open Economy (SOE) Trades goods and assets freely but is too small to affect world prices. The world real interest rate $r^*$ is exogenous. Domestic $r = r^*$ always (perfect capital mobility).
Large Open Economy Large enough that its borrowing/lending affects $r^*$. A mix of closed and SOE: shocks raise $r^*$ partially, affecting both domestic investment and NX.

This page covers the classical long-run SOE model, where prices are flexible and output is at full employment. Exchange rate dynamics and the short-run Mundell-Fleming model are covered on the next page.

National Income Accounting in an Open Economy

Closed Economy

All income is spent domestically.

$Y = C + I + G$

Saving must equal investment: $S \equiv Y - C - G = I$.

Open Economy

Part of domestic production is sold abroad; part of domestic spending falls on imports. We add Net Exports $NX = X - IM$.

$Y = C + I + G + NX$

$NX > 0$ = trade surplus (net exporter). $NX < 0$ = trade deficit (net importer).

The Fundamental Identity: $NX = S - I$

Rearranging the open-economy GDP equation: $NX = Y - C - I - G$. Since national saving is $S = Y - C - G$:

$\boxed{NX = S - I}$

The Twin Accounting Identity

This identity also links trade and capital flows. If a country runs a trade deficit ($NX < 0$), it must be borrowing from the rest of the world ($S < I$). If it runs a surplus, it is lending abroad. The identity $NX = NCO$ (Net Capital Outflow) always holds by accounting.

Trade BalanceCapital FlowInterpretation
$NX > 0$ (surplus)NCO > 0 (outflow)Country lends abroad
$NX < 0$ (deficit)NCO < 0 (inflow)Country borrows from abroad
$NX = 0$ (balanced)NCO = 0Autarky in finance

The Small Open Economy (SOE) Model

We now build the analytical model. The SOE operates in the long run: prices are flexible, output is at full employment $Y = \bar{Y} = F(\bar{K}, \bar{L})$, and capital is perfectly mobile internationally.

Key Assumptions

$r = r^*$
Interest Rate Parity (long run): With perfect capital mobility, domestic investors can always borrow or lend abroad at $r^*$. Any domestic rate above $r^*$ attracts foreign capital inflows until $r = r^*$. Any rate below triggers outflows. Result: $r$ is pinned at $r^*$.
$Y = \bar{Y}$
Fixed Output: In the long run, output is determined by factor endowments and technology, not by demand. The SOE model is classical, not Keynesian.
$C, I$
Behaviour: Consumption $C = C(\bar{Y} - T)$ depends on disposable income. Investment $I = I(r^*)$ depends on the world real interest rate. Both are pinned by exogenous variables.

What Determines $NX$?

Since $Y$, $C$, and $I$ are all determined independently of the trade balance, $NX$ is the residual that balances the equation:

$NX = \underbrace{\bar{Y} - C(\bar{Y}-T)}_{\text{National Saving } S} - \;I(r^*)$
The Key Insight of the SOE Model

The trade balance is not determined by trade policy — it is determined by the gap between national saving and investment. If saving exceeds investment ($S > I$), the excess must be lent abroad, generating a trade surplus. If investment exceeds saving, the gap is filled by foreign borrowing, generating a trade deficit.

Any policy that changes $S$ or $I$ will change $NX$ — even if it has nothing to do with trade directly.

Closed vs. Open Economy: The Adjustment Mechanism

Closed Economy

If saving exceeds demand for investment at a given $r$, the interest rate falls until $S = I$. The domestic real rate is the endogenous variable.

Small Open Economy

$r$ is fixed at $r^*$. If $S \neq I$ at $r^*$, the difference flows across borders as Net Exports. The exchange rate (or $NX$) is the endogenous variable.

Policy Shocks and the Trade Balance

Using $NX = S - I(r^*)$, we can analyze how different shocks affect the trade balance. The logic is always: (1) what changes $S$? (2) what changes $I$? (3) what happens to $NX = S - I$?

Shock 1 — Domestic Fiscal Expansion ($G \uparrow$ or $T \downarrow$)
Chain Reaction
1
$G \uparrow$ or $T \downarrow$ → public saving falls → national saving $S \downarrow$
2
$r^*$ unchanged → investment $I(r^*)$ unchanged
3
$NX = S \downarrow - I$ → trade balance deteriorates
Trade surplus shrinks (or deficit grows). The country borrows more from abroad to fund the gap.

Key takeaway: Budget deficits tend to create or worsen trade deficits. This link is called the "Twin Deficits" hypothesis.

Shock 2 — Fiscal Expansion Abroad
Chain Reaction
1
Foreign governments expand → world savings fall → $r^* \uparrow$
2
Higher $r^*$ → domestic investment falls: $I(r^* \uparrow) \downarrow$
3
$NX = S - I \downarrow$ → trade surplus grows (the SOE lends more abroad)
$NX \uparrow$ — a foreign fiscal expansion raises the domestic trade surplus.
Shock 3 — Rise in Domestic Investment Demand
Chain Reaction
1
Investment tax credit or surge in business confidence → $I_0 \uparrow$
2
At $r^*$, investment spending rises: $I \uparrow$
3
$NX = S - I \uparrow$ → trade deficit grows (must borrow more from abroad)
$NX \downarrow$ — an investment boom triggers a trade deficit.

Key takeaway: A booming economy that attracts investment naturally runs a trade deficit — this is not necessarily a sign of weakness.

Summary Table — Policy Shocks in the SOE

Shock Effect on $S$ Effect on $I$ Effect on $NX$
Fiscal expansion at home ($G \uparrow$) $S \downarrow$ unchanged $NX \downarrow$ (deficit)
Fiscal expansion abroad ($r^* \uparrow$) unchanged $I \downarrow$ $NX \uparrow$ (surplus)
Investment demand surge ($I_0 \uparrow$) unchanged $I \uparrow$ $NX \downarrow$ (deficit)
Trade restriction (tariff) unchanged unchanged $NX$ unchanged!

Why Trade Policy Cannot Fix the Trade Balance in the SOE

A tariff reduces imports, which raises $NX$ initially. But since $S$ and $I$ are unchanged (a tariff doesn't affect them), the identity $NX = S - I$ means the trade balance cannot change in the long run. The exchange rate (or dollar flows) adjusts to offset the tariff — NX returns to its original level, just with less trade overall (less exports and less imports).

Exchange Rates

 Defining Exchange Rates — Be Careful with Conventions

Nominal Rate ($E$) Price of one unit of foreign currency in domestic currency. Example: $E = 20.80$ CZK/USD means 1 dollar costs 20.80 koruna. $E \uparrow$ = domestic currency depreciates (it takes more CZK to buy 1 USD).
Real Rate ($\epsilon$) Relative price of foreign goods in terms of domestic goods.
$$\epsilon = \frac{E \cdot P^*}{P}$$ where $P^*$ = foreign price level, $P$ = domestic price level.
$\epsilon \uparrow$ = domestic goods become relatively cheaper (depreciation in real terms) → $NX \uparrow$.
Appreciation Domestic currency becomes more valuable: $E \downarrow$ (nominal); $\epsilon \downarrow$ (real). Domestic goods become more expensive for foreigners → $NX \downarrow$.
Depreciation Domestic currency becomes less valuable: $E \uparrow$ (nominal); $\epsilon \uparrow$ (real). Domestic goods become cheaper for foreigners → $NX \uparrow$.

Net Exports and the Real Exchange Rate

Net exports depend negatively on the real exchange rate (when domestic goods become relatively expensive, we export less and import more):

$NX = NX(\epsilon), \quad \dfrac{dNX}{d\epsilon} > 0$

Wait — why $> 0$? Because $\epsilon \uparrow$ means foreign goods are more expensive relative to domestic goods → domestic consumers switch away from imports, foreigners buy more of our exports → $NX$ rises.

Exchange Rate Equilibrium in the SOE

The real exchange rate $\epsilon^*$ adjusts to ensure the goods market clears: $NX(\epsilon^*) = S - I(r^*)$. This is the key equilibrium condition linking the goods market and the foreign exchange market in the long-run SOE.

Purchasing Power Parity and The Balassa-Samuelson Effect

The Law of One Price and PPP

Arbitrage ensures that identical goods sell for the same price globally (when expressed in a common currency). This is the Law of One Price (LOOP). Extending it to price levels gives Purchasing Power Parity (PPP):

$\text{PPP: } P = E \cdot P^* \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \epsilon = 1 \text{ always}$

Why does PPP fail in practice? Transport costs, tariffs, non-tradable goods (haircuts, rent), and different consumption baskets. The real exchange rate $\epsilon$ persistently deviates from 1.

The Balassa-Samuelson Effect

Richer economies systematically have higher price levels than PPP would predict. The mechanism runs through sectoral productivity differences.

Tradable Sector

Goods traded internationally (cars, steel, electronics). Prices are pinned by global competition — a French car and a German car of the same quality must sell at similar prices.

Productivity differences $\Rightarrow$ wage differences (not price differences) in tradables.

Non-Tradable Sector

Services consumed where produced (haircuts, restaurants, real estate). Prices are set by domestic costs — especially wages — not global competition.

No arbitrage here — a Prague haircut is cheaper than a London haircut.

The Chain Reaction — Balassa-Samuelson
Productivity Surge in Tradables of a Rich/Fast-Growing Economy…
1
Productivity rises in tradables: $A_T \uparrow$ → firms can afford to pay higher wages in that sector
2
Labour market pressure: non-tradable workers demand similar wages (or labour flows toward tradables)
3
Non-tradable productivity $A_N$ is unchanged → higher wages → higher unit labour costs in non-tradables
4
Non-tradable prices rise: $P_N \uparrow$ → overall price level $P \uparrow$
Rich and fast-growing economies tend to have higher price levels → their real exchange rate appears "overvalued" relative to PPP — but this is structural, not a misalignment.

Related: Dutch Disease — same mechanism, but triggered by a natural resource boom (e.g., oil discovery) rather than general productivity growth. The resource sector bids up wages, hurting the rest of the traded sector's competitiveness.

Key Takeaways — Full Recap

1. The Core Identity: $NX = S - I$. This is non-negotiable — it follows from GDP accounting. Any change in the trade balance must be matched by a change in saving or investment. Trade policy alone cannot change $NX$ in the long run.

2. SOE with $r = r^*$. The domestic interest rate is pinned globally. Investment $I(r^*)$ is fixed. Fiscal policy affects saving $S$ → directly changes $NX$. Crowding out happens through the exchange rate, not through the domestic interest rate (as in a closed economy).

3. Exchange Rate Conventions. $E \uparrow$ (or $\epsilon \uparrow$) = depreciation = domestic goods cheaper = $NX \uparrow$. Always think about which variable you are changing and what "rising" means in your convention.

4. PPP Failures. Non-tradables, transport costs, and preferences break PPP. The Balassa-Samuelson effect explains systematic PPP failure: richer countries have higher non-tradable prices due to higher wages from their productive tradable sectors.
