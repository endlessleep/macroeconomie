The Open Economy

Trade Balances, Capital Mobility, and Real Exchange Rates

1. The Small Open Economy (SOE) Model

The Small Open Economy (SOE) model extends macroeconomic analysis to international trade and finance. It assumes the domestic economy interacts with global markets without influencing global prices or interest rates.

Structural Assumptions

1. Long-Run Horizon Prices are perfectly flexible. Consequently, aggregate output ($Y$) is determined exogenously by available factors of production and technology, fixed at its full-employment potential level ($\bar{Y}$).
2. Price Taking The domestic economy constitutes a negligible fraction of global markets. Domestic policy shifts or economic shocks have zero impact on the world interest rate or global aggregate demand.
3. Perfect Capital Mobility Capital flows without friction across international borders seeking the highest return. Arbitrage ensures that the domestic interest rate ($r$) is strictly equal to the exogenous world interest rate ($r^*$).

The National Income Accounting Identity: $NX = S - I$

The fundamental accounting identity in an open economy defines output as $Y = C + I + G + NX$. Rearranging this identity isolates Net Exports ($NX$) as the difference between domestic production and domestic absorption.

$Y - C - G = I + NX$

The term $(Y - C - G)$ constitutes National Saving ($S$), the sum of private saving $(Y - T - C)$ and public saving $(T - G)$. Substituting $S$ into the identity yields the fundamental macroeconomic equilibrium condition for the open economy:

$\boxed{NX = S - I}$
Macroeconomic Interpretation

The identity $NX = S - I$ establishes that a nation's trade balance is structurally equivalent to its net capital outflow.

If National Saving exceeds domestic Investment ($S > I$), the excess savings are lent abroad, corresponding to a trade surplus ($NX > 0$). Conversely, if domestic investment exceeds national saving ($S < I$), the shortfall must be financed by borrowing from international markets, manifesting as a trade deficit ($NX < 0$).

Consequently, trade imbalances are not inherently symptomatic of trade policy inefficiencies, but are direct reflections of underlying macroeconomic savings and investment disparities.

2. Comparative Statics in the SOE Model

The equilibrium identity $NX = S(Y, G, T) - I(r^*)$ permits formal analysis of domestic and exogenous shocks. In the long run, $Y$ is fixed at $\bar{Y}$, and $r$ is fixed at $r^*$.

Fundamental Identity Reminder
$NX = S - I$
  • If $S < I$: The country borrows from abroad $\Rightarrow$ Capital inflow $\Rightarrow$ Trade deficit ($NX < 0$).
  • If $S > I$: The country lends to the rest of the world $\Rightarrow$ Capital outflow $\Rightarrow$ Trade surplus ($NX > 0$).
1. Domestic Fiscal Expansion: lower saving, unchanged investment, trade deficit

A domestic fiscal expansion means either an increase in government spending ($G \uparrow$) or a decrease in taxes ($T \downarrow$).

Step 1: National saving decreases

Public saving is defined as $S_{public} = T - G$. If government spending increases or taxes decrease, public saving falls. Since national saving includes public saving, total national saving also decreases:

$S \downarrow$

Economic intuition: The government absorbs more national resources, so less income is left available as national saving.

Step 2: Domestic investment remains constant

In a small open economy with perfect capital mobility, the domestic interest rate is strictly anchored to the world interest rate ($r = r^*$). Since the domestic fiscal shock does not alter $r^*$, the domestic cost of capital does not change. Therefore, domestic investment remains constant:

$I = \text{constant}$

Step 3: Net exports decrease

Using the fundamental identity $NX = S - I$. Since saving falls while investment remains constant ($S \downarrow, I = \text{constant}$), we get:

$NX \downarrow$

Economic intuition: The country now saves less than it invests domestically. It needs foreign financing, generating a capital inflow. On the real side, this manifests as a trade deficit: the country imports more than it exports.

Final Chain & Implication

$G \uparrow \text{ or } T \downarrow \Rightarrow S \downarrow \Rightarrow S < I \Rightarrow \text{Capital Inflow} \Rightarrow NX \downarrow \Rightarrow \text{Trade Deficit}$

Expansionary fiscal policy structurally deteriorates the trade balance. This formalizes the logic of the Twin Deficits hypothesis: a larger public deficit systematically generates a larger trade deficit.

2. Increase in the World Interest Rate: unchanged saving, lower investment, trade surplus

An exogenous world interest rate shock means an increase in the global cost of capital ($r^* \uparrow$).

Step 1: Domestic investment decreases

In a small open economy ($r = r^*$). Therefore, when the world interest rate increases, the domestic interest rate instantly increases. Borrowing becomes more expensive, and fewer investment projects remain profitable. As a result, domestic investment falls:

$I \downarrow$

Step 2: National saving remains constant

Assuming domestic fiscal policy is unchanged and consumption does not directly depend on the interest rate, national saving remains constant:

$S = \text{constant}$

Economic intuition: Households and the government are saving the exact same amount as before, but firms now want to invest less because capital is more expensive.

Step 3: Net exports increase

Using the fundamental identity $NX = S - I$. Since saving remains constant while investment falls ($S = \text{constant}, I \downarrow$), we get:

$NX \uparrow$

Economic intuition: The country now saves more than it invests domestically. The unused domestic saving is invested or lent abroad, creating a capital outflow. On the real side, this manifests as a trade surplus: the country exports more than it imports.

Final Chain & Implication

$r^* \uparrow \Rightarrow r \uparrow \Rightarrow I \downarrow \Rightarrow S > I \Rightarrow \text{Capital Outflow} \Rightarrow NX \uparrow \Rightarrow \text{Trade Surplus}$

A rise in the world interest rate reduces domestic investment and pushes excess domestic saving abroad. This structurally generates a trade surplus.

3. The Real Exchange Rate ($\epsilon$)

The equilibrium adjustment of $NX$ to changes in $S$ or $I$ is mediated by the real exchange rate.

Nominal Exchange Rate ($e$) The relative price of domestic currency in terms of foreign currency (e.g., USD/EUR). An increase in $e$ denotes an appreciation of the domestic currency.
Real Exchange Rate ($\epsilon$) The relative price of domestic goods in terms of foreign goods, defined as $\epsilon = e \times \frac{P}{P^*}$. It serves as the primary metric of international competitiveness.

Net Exports are modeled as a monotonically decreasing function of the real exchange rate: $NX = NX(\epsilon)$. A high $\epsilon$ (real appreciation) renders domestic goods relatively expensive, depressing exports and stimulating imports, thereby reducing $NX$.

Exchange Rate Adjustment Behind the Trade Balance Effect

The previous section established that a domestic fiscal expansion reduces national saving:

$G \uparrow \text{ or } T \downarrow \Rightarrow S \downarrow$

Since domestic investment remains constant in a small open economy, the identity $NX = S - I$ implies:

$NX \downarrow$

However, this identity only gives the final accounting result. It does not yet explain how exports and imports actually adjust. The adjustment occurs through the real exchange rate ($\epsilon$).

Step 1: Lower saving reduces net capital outflow

When national saving falls, the country has fewer domestic funds available to lend or invest abroad. Therefore:

$S \downarrow \Rightarrow \text{Net Capital Outflow} \downarrow$

Economic intuition: Residents buy fewer foreign assets because less domestic saving is available. If Net Capital Outflow (NCO) becomes negative, it means the country receives more foreign capital than it sends abroad—it is effectively borrowing from the rest of the world.

Step 2: Lower capital outflow reduces the supply of domestic currency

To buy foreign assets, domestic residents normally need to exchange domestic currency for foreign currency. If they buy fewer foreign assets, they sell less domestic currency on the foreign exchange market. Therefore:

$\text{Supply of Domestic Currency} \downarrow$

This makes the domestic currency scarcer on the foreign exchange market.

Step 3: The domestic currency appreciates

Because the domestic currency becomes scarcer, its relative value increases. Therefore:

$\epsilon \uparrow$

This is a real appreciation.

Step 4: Appreciation reduces net exports

A real appreciation makes domestic goods more expensive relative to foreign goods. As a result:

  • Exports decrease, because domestic goods become more expensive for foreigners;
  • Imports increase, because foreign goods become cheaper for domestic consumers.
$X \downarrow,\quad M \uparrow \Rightarrow NX \downarrow$

Conclusion

The real exchange rate is the adjustment variable that translates the fall in national saving into a fall in net exports. The complete mechanism is:

$G \uparrow \text{ or } T \downarrow \Rightarrow S \downarrow \Rightarrow \text{Net Capital Outflow} \downarrow \Rightarrow \text{Currency Supply} \downarrow \Rightarrow \epsilon \uparrow \Rightarrow X \downarrow, M \uparrow \Rightarrow NX \downarrow$

4. Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) & Balassa-Samuelson

The Core Idea

Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) states that exchange rates should adjust so that the same basket of goods has the same price in every country, once prices are converted into the same currency.

The underlying logic rests on the Law of One Price: in competitive markets with no trade barriers, identical goods must sell at the same price internationally. If a good is cheaper in one country, consumers and firms will buy it there, driving demand up in that country until the price difference disappears. This process is called goods arbitrage.

Intuitive Example

Suppose the same basket of goods costs:

$100 \text{ € in Europe}, \quad 120 \text{ \$ in the United States}$

PPP implies the equilibrium nominal exchange rate should be:

$1 \text{ €} = 1.20 \text{ \$}$

At this exchange rate, €100 converts exactly to $120, so the basket costs the same in both currencies. If the exchange rate were different — say 1€ = 1.50$ — the European basket would appear cheaper for American consumers, who would switch to buying European goods. This demand would drive the euro up until parity is restored.

Key Intuition: Why Arbitrage Enforces PPP

Under PPP:

  • No country remains permanently "cheaper" or "more expensive" than another.
  • If a price gap appears, consumers shift spending toward the cheaper country.
  • This increases demand for the cheaper country's currency, causing it to appreciate.
  • Exchange rates and/or prices then adjust until purchasing power is equalised across countries.

The Real Exchange Rate

The real exchange rate ($\epsilon$) measures the relative price of domestic goods compared to foreign goods. It is the key variable for assessing a country's international competitiveness.

Nominal Exchange Rate ($e$) The relative price of domestic currency in terms of foreign currency. An increase in $e$ means the domestic currency can buy more foreign currency — it has appreciated.
Real Exchange Rate ($\epsilon$) The relative price of domestic goods in terms of foreign goods, defined as $\epsilon = \frac{eP}{P^*}$. It captures how expensive domestic production is relative to foreign production.

Key Formula

$\epsilon = \frac{e \cdot P}{P^*}$

with $e$ = nominal exchange rate, $P$ = domestic price level, $P^*$ = foreign price level.

Interpreting the Real Exchange Rate
  • If $\epsilon > 1$: domestic goods are relatively expensive — competitiveness is low. Exports fall, imports rise, $NX \downarrow$.
  • If $\epsilon < 1$: domestic goods are relatively cheap — competitiveness is high. Exports rise, imports fall, $NX \uparrow$.
  • If $\epsilon = 1$: domestic and foreign goods are priced identically — this is the PPP benchmark.

Absolute PPP

Under absolute PPP, the nominal exchange rate adjusts so that the same basket of goods costs exactly the same in every country after currency conversion. This is written as:

$e \cdot P = P^*$

This means: if you convert the domestic price $P$ into foreign currency using the exchange rate $e$, you get exactly the foreign price $P^*$. Now substitute this condition into the real exchange rate formula:

$\epsilon = \frac{eP}{P^*} = \frac{P^*}{P^*} = 1$
Under Absolute PPP: $\epsilon = 1$
  • Domestic and foreign goods have identical purchasing power.
  • There is no persistent price advantage for either country.
  • Any nominal price difference is exactly offset by the exchange rate.
  • Net exports are perfectly elastic with respect to $\epsilon$: even a tiny deviation in relative prices triggers unlimited arbitrage flows.

Consequences for Net Exports

Under strict PPP, $NX$ becomes a function of $\epsilon$ that reacts very strongly to any price deviation. The mechanism is:

  • If domestic goods become slightly more expensive ($\epsilon \uparrow$), consumers — both domestic and foreign — switch toward cheaper foreign goods.
  • Exports decrease immediately; imports increase immediately.
  • The resulting shift in currency demand then pushes the exchange rate back toward parity.

In practice, PPP holds better in the long run than the short run. In the short run, prices are sticky and exchange rates are volatile, so $\epsilon$ can deviate significantly from 1. Empirical evidence consistently shows that PPP deviations can persist for years.

Structural Deviations: The Balassa-Samuelson Effect

Even in the long run, PPP fails to hold perfectly across countries with different income levels. The Balassa-Samuelson effect explains why price levels are systematically higher in rich countries.

The Balassa-Samuelson Effect: Step by Step

Step 1: Productivity differentials in tradables

Advanced economies are much more productive in the tradable goods sector (manufacturing, technology) than developing economies. The Law of One Price equalises the prices of tradable goods internationally, so advanced economies earn more per unit of output in that sector.

Step 2: Wages equalise domestically

High productivity in tradables generates high wages. Because workers can move freely across sectors domestically, wages in the non-tradable sector (haircuts, restaurants, local services) must match — otherwise workers leave for the tradable sector. High wages flow into services.

Step 3: Non-tradable prices become structurally high

Since productivity in non-tradables is roughly similar across countries, higher wages translate directly into higher prices for non-tradable goods in rich countries. The overall price level $P$ — a weighted average of tradable and non-tradable prices — is therefore persistently higher in advanced economies, causing a structural overvaluation relative to PPP.
