Seminar 1: The IS Equation

DMM — Practical Exercises

Exercise 1

Understanding the IS Curve

Key Formula

$y_t = \bar{y}_t - \alpha(r_t - \rho) + \epsilon_t$

Consider the IS curve equation above and answer the following:

  1. How can we define the natural real interest rate?
  2. How is it related to monetary policy being tight or loose?
  3. Why is the output gap in the IS equation related to the interest rate?
Show Solution
Question 1 — Natural Real Interest Rate

The natural real interest rate ($\rho$) is the real interest rate at which the economy produces exactly at its potential output, with no shocks and stable inflation.

When $r_t = \rho$, the IS equation simplifies to:

$r_t = \rho \implies y_t = \bar{y}_t$

At this point, the economy is neither overheating nor in recession. The natural rate can also be understood as the interest rate that balances saving and investment in the absence of shocks. It is often called the neutral interest rate, because it neither stimulates nor restricts economic activity.

Question 2 — Tight vs. Loose Policy

The stance of monetary policy depends on comparing the actual real interest rate ($r_t$) with the natural rate ($\rho$):

Tight Policy ($r_t > \rho$)

Borrowing is expensive → households save more, firms invest less → aggregate demand falls → output drops below potential → negative output gap.

Loose Policy ($r_t < \rho$)

Borrowing is cheap → households consume more, firms invest more → aggregate demand rises → output exceeds potential → positive output gap.

The natural rate therefore serves as the benchmark for evaluating monetary policy stance. When $r_t = \rho$, policy is neutral.

Question 3 — Output Gap and Interest Rate

The output gap ($y_t - \bar{y}_t$) depends on the "interest rate gap" ($r_t - \rho$) because interest rates directly influence spending decisions:

  • Households: A higher real interest rate increases the return on saving, making it more attractive to postpone consumption. Current consumption falls.
  • Firms: The real interest rate is the cost of borrowing. Higher rates make investment projects less profitable, so firms invest less.

Since aggregate demand = consumption + investment (+ other components), higher $r_t$ reduces demand → lower output → negative output gap. The parameter $\alpha$ measures how strongly output reacts to a change in the interest rate gap.

Exercise 2

Life-Cycle Hypothesis and Natural Interest Rate

Consider the life-cycle hypothesis and its implications for the natural interest rate. For each of the following demographic shifts, discuss the implied effect on the natural interest rate:

  1. The share of people in working age increases relative to those not working, as a result of an earlier baby boom.
  2. The expected length of retirement increases due to improvements in health care and longer life expectancy.
Show Solution
Part 1 — Baby Boom Effect

According to the life-cycle hypothesis, people save during their working years and spend their savings during retirement.

If the share of working-age people increases (due to a baby boom), more individuals are in their saving phase. This increases aggregate saving in the economy.

When the supply of savings rises relative to investment demand, the equilibrium interest rate falls:

$\text{More workers} \implies \text{Savings} \uparrow \implies \rho \downarrow$

Result: The natural real interest rate decreases.

Part 2 — Longer Retirement

If people expect to live longer, they need to accumulate more savings during their working years to finance a longer retirement. This means they consume less today and save more.

Again, aggregate saving increases, pushing the equilibrium interest rate down:

$\text{Longer retirement} \implies \text{Savings} \uparrow \implies \rho \downarrow$

Result: The natural real interest rate decreases. This mechanism is often cited as one explanation for the long-term decline in natural interest rates observed in many developed countries.

Exercise 3

Slope of the IS Curve

Consider the consumption smoothing theory as the microfoundation for the IS curve. Suppose that consumers become more willing to postpone consumption due to increased financial literacy about the benefits of saving.

  1. What happens to the slope of the IS curve?
  2. How does this affect the effectiveness of monetary policy?
Show Solution
Part 1 — Change in Slope
Recall

$y_t = \bar{y}_t - \alpha(r_t - \rho) + \epsilon_t$

The parameter $\alpha$ measures how sensitive demand is to interest rate changes.

If consumers are more willing to postpone consumption, they react more strongly to changes in the interest rate. When $r_t$ rises, they save more (larger reduction in consumption). When $r_t$ falls, they consume more (larger increase in consumption).

This means $\alpha$ increases.

Result: The IS curve becomes flatter (less steep). A given change in the real interest rate produces a larger change in output.

Part 2 — Effectiveness of Monetary Policy

If the IS curve is flatter, a small change in the interest rate leads to a large change in output.

Since the central bank controls the economy primarily through interest rates, this means that monetary policy becomes more effective. Smaller rate changes are sufficient to generate large changes in aggregate demand and output.

$\alpha \uparrow \implies \text{IS flatter} \implies \text{Monetary policy more effective}$
Exercise 4

IS Curve — Graphical Analysis

On the IS diagram (vertical axis: real interest rate $r$, horizontal axis: output $y$), represent each of the following situations. Determine whether each event causes a movement along the IS curve or a shift of the IS curve.

  1. Increase in nominal interest rates.
  2. Drop in real interest rate due to an increase in expected inflation without a corresponding increase in nominal interest rates.
  3. Increase in the neutral interest rate without a change in nominal interest rates.
  4. Jump in confidence among firms due to the advent of AI.
Show Solution
Situation 1 — Increase in Nominal Rates
Fisher Equation

$r_t = i_t - E_t\pi_{t+1}$

If expected inflation stays constant, a higher nominal rate ($i_t \uparrow$) means a higher real rate ($r_t \uparrow$). This reduces consumption and investment → output falls.

Since the real interest rate itself changes, this is a movement along the IS curve (upward = lower output).

Situation 2 — Real Rate Drop from Higher Expected Inflation

If $E_t\pi_{t+1}$ increases while $i_t$ stays constant, then $r_t$ decreases. Lower real rates stimulate consumption and investment → output rises.

Again, the real interest rate changes → movement along the IS curve (downward = higher output).

Situation 3 — Natural Rate Increase (no change in $i_t$)

In the IS equation $y_t = \bar{y}_t - \alpha(r_t - \rho) + \epsilon_t$, if $\rho$ increases, the gap $(r_t - \rho)$ becomes smaller for any given $r_t$. This means output is higher at every interest rate level.

Result: Rightward shift of the IS curve.

Situation 4 — Confidence Jump (AI)

Higher firm confidence raises investment demand. In the IS equation, this is captured by a positive demand shock ($\epsilon_t > 0$). Output increases at every interest rate level.

Result: Rightward shift of the IS curve.

Key Rule

Changes in the real interest rate → movement along the IS curve.
Changes in structural parameters or shocks ($\rho$, $\bar{y}_t$, $\epsilon_t$) → shift of the IS curve.

Exercise 5

Microfounding the IS Curve

Consider a representative household living for two periods $t = 1, 2$. The household earns income $y_1$ in period 1 and $y_2$ in period 2, and holds initial financial wealth $f_1$. The household chooses consumption $c_1$, $c_2$ and saving $s_1$ to maximize lifetime utility:

$\max_{c_1, c_2} \; \ln(c_1) + \beta \ln(c_2)$

subject to the budget constraints:

$c_1 + s_1 = y_1 + f_1 \qquad \text{and} \qquad c_2 = y_2 + (1+R)s_1$

where $\beta$ is the household's time preference and $R$ is the interest rate. No borrowing constraints.

  1. State the intertemporal budget constraint.
  2. Derive the household's equilibrium condition for optimal $c_1$ and $c_2$ (MRS method).
  3. Express $c_1$ and $c_2$ as functions of $y_1, y_2, f_1, R$, and $\beta$.
  4. Express saving $s_1$ as a function of $R, y_1, y_2$, and $f_1$.
  5. Explain how these results relate to the IS curve.
  6. Discuss how an increase in $y_2$ or $\beta$ shifts the IS curve.
Show Solution
Q1 — Intertemporal Budget Constraint

From the first-period constraint: $s_1 = y_1 + f_1 - c_1$.

Substituting into the second-period constraint:

$c_2 = y_2 + (1+R)(y_1 + f_1 - c_1)$

Rearranging to get the intertemporal budget constraint:

$c_1 + \frac{c_2}{1+R} = y_1 + f_1 + \frac{y_2}{1+R}$

The present value of consumption equals the present value of lifetime income and wealth.

Q2 — Equilibrium Condition (MRS Method)

In equilibrium, the consumer allocates consumption so that the Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS) equals the relative price of consumption across periods.

The marginal utilities are:

$\frac{\partial U}{\partial c_1} = \frac{1}{c_1} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \frac{\partial U}{\partial c_2} = \frac{\beta}{c_2}$

The MRS between period 1 and period 2 consumption is:

$MRS_{1 \to 2} = \frac{\partial U / \partial c_2}{\partial U / \partial c_1} = \frac{\beta \, c_1}{c_2}$

Setting the MRS equal to the relative price $\frac{1}{1+R}$:

$\frac{\beta \, c_1}{c_2} = \frac{1}{1+R}$

This gives the optimal consumption rule:

$c_2 = \beta(1+R) \, c_1$
Q3 — Solving for $c_1$ and $c_2$

Substitute $c_2 = \beta(1+R)c_1$ into the intertemporal budget constraint:

$c_1 + \frac{\beta(1+R)c_1}{1+R} = y_1 + f_1 + \frac{y_2}{1+R}$

Simplifying:

$c_1(1 + \beta) = y_1 + f_1 + \frac{y_2}{1+R}$

Defining lifetime wealth as $W_1 = y_1 + f_1 + \frac{y_2}{1+R}$, we get:

$c_1 = \frac{1}{1+\beta} \, W_1 = \frac{1}{1+\beta}\left(y_1 + f_1 + \frac{y_2}{1+R}\right)$

And using $c_2 = \beta(1+R)c_1$:

$c_2 = \frac{\beta}{1+\beta}\left((1+R)(y_1 + f_1) + y_2\right)$

Households consume a constant fraction of their lifetime wealth.

Q4 — Saving Function

From $s_1 = y_1 + f_1 - c_1$:

$s_1 = \frac{1}{1+\beta}\left(\beta(y_1 + f_1) - \frac{y_2}{1+R}\right)$

Saving increases when current income is high ($y_1 \uparrow$), decreases when future income is high ($y_2 \uparrow$, no need to save), and increases when interest rates are high ($R \uparrow$, saving is more rewarding).

Q5 — Connection to the IS Curve

The key result is the equilibrium condition:

$c_2 = \beta(1+R)c_1$

This shows that the household's consumption choice depends on the interest rate.

When $R$ increases → saving becomes more attractive → households reduce current consumption $c_1$ → aggregate demand falls → output decreases.

This is exactly the negative relationship between the interest rate and output captured by the IS curve:

$R \uparrow \implies c_1 \downarrow \implies y_t \downarrow \quad \text{(IS curve logic)}$
Q6 — Shift from $y_2 \uparrow$ or $\beta \uparrow$

Increase in $y_2$: Higher future income raises lifetime wealth $W_1 = y_1 + f_1 + \frac{y_2}{1+R}$. This increases current consumption $c_1$ at every interest rate → aggregate demand rises → IS curve shifts right.

Increase in $\beta$: Households place more value on future consumption. This changes the optimal allocation across periods and can increase the responsiveness of consumption to intertemporal trade-offs.

In both cases, current demand increases at every interest rate level, causing the IS curve to shift rightward.
