IS-LM Applications

Multipliers, Crowding Out, Liquidity Trap, Deflation, and the Great Depression

Multipliers at Work

In the Keynesian Cross (fixed interest rate), a change in autonomous spending has a multiplied effect on output. In IS-LM, the interest rate rises endogenously and partially offsets this — making the real-world multiplier smaller.

The Two Multipliers Side by Side

Keynesian Cross Multiplier

$\dfrac{dY}{dG} = \alpha_g = \dfrac{1}{1-c(1-t)}$

This is the maximum possible multiplier. It applies only if the interest rate is held constant (e.g., by the CB accommodating the shock).

IS-LM Fiscal Multiplier

$\dfrac{dY}{dG} < \alpha_g$

The actual IS-LM multiplier is smaller because fiscal expansion raises money demand → $i$ rises → private investment is crowded out, partially offsetting the stimulus.

The Size of the IS-LM Multiplier Depends on…

  • How steeply the LM curve rises (steep LM = bigger interest rate response = more crowding out = smaller multiplier)
  • How interest-sensitive investment is ($b$ large = IS flatter = more crowding out)
  • In the limit: vertical LM (Classical) → no stimulus at all. Horizontal LM (Liquidity Trap) → full Keynesian Cross multiplier applies.

The Crowding Out Effect

When the government increases spending, it does not simply add to output. It sets off a chain of interactions in the money market that partially undoes the fiscal stimulus.

Fiscal Expansion ($G \uparrow$) → Full Chain
1
$G \uparrow$ → IS shifts right → at the old $i$, planned expenditure exceeds output
2
Output rises → transactions demand for money rises ($L^d \uparrow$)
3
With fixed $M/P$, excess money demand pushes interest rate up ($i \uparrow$)
4
Higher $i$ reduces private investment ($I \downarrow$) — this partially offsets step 1
Net result: $Y$ rises, but by less than the Keynesian Cross multiplier predicts. $i$ also rises. Investment is "crowded out."

Summary: Policy Shocks in IS-LM

Policy Shock Curve Moves $Y^*$ $i^*$ Key Channel
$G \uparrow$ (fiscal expansion) IS → right ↑ ↑ Crowding out reduces private $I$
$T \uparrow$ (tax increase) IS → left ↓ ↓ $C \downarrow$ (disposable income drops)
$M \uparrow$ (monetary expansion) LM → right ↑ ↓ Lower $i$ stimulates investment
$M \downarrow$ (monetary contraction) LM → left ↓ ↑ Higher $i$ kills investment
Demand pessimism (IS shock) IS → left ↓ ↓ Autonomous spending drops unexpectedly
Money demand shock (LM shock) LM → left ↓ ↑ Liquidity preference spikes

Policy Applications — Graphical Analysis

Monetary Expansion ($M \uparrow$)

The CB increases money supply. Real balances $M/P$ rise.

  • LM shifts right (downward)
  • $i^* \downarrow$ → $I \uparrow$ → $Y^* \uparrow$
  • No crowding out — $i$ falls, not rises

Fiscal Expansion ($G \uparrow$)

The government raises spending. Autonomous demand $A$ rises.

  • IS shifts right
  • Both $i^*$ and $Y^*$ rise
  • Crowding out: private $I$ falls

Tax Increase ($T \uparrow$)

Higher taxes reduce disposable income → consumption falls.

  • IS shifts left
  • Both $i^*$ and $Y^*$ fall
  • Investment is "un-crowded" — rises slightly

Deriving the Aggregate Demand Curve

The IS-LM model is drawn for a given price level $P$. What happens when $P$ changes?

Price Level rises ($P \uparrow$)…
1
Real money supply falls: $M/P \downarrow$ (with nominal $M$ fixed)
2
LM shifts left → equilibrium $i^*$ rises
3
Higher $i^*$ reduces investment → equilibrium $Y^*$ falls
Conclusion: $P \uparrow \Rightarrow Y^* \downarrow$ — the AD curve is downward-sloping in $(P, Y)$ space.

What Shifts the AD Curve?

Any change that shifts IS or LM independently of $P$ shifts the AD curve:

  • Fiscal expansion ($G \uparrow$, $T \downarrow$): shifts IS right → AD shifts right.
  • Monetary expansion ($M \uparrow$): shifts LM right → AD shifts right.
  • Negative demand shock (business pessimism): IS shifts left → AD shifts left.

From LM to MP: The Fixed-Rate Case

IS-LM: Fixed Money Supply

The CB targets $\bar{M}$. When the government spends more, money demand rises, $i$ rises endogenously → crowding out occurs. The fiscal multiplier is damped.

IS-MP: Fixed Interest Rate

The CB targets $\bar{i}$. When the government spends more, the CB supplies additional liquidity to keep $i$ constant. No interest-rate crowding out → the full Keynesian Cross multiplier applies.

Why the IS-MP Framework is More Realistic

Modern central banks communicate a policy rate target (e.g., ECB's main refinancing rate, Fed funds rate), not a money target. The IS-MP framework better captures how monetary policy works today. The IS curve is identical in both frameworks; only the monetary policy block differs.

The Effective Lower Bound (ELB) and the Liquidity Trap

 Key Concepts

Effective Lower Bound When the nominal policy rate approaches zero, the CB can no longer cut further through conventional tools. Real rates $r = i - \pi^e$ may still be too high if inflation expectations fall.
Liquidity Trap When money demand is perfectly elastic (horizontal LM), any additional money supply is hoarded — it does not lower $i$ further. Monetary policy loses traction.
Unconventional Policy When conventional rate cuts are exhausted: QE (asset purchases), forward guidance (promise to keep rates low), negative rates.

At the ELB: Monetary Policy Weak

  • $i$ cannot fall below zero (or barely)
  • Increased $M$ is hoarded (horizontal LM segment)
  • Investment does not respond to additional money
  • The LM curve is kinked — flat at the bottom

At the ELB: Fiscal Policy Powerful

  • IS shift right → $Y$ rises without raising $i$
  • No crowding out (money demand absorbs extra liquidity)
  • Full Keynesian multiplier applies
  • This is the orthodox case for fiscal stimulus in a recession

Post-2008 and Post-2020 Context

After the Global Financial Crisis and again during Covid-19, many central banks hit the ELB. This is why fiscal packages (government spending) became the dominant stabilization tool. Unconventional monetary policy (QE) worked mainly through expectations and credit spreads rather than the classic LM mechanism.

The Great Depression: IS vs. LM Interpretations

The Great Depression (1929–1933) remains the canonical application of IS-LM analysis. Two competing hypotheses explain it, and the evidence requires careful interpretation.

Hypothesis 1: IS Shock (Spending)

Claim: The Depression was driven by a collapse in autonomous demand — a large leftward shift of IS.

Evidence for:

  • S&P fell 71% (Oct 1929→Dec 1933) → $C \downarrow$ (wealth effect)
  • Massive drop in investment (overbuilding correction + bank failures)
  • Contractionary fiscal policy (governments raised taxes to balance budgets)
  • Both $Y$ and $i$ fell — consistent with a leftward IS shift

Hypothesis 2: LM Shock (Money)

Claim: The Depression was amplified by a massive monetary contraction — a leftward shift of LM.

Evidence for:

  • M1 fell 25% during 1929–33 (Friedman & Schwartz)
  • Wave of bank failures destroyed deposits

Complication: $P$ fell even more than $M$, so $M/P$ actually rose slightly 1929–31. Nominal rates fell. So was LM really contracting?

The Deflation Complication

Prices fell ~25% during 1929–33. This is the key nuance. Deflation has both stabilizing and destabilizing effects:

Stabilizing Channels

Keynes Effect:
$P \downarrow \Rightarrow M/P \uparrow \Rightarrow$ LM right $\Rightarrow i \downarrow \Rightarrow Y \uparrow$

Pigou (Real Balance) Effect:
$P \downarrow \Rightarrow$ real wealth $\uparrow \Rightarrow C \uparrow \Rightarrow$ IS right $\Rightarrow Y \uparrow$

Destabilizing Channels

Expected Deflation:
$\pi^e \downarrow \Rightarrow r = i - \pi^e \uparrow \Rightarrow I \downarrow \Rightarrow Y \downarrow$
(IS shifts left even if $i$ is low)

Debt-Deflation (Fisher):
Unexpected $P \downarrow$ redistributes real wealth from borrowers (high MPC) to lenders (low MPC) → aggregate spending falls → IS shifts left.

In the 1930s, the destabilizing channels dominated: deflation raised real debt burdens, caused banking panics, and depressed expectations. The self-correction mechanism failed to operate in a reasonable timeframe.

What Have We Learned? — Full Recap

1. Multipliers: In IS-LM, the fiscal multiplier is smaller than the Keynesian Cross $\alpha_g$ because higher $Y$ raises money demand, raises $i$, and crowds out investment. Only at the ELB (or under IS-MP) does the full multiplier apply.

2. Crowding Out: $G \uparrow$ → IS right → $i \uparrow$ → $I \downarrow$. Output rises but by less than the pure Keynesian prediction.

3. AD Curve: Derived from IS-LM by varying $P$. Higher $P$ → $M/P \downarrow$ → LM left → $i \uparrow$ → $Y \downarrow$. AD is downward-sloping in $(P, Y)$ space.

4. ELB & Liquidity Trap: At zero lower bound, LM is horizontal — monetary policy ineffective. Fiscal policy regains full power (no crowding out through interest rates).

5. Great Depression: Both IS shocks (investment collapse, fiscal contraction) and LM dynamics (money contraction, banking panic) contributed. Deflation was mostly destabilizing through the debt-deflation and expected-deflation channels.
