IS-LM Applications

Policy Shocks, the Crowding Out Effect, and the Great Depression

1. Fiscal Policy and the Crowding Out Effect

The government decides to stimulate the economy. It builds highways, hospitals, and recruits civil servants (an increase in government spending, $G \uparrow$). According to the Keynesian Cross we saw in the previous chapter, this should massively increase GDP ($Y$) thanks to the multiplier. The IS curve shifts sharply to the right.

But the IS-LM model contains a crucial trap. Because the money market (LM) exists, this fiscal stimulus will not be as powerful as expected. This phenomenon is called the Crowding Out effect.

Mechanism: The battle for capital
1
The stimulus: The government increases $G$. Output $Y$ begins to increase due to the standard Keynesian multiplier. The IS curve shifts to the right.
2
The monetary tension: This new economic activity requires liquidity. People need more cash to carry out these new transactions (Money demand $L^d \uparrow$).
3
The rate spike: Since the Central Bank has not printed more money (LM is unchanged), this shortage of liquidity forces agents to sell bonds. The price of bonds falls, so the interest rate ($i$) shoots up.
4
The Crowding Out: Faced with these much higher interest rates, private firms cancel their investment projects ($I \downarrow$). The state has "crowded out" private investment.
Final result: GDP ($Y$) increases, but much less than what the pure Keynesian multiplier predicted, because the collapse of private investment has erased a part of the gains.
A question of sensitivity

Is this crowding out effect severe? It depends on the slope of the curves!

If investment is extremely sensitive to the interest rate (a very flat IS curve), the slightest rise in rates will massacre private investment: the crowding out effect is almost total.
If money demand does not care about the interest rate (a vertical LM curve), the rates will have to soar to stratospheric levels to restore equilibrium: here again, the crowding out effect is massive.

2. Monetary Policy Expansion

Now, suppose the government does nothing, but the Central Bank decides to act. It runs the printing presses and injects liquidity into the economy (an increase in the money supply, $M \uparrow$). The LM curve shifts downward/to the right.

Mechanism: The credit channel
1
The injection: The Central Bank increases the money supply ($M \uparrow$). The market is flooded with liquidity.
2
The rate drop: With too much money on their hands, agents buy bonds. The price of bonds soars, causing the interest rate ($i$) to plummet.
3
The investment boom: Credit has become incredibly cheap. Firms jump on the opportunity to borrow and build factories. Investment explodes ($I \uparrow$).
4
The multiplier kicks in: This new investment generates jobs, which generate consumption, driving up GDP ($Y \uparrow$).
Final result: The interest rate has fallen, investment is strong, and GDP increases.
Policy Mix (The combination)

What happens if the government and the Central Bank work together? Imagine the state does a fiscal stimulus ($G \uparrow$), which normally causes crowding out. But at the very same moment, the Central Bank prints money ($M \uparrow$) to keep the interest rate artificially low!

Result: The Central Bank "accommodates" the fiscal policy. The interest rate does not change, crowding out is neutralized, and GDP ($Y$) shoots up to its maximum potential.

3. The Zero Lower Bound and the Liquidity Trap

The monetary policy we just described sounds magical. If there is a crisis, the Central Bank just needs to lower interest rates to restart the machine. But there is a physical limit to this magic: interest rates cannot easily go below 0%. This is the Zero Lower Bound (ZLB).

The Liquidity Trap

The mechanism When the interest rate reaches 0%, bonds no longer pay anything. From an investor's perspective, a bond that pays 0% is exactly the same thing as a wad of cash under a mattress. Money and bonds become perfect substitutes.
The impotence of the BC If the Central Bank prints money to buy bonds (to inject liquidity), investors simply trade one zero-yield asset for another zero-yield asset. The new money is just hoarded by banks and individuals. It does not enter the real economy.
The LM curve shape At 0%, the LM curve becomes perfectly horizontal. Any increase in the money supply ($M \uparrow$) just stretches this flat line further to the right, without lowering the rate (since it's already at zero) and without increasing GDP. "It's like pushing on a string," as Keynes famously said.

Conclusion: In a liquidity trap, monetary policy is completely dead. Only a massive fiscal stimulus (Fiscal Policy) can shift the IS curve to save the economy.

4. Case Study: The Great Depression (1929-1933)

Between 1929 and 1933, the US GDP fell by 30% and unemployment exploded. How does the IS-LM model explain the greatest economic catastrophe of the 20th century? Economists are divided between two major hypotheses.

Hypothesis 1: An IS Curve Shock

According to this hypothesis, the fault lies in a massive collapse of aggregate demand.

  • Stock market crash of 1929: Destroyed household wealth, completely collapsing Consumption ($C \downarrow$).
  • Drop in real estate: A collapse in residential investment ($I \downarrow$).
  • Bank failures: Without banks to lend to them, businesses could no longer invest, even if they wanted to ($I \downarrow$).
  • Fiscal mistake: The government actually raised taxes to balance its budget in the middle of a crisis, crushing demand further.

Result: A massive leftward shift of the IS curve.

Hypothesis 2: An LM Curve Shock

According to Milton Friedman and the monetarists, the fault lies entirely with the Central Bank.

  • The Fed's passivity: Following the bank runs, the money supply collapsed by 25%.
  • The contraction: The Fed refused to inject liquidity to save the failing banks.

Result: A massive leftward shift of the LM curve.

The flaw in this theory: If LM had shifted left, interest rates should have skyrocketed. Yet, nominal interest rates actually fell during the Depression. This points back to the IS shock theory.

The ultimate killer: The Debt-Deflation Fisher Effect

There is a piece missing from the puzzle. Prices fell drastically during the Depression (massive deflation). Why did this make things so much worse?

The destructive cycle of deflation

Remember that investment depends on the real interest rate ($r = i - \pi^e$).

When deflation hits, expected inflation ($\pi^e$) becomes deeply negative. If your bank lends you money at a nominal rate of 2% ($i = 2\%$), but prices are falling by 10% a year ($\pi^e = -10\%$), the real cost of your loan is $2\% - (-10\%) = 12\%$!

The trap: Even if the Central Bank lowers nominal rates ($i$) to 0%, the real rate ($r$) remains extremely high because of deflation. This crushes investment, which shifts IS further to the left, which worsens the recession, which causes even more deflation... It is a deadly downward spiral.
