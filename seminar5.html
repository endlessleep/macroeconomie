Seminar 5: Shock Analysis (MP & Disinflation)

Analyzing central bank interventions, monetary policy errors, and shifting targets.

Exercise 1

Monetary Policy Shock ($\eta_t$)

The standard Monetary Policy Rule (MPR) is augmented with a monetary policy shock $\eta_t$:

Augmented Taylor Rule

$i_t = \pi_t + \rho + \theta_\pi (\pi_t - \pi_t^*) + \theta_y (y_t - \bar{y}_t) + \eta_t$

  1. What does $\eta_t$ represent economically?
  2. Derive the modified Dynamic Aggregate Demand (DAD).
  3. For a one-period shock $\eta_0 = -1$ (unexpected rate cut), calculate the period 0 effects on all variables.
  4. Trace the dynamics in subsequent periods with $\eta_t = 0$ for $t \ge 1$.
Show Solution
Q1 — Economic Interpretation of $\eta_t$

The shock $\eta_t$ represents any deviation of the central bank from its own Taylor Rule. It captures discretionary monetary policy actions that cannot be explained by current inflation or the output gap.

Interpreting the sign:

  • If $\eta_t > 0$: The central bank sets interest rates higher than what the rule prescribes. This is an unexpected rate hike (hawkish mistake / deliberate tightening).
  • If $\eta_t < 0$: The central bank sets interest rates lower than prescribed. This is an unexpected rate cut (dovish mistake / artificial stimulus).
Q2 — Deriving the Modified DAD

To find the DAD, we substitute the Fisher equation and the new MPR into the IS equation.

1. The 3 core equations:

  • IS: $y_t = \bar{y} - \alpha(r_t - \rho) + \epsilon_t$
  • Fisher + Expectations: $r_t = i_t - \pi_t$ (assuming $E_t\pi_{t+1} = \pi_t$)
  • New MP: $i_t = \pi_t + \rho + \theta_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \theta_y(y_t - \bar{y}) + \eta_t$

2. Substitute Fisher into IS:

$y_t = \bar{y} - \alpha(i_t - \pi_t - \rho) + \epsilon_t$

3. Substitute MP into the new IS expression:

$y_t = \bar{y} - \alpha \left[ \pi_t + \rho + \theta_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \theta_y(y_t - \bar{y}) + \eta_t - \pi_t - \rho \right] + \epsilon_t$

Notice that $\pi_t$ and $\rho$ cancel out directly inside the brackets:

$y_t - \bar{y} = -\alpha \left[ \theta_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \theta_y(y_t - \bar{y}) + \eta_t \right] + \epsilon_t$

4. Isolate the output gap $(y_t - \bar{y})$:

$(1 + \alpha\theta_y)(y_t - \bar{y}) = -\alpha\theta_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*) - \alpha\eta_t + \epsilon_t$
Modified DAD

$y_t = \bar{y} - \frac{\alpha\theta_\pi}{1+\alpha\theta_y}(\pi_t - \pi^*) - \frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha\theta_y}\eta_t + \frac{1}{1+\alpha\theta_y}\epsilon_t$

An unexpected rate cut ($\eta_t < 0$) mathematically shifts the DAD curve to the right, acting exactly like a positive demand shock.

Q3 — Period 0 Effects ($\eta_0 = -1$)

Assuming standard parameters ($\alpha = 1, \phi = 0.25, \theta_\pi = 0.5, \theta_y = 0.5$) and no other shocks ($\epsilon_0 = 0$), the DAD slope coefficient is $1/3 \approx 0.33$ and the shock multiplier is $1/1.5 = 2/3 \approx 0.67$.

  • DAD$_0$: $y_0 - 100 = -0.33(\pi_0 - 2) - 0.67(-1)$ $\implies y_0 - 100 = -0.33(\pi_0 - 2) + 0.67$
  • DAS$_0$: $\pi_0 - 2 = 0.25(y_0 - 100)$

Substitute DAS into DAD:

$y_0 - 100 = -0.33 \cdot \left[ 0.25(y_0 - 100) \right] + 0.67$
$y_0 - 100 = -0.083(y_0 - 100) + 0.67$
$1.083(y_0 - 100) = 0.67 \implies y_0 - 100 \approx 0.62$

Period 0 Results:

  • Output: $y_0 = 100 + 0.62 = \mathbf{100.62}$ *(Output rises, boom)*
  • Inflation: $\pi_0 = 2 + 0.25(0.62) = \mathbf{2.155}$ *(Inflation rises)*
  • Nominal Rate: $i_0 = 2.155 + 2 + 0.5(0.155) + 0.5(0.62) - 1 = \mathbf{3.54}$ *(Rate is forcefully lowered)*
  • Real Rate: $r_0 = 3.54 - 2.155 = \mathbf{1.385}$ *(Lower than natural rate 2, stimulating demand)*
Q4 — Subsequent Dynamics ($\eta_t = 0$)

In period $t=1$, the central bank stops its discretionary cut and strictly follows the Taylor rule again ($\eta_1=0$). The DAD curve shifts back to its original position.

However, expected inflation is now higher, based on the period 0 inflation spike ($\pi^e_1 = \pi_0 = 2.155$). This shifts the DAS curve upward.

To fight this inherited inflation, the Taylor rule will automatically generate a high real interest rate, pushing the economy into a localized recession ($y_1 < 100$) to "squeeze" the inflation out of the system.

Convergence of Variables: The short boom is followed by a hangover recession to correct the resulting inflation.

Exercise 2

Task 5: Disinflation Strategy

The economy is in long-run equilibrium at an unfortunately high level: $\pi = \pi^* = 4$. In period 0, the Central Bank announces a new, lower target: $\pi_{new}^* = 2$.

  1. What must happen to output for inflation to decline?
  2. Calculate the immediate impact on $i_0$ and $r_0$.
  3. Trace the path of all variables over time.
  4. Show the dynamics in the DAD-DAS diagram.
Show Solution
Q1 — The Requirement for Disinflation

From the Phillips curve: $\Delta\pi_t = \phi(y_t - \bar{y})$.

If we want inflation to fall over time ($\Delta\pi_t < 0$), we mathematically require a negative output gap ($y_t < \bar{y}$).

Economically, the central bank must intentionally induce a recession to reduce aggregate demand, forcing firms to slow their price increases.

Q2 — Immediate Impact on Interest Rates

At $t=0$, the new target $\pi^*=2$ is inserted into the Taylor Rule. Since past inflation is stuck at $4$, the rule perceives a massive positive inflation gap ($\pi_0 - \pi^* > 0$).

Step-by-step resolution:

The DAD curve permanently shifts to the left because of the lower target. Using standard parameters and solving the DAD/DAS system for $\pi^*=2$ and $\pi_{t-1}=4$:

  • DAD: $y_0 - 100 = -0.33(\pi_0 - 2)$
  • DAS: $\pi_0 = 4 + 0.25(y_0 - 100)$

Substitute DAD into DAS:

$\pi_0 - 4 = 0.25 \left[ -0.33(\pi_0 - 2) \right]$
$\pi_0 - 4 = -0.083\pi_0 + 0.166$
$1.083\pi_0 = 4.166 \implies \pi_0 \approx \mathbf{3.84}$

And Output:

$y_0 - 100 = -0.33(3.84 - 2) = -0.61 \implies y_0 = \mathbf{99.39}$

Impact on interest rates:

  • Nominal Rate: $i_0 = 3.84 + 2 + 0.5(3.84 - 2) + 0.5(-0.61) = 5.84 + 0.92 - 0.305 = \mathbf{6.455}$
  • Real Rate: $r_0 = i_0 - \pi_0 = 6.455 - 3.84 = \mathbf{2.615}$

Both rates jump immediately. The $r_0$ rises sharply above the natural rate (2), confirming the restrictive policy that causes the recession ($y_0 = 99.39$).

Q3 — The Path Over Time

Inflation does not instantly fall to 2. It drops to 3.84 in period 0, then grinds down period after period due to adaptive expectations keeping it sticky.

Output remains trapped in a recession ($y_t < 100$) for several periods, only slowly recovering to 100 as inflation finally approaches the new target of 2.

Disinflation over time: A prolonged recession is required to break entrenched inflation expectations.

Q4 — DAD-DAS Dynamics
  • Period 0: The DAD curve permanently shifts to the left (downward) due to the lowered target $\pi^*=2$. The economy slides down the existing old DAS curve ($DAS_{-1}$) to a lower output and slightly lower inflation ($E_0$).
  • Periods $1 \dots \infty$: As inflation slowly falls, expected inflation falls with it. This causes the DAS curve to gradually shift downward in tiny steps every period.
  • Convergence: The sequence ends when the DAS curve has fallen fully to intersect the new DAD curve exactly at potential output ($y=100$) and the new target inflation ($\pi=2$).
