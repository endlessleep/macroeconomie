Seminar 4: Shock Analysis (continued)

Expectations & Demand Shocks — Mathematical impact of anchors and shifts.

Exercise 1

Partially Anchored Expectations

Assume that inflation expectations follow: $$\pi_t^e - \pi^* = \psi(\pi_{t-1} - \pi^*), \quad 0 < \psi < 1$$

  1. Modify the DAS curve to incorporate this expectations mechanism.
  2. For a one-period supply shock $\nu_0 = 1$ with $\psi = 0.7$, calculate the path of inflation for periods 0, 1, and 2.
  3. Compare with fully adaptive expectations ($\psi = 1$). How much faster does inflation return to target?
Show Solution
1 — Modified DAS Curve

First, a reminder about the interpretation of $\psi$:

  • If $\psi=1$: Fully adaptive expectations. Expected inflation fully follows last period's inflation: $\pi_t^e = \pi_{t-1}$.
  • If $\psi=0$: Fully anchored expectations. Expected inflation is always equal to the target: $\pi_t^e = \pi^*$.
  • If $0 < \psi < 1$: Partially anchored expectations. Expected inflation depends partly on last period's inflation gap and partly on the target.

Rewrite the expectations equation:

$\pi_t^e = \pi^* + \psi(\pi_{t-1} - \pi^*)$

Define the inflation gap as $\tilde{\pi}_t = \pi_t - \pi^*$. Then expected inflation is:

$\pi_t^e = \pi^* + \psi \tilde{\pi}_{t-1}$

Substitute this into the Phillips curve / DAS relation:

$\pi_t = \pi^* + \psi \tilde{\pi}_{t-1} + \phi(y_t - \bar{y}) + \nu_t$

Subtracting $\pi^*$ from both sides, we get the modified DAS:

$\tilde{\pi}_t = \psi \tilde{\pi}_{t-1} + \phi(y_t - \bar{y}) + \nu_t$

Interpretation

Persistence is inherited from $\psi$. When $0 < \psi < 1$, inflation is still persistent, but less than under full adaptive expectations.

2 — Path Analysis (t=0, 1, 2)

Parameters: $\psi = 0.7$, $\phi = 0.25$, $\bar{y} = 100$, $\pi^* = 2$.
Initial state: $\tilde{\pi}_{-1} = 0$. One-period shock: $\nu_0 = 1$.

Period $t=0$ (The Shock Hits)

DAS: $\tilde{\pi}_0 = 0.7(0) + 0.25(y_0 - 100) + 1 \implies \tilde{\pi}_0 = 0.25(y_0 - 100) + 1$

DAD: $y_0 - 100 = -\frac{1}{3}\tilde{\pi}_0$

Substitute DAD into DAS: $\tilde{\pi}_0 = 0.25(-\frac{1}{3}\tilde{\pi}_0) + 1 \implies \frac{13}{12}\tilde{\pi}_0 = 1$

$\boxed{\tilde{\pi}_0 = \frac{12}{13} \approx 0.923} \quad \boxed{\pi_0 \approx 2.923} \quad \boxed{y_0 = \frac{1296}{13} \approx 99.692}$

Period $t=1$ (Persistence remains)

$\nu_1 = 0$, $\psi \tilde{\pi}_0 = 0.7 \cdot \frac{12}{13} = \frac{42}{65}$

DAS: $\tilde{\pi}_1 = \frac{42}{65} - \frac{1}{12}\tilde{\pi}_1 \implies \frac{13}{12}\tilde{\pi}_1 = \frac{42}{65}$

$\boxed{\tilde{\pi}_1 = \frac{504}{845} \approx 0.596} \quad \boxed{\pi_1 \approx 2.596} \quad \boxed{y_1 \approx 99.801}$

Period $t=2$ (Convergence)

$\psi \tilde{\pi}_1 = 0.7 \cdot \frac{504}{845} = \frac{3528}{8450}$

DAS: $\tilde{\pi}_2 = \frac{3528}{8450} \cdot \frac{12}{13} = \frac{42336}{109850} \approx 0.385$

$\boxed{\tilde{\pi}_2 \approx 0.385} \quad \boxed{\pi_2 \approx 2.385} \quad \boxed{y_2 \approx 99.872}$
3 — Comparison with Fully Adaptive Expectations ($\psi = 1$)

To compare, we take $\psi = 1$. The DAS equation becomes:

$\tilde{\pi}_t = 1 \cdot \tilde{\pi}_{t-1} + 0.25(y_t - 100) + \nu_t$

Period $t=0$

Since $\tilde{\pi}_{-1}=0$, $\tilde{\pi}_0 = 0.25(y_0 - 100) + 1$. Combined with DAD ($y_0 - 100 = -\frac{1}{3}\tilde{\pi}_0$):

$\boxed{\tilde{\pi}_0 = \frac{12}{13} \approx 0.923} \quad \boxed{\pi_0 \approx 2.923} \quad \boxed{y_0 \approx 99.692}$

Note: At $t=0$, the result is exactly the same as for $\psi=0.7$ because the lagged term $\psi \tilde{\pi}_{t-1}$ is zero.

Period $t=1$ ($\nu_1 = 0$)

DAS: $\tilde{\pi}_1 = 1 \cdot \frac{12}{13} + 0.25(y_1 - 100) \implies \tilde{\pi}_1 = \frac{12}{13} - \frac{1}{12}\tilde{\pi}_1$

$\boxed{\tilde{\pi}_1 = \frac{12}{13} \cdot \frac{12}{13} = \frac{144}{169} \approx 0.852} \quad \boxed{\pi_1 \approx 2.852} \quad \boxed{y_1 \approx 99.716}$

Period $t=2$ ($\nu_2 = 0$)

DAS: $\tilde{\pi}_2 = \frac{144}{169} - \frac{1}{12}\tilde{\pi}_2 \implies \frac{13}{12}\tilde{\pi}_2 = \frac{144}{169}$

$\boxed{\tilde{\pi}_2 = \frac{144}{169} \cdot \frac{12}{13} = \frac{1728}{2197} \approx 0.786} \quad \boxed{\pi_2 \approx 2.786} \quad \boxed{y_2 \approx 99.738}$

Speed of Return

With $\psi = 0.7$ (partially anchored): $\tilde{\pi}_1 \approx 0.596, \; \tilde{\pi}_2 \approx 0.385$.
With $\psi = 1$ (fully adaptive): $\tilde{\pi}_1 \approx 0.852, \; \tilde{\pi}_2 \approx 0.786$.

Conclusion: With $\psi = 0.7$, inflation is less persistent, so it returns to target much faster than under fully adaptive expectations ($\psi = 1$).

Visualizing the difference in convergence speed: anchored expectations act as a "stabilizing magnet".

Exercise 2

Fully Anchored Expectations

Recall — Expectations Mechanisms

  • $\psi = 1$ (Fully Adaptive): $\pi_t^e = \pi_{t-1}$. Expected inflation fully follows last period's inflation — no anchoring.
  • $\psi = 0$ (Fully Anchored): $\pi_t^e = \pi^*$. People always expect inflation to be equal to the target, regardless of past inflation.

Assume expectations are fully anchored: $\psi = 0 \implies \pi_t^e = \pi^*$.

Parameters: $\phi = 0.25$, $\bar{y} = 100$, $\pi^* = 2$. One-period supply shock: $\nu_0 = 1$ at $t=0$.

  1. Modify the DAS curve accordingly.
  2. What happens to inflation following a one-period supply shock?
  3. Does the central bank need to create a recession to stabilise inflation?
  4. Is the Taylor principle ($\theta_\pi > 0$) still necessary for stability?
  5. How does this relate to post-pandemic inflation dynamics in Czechia?
Show Solution
1 — Modified DAS Curve

Start from the standard DAS (Phillips curve):

$\pi_t = \pi_t^e + \phi(y_t - \bar{y}) + \nu_t$

With fully anchored expectations, $\psi = 0 \implies \pi_t^e = \pi^*$ for all $t$. Substituting:

$\pi_t = \pi^* + \phi(y_t - \bar{y}) + \nu_t$

Inflation no longer depends on past inflation. There is no intrinsic persistence — it is driven only by the current output gap and supply shocks.

2 — What happens to inflation after a one-period supply shock?

Parameters: $\phi = 0.25$, $\bar{y} = 100$, $\pi^* = 2$. One-period shock: $\nu_0 = 1$ at $t=0$, and $\nu_t = 0$ for $t \geq 1$.

Period $t=0$ (The Shock Hits)

DAS: $\pi_0 = \pi^* + 0.25(y_0 - 100) + \nu_0 = 2 + 0.25(y_0 - 100) + 1$

DAD: $y_0 = 100 - \frac{1}{3}(\pi_0 - \pi^*) = 100 - \frac{1}{3}(\pi_0 - 2)$

So: $y_0 - 100 = -\frac{1}{3}(\pi_0 - 2)$

Substitute DAD into DAS:

$\pi_0 = 3 + 0.25 \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{3}(\pi_0 - 2)\right) = 3 - \frac{1}{12}(\pi_0 - 2)$

$\pi_0 - 3 = -\frac{1}{12}(\pi_0 - 2) \implies 12(\pi_0 - 3) = -(\pi_0 - 2)$

$12\pi_0 - 36 = -\pi_0 + 2 \implies 13\pi_0 = 38$

$\boxed{\pi_0 = \frac{38}{13} \approx 2.923}$

Then: $y_0 = 100 - \frac{1}{3}\left(\frac{38}{13} - 2\right) = 100 - \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{12}{13} = 100 - \frac{4}{13} \approx 99.692$

$\boxed{y_0 \approx 99.692}$

Period $t=1$ (Shock Gone)

$\nu_1 = 0$. With fully anchored expectations: $\pi_1^e = \pi^* = 2$.

DAS: $\pi_1 = 2 + 0.25(y_1 - 100) + 0 = 2 + 0.25(y_1 - 100)$

DAD: $y_1 - 100 = -\frac{1}{3}(\pi_1 - 2)$

Substitute:

$\pi_1 = 2 + 0.25 \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{3}(\pi_1 - 2)\right) = 2 - \frac{1}{12}(\pi_1 - 2)$

$(\pi_1 - 2) = -\frac{1}{12}(\pi_1 - 2) \implies \frac{13}{12}(\pi_1 - 2) = 0$

$\boxed{\pi_1 = \pi^* = 2} \quad \boxed{y_1 = \bar{y} = 100}$

This holds for all $t \geq 1$: $\pi_t = \pi^* = 2$.

Key Insight

Inflation returns to target immediately after the shock disappears. Because $\pi_t^e = \pi^*$ always, expected inflation acts as a self-fulfilling anchor — there is no expectation-driven persistence.

3 — Does the CB need to create a recession?

From the calculation in Q2, at $t=1$:

$y_1 = \bar{y} = 100$

Output returns to its natural level automatically. The central bank does not need to create a recession (i.e., no negative output gap is needed) to bring inflation back to target.

This is the key advantage of fully anchored expectations: the economy self-corrects because there is no expectations-driven spiral to break.

4 — Is the Taylor Principle Still Necessary for Stability?

Recall the Taylor Rule:

$i_t = \pi_t + r^* + \theta_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \theta_y(y_t - \bar{y})$

The Taylor Principle ($\theta_\pi > 0$) is required so that the CB raises the real interest rate when inflation rises. Without it, the DAD curve becomes upward-sloping, creating an explosive spiral.

But under fully anchored expectations, let's test what happens if $\theta_\pi = -0.5 < 0$, keeping $\alpha = 1$, $\theta_y = 0.5$.

The DAD slope becomes $-\frac{\alpha(1+\theta_\pi)}{1 + \alpha\theta_y} = -\frac{1 \cdot 0.5}{1.5} = -\frac{1}{3}$, so the DAD is:

$y_t - \bar{y} = -\frac{1}{3}(\pi_t - \pi^*)$   (same as before — DAD remains downward-sloping)

Period $t=0$ with $\theta_\pi = -0.5$

DAS (with $\pi_0^e = \pi^* = 2$): $\pi_0 = 2 + 0.25(y_0 - 100) + 1$

DAD: $y_0 - 100 = -\frac{1}{3}(\pi_0 - 2)$

This is the same system as Q2. The calculation yields:

$\boxed{\pi_0 = \frac{38}{13} \approx 2.923}$   (same result as standard Taylor Principle)

Period $t=1$ with $\theta_\pi = -0.5$

$\nu_1 = 0$, $\pi_1^e = \pi^* = 2$. The DAS/DAD system is again the same as Q2:

$\boxed{\pi_1 = \pi^* = 2}$

Conclusion on the Taylor Principle

Even with $\theta_\pi < 0$ (violating the Taylor Principle), inflation still returns to $\pi^*$ from $t=1$ onward — as long as expectations are fully anchored.

Why? Because $\pi_t^e = \pi^*$ always, which eliminates any persistence in the DAS. The CB's reaction function matters much less when expectations themselves act as the stabilizer.

However, the risk is that the CB may lose credibility over time. If people observe the CB consistently under-reacting, they may start to de-anchor their expectations ($\psi$ drifts toward 1), at which point the Taylor Principle becomes critical again.

Figure (for reference): This graph shows what happens when the Taylor Principle is violated ($\theta_\pi \leq 0$) under non-anchored expectations: the DAD curve becomes upward-sloping. A demand shock pushes DAD right; output and inflation rise; higher inflation raises expected inflation, pushing DAS up. The new intersection keeps output above potential, and inflation spirals indefinitely.
Note: This explosive scenario does NOT occur here because $\pi_t^e = \pi^*$ always — the anchor short-circuits the spiral.

5 — Post-Pandemic Inflation Dynamics: The Case of Czechia

After the COVID-19 supply shocks of 2021–2022, Czechia experienced a sharp inflation surge. However, the Czech National Bank did not raise interest rates enough to fully match the Taylor Principle.

Despite this, inflation eventually returned toward target. The key reason: expectations were partially anchored ($0 < \psi < 1$). The public still broadly believed in the CB's long-run commitment to price stability, which limited the expectations-driven persistence.

Link to the Model

This episode illustrates why anchored expectations are so valuable: even if the CB under-reacts (violating the Taylor Principle), a sufficient degree of anchoring limits inflation persistence and allows the economy to return to target without a deep recession.

The risk is that sustained under-reaction erodes credibility and causes $\psi$ to drift toward 1 — at which point the Taylor Principle once again becomes critical for stability.

Exercise 3

One-period Demand Shock

The economy starts in equilibrium. A positive demand shock $\epsilon_0 = 2$ occurs in period 0.

  1. Determine the impact on $y_0, \pi_0, r_0$, and $i_0$.
  2. Why does output increase by less than the shock?
  3. Explain the mechanism connecting the shock to higher inflation.
  4. Show the impact graphically in the DAD-DAS diagram.
Show Solution
1 — Impact in Period 0

A positive demand shock shifts DAD right.

$y_0 \uparrow, \quad \pi_0 \uparrow, \quad r_0 \uparrow, \quad i_0 \uparrow$

Output and inflation rise; the CB reacts by raising nominal and real rates.

2 — Output vs. Shock Size

Output rises by less than 2 because the rise in inflation triggers a CB reaction. The resulting higher real interest rate dampens spending (crowding out part of the shock).

3 — Inflation Mechanism

Shock $\uparrow \implies y_0 > \bar{y} \implies$ Positive output gap $\implies$ Upward pressure on prices in the Phillips curve $\implies \pi_0 \uparrow$.

4 — Graphical Impact

DAD shifts rightward. The economy moves upward along the stationary DAS curve.

Exercise 4

Demand Shock Dynamics

Continue from Ex 3 with $\epsilon_t = 0$ for $t \ge 1$.

  1. What happens to output in period 1? Why does it fall below potential?
  2. Why does inflation remain above target in period 1?
  3. Trace the path of all variables for periods 1, 2, and 3.
  4. Why must policy remain restrictive after output returns to normal?
  5. Sketch the complete time paths through convergence.
Show Solution
1 — Period 1: The Undershoot

$\epsilon_1 = 0$, but $\pi_0$ was high. The CB keeps rates high. The restrictive policy depresses demand, leading to $y_1 < \bar{y}$.

2 — Inflation Persistence

Inflation remains above target because it inherits the high level from period 0 via expectations ($\pi_1^e = \pi_0$). It only declines gradually as $y < \bar{y}$ persists.

3 — Path of Variables

P1: $y_1 < \bar{y}, \pi_1 > \pi^*, r_1 > r^*, i_1 > i^*$ (Recession for disinflation).
P2: Output recovers toward $\bar{y}$, $\pi$ falls toward $\pi^*$, rates ease.
P3: All variables converge closer to long-run equilibrium.

4 — Restrictive Policy Logic

Policy must stay restrictive ($r > \rho$) to maintain the negative output gap until inflation is fully back at target. Early relaxation would let inflation remain stuck above target.

Time paths: Output undershoot followed by gradual convergence.
