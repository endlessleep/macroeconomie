FX and Interest Rate Parities

Revision Block: Comparing Financial Returns Across Borders

The General Principle

This section studies how investors compare financial assets denominated in different currencies. An investor does not only compare interest rates ($i_{domestic}$ vs $i_{foreign}$); they must also factor in the exchange rate, as investing abroad involves currency conversion today and conversion back in the future.

\text{Interest Return} + \text{Exchange Gain/Loss}

Key Exchange Rate Concepts

Spot Rate ($e_t$)

The exchange rate for immediate transactions today.

Example: $e_t = 25$ CZK/EUR means one Euro costs 25 CZK today.

  • $e_t \uparrow$ (25 to 26): CZK depreciates.
  • $e_t \downarrow$ (25 to 24): CZK appreciates.

Expected Rate ($E_t(e_{t+1})$)

The exchange rate investors expect today for the future. It is not guaranteed and carries uncertainty.

Critical for Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP).

Forward Rate ($F_t$)

The rate fixed today by contract for a transaction that will happen in the future. It removes all exchange-rate uncertainty.

Critical for Covered Interest Parity (CIP).

Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP)

UIP compares expected returns across currencies when the investor does not cover exchange-rate risk.

The UIP Intuition

Investors should be indifferent between domestic and foreign assets once expected currency movements are factored in. If one country offers a higher interest rate, it must be offset by an expected depreciation of its currency.

$i_d > i_f \Rightarrow \text{Domestic currency is expected to depreciate}$

The UIP Distinction: Immediate vs. Future

  • Immediate Reaction: $i_d \uparrow \Rightarrow \text{Capital Inflows} \Rightarrow \text{Currency Appreciates TODAY}$.
  • Future Adjustment: $i_d > i_f \Rightarrow \text{Expected Future DEPRECIATION}$ (to equalize returns).
How to Solve a UIP Exercise
  1. Identify variables: Find $e_t$, $i_d$, and $i_f$ from the prompt.
  2. Apply the equation: Use the specific formula provided in the question (quotations vary!).
  3. Solve for expectations: Calculate $E_t(e_{t+1})$.
  4. Compare: If $e_t$ is (domestic/foreign):
    • $E_t(e_{t+1}) > e_t \Rightarrow$ Expected Depreciation.
    • $E_t(e_{t+1}) < e_t \Rightarrow$ Expected Appreciation.

Covered Interest Parity (CIP)

The CIP Principle

Once the future rate is locked in via $F_t$, domestic and foreign investments must offer the same riskless return. Otherwise, arbitrage is possible.

Dominance Rule

"Which investment dominates?" means "Which gives a higher final return?" Compare $i_d$ vs. the covered strategy $(e_t, i_f, F_t)$.

Arbitrage Strategy

The rule is simple: Borrow in the low-return strategy, invest in the high-return strategy.

  • If covered foreign return > domestic: Borrow domestic $\rightarrow$ Convert at $e_t$ $\rightarrow$ Invest at $i_f$ $\rightarrow$ Lock exit with $F_t$.
  • If domestic return > covered foreign: Borrow foreign $\rightarrow$ Convert at $e_t$ $\rightarrow$ Invest at $i_d$ $\rightarrow$ Lock exit with $F_t$.

Real Interest Parity

Investors care about real returns, not just nominal ones. A country may have a high nominal rate, but if its inflation is higher, the real return may be lower.

Calculation Workflow
  1. Compute domestic real return: $r_d \approx i_d - E_t \pi_d$
  2. Compute foreign real return: $r_f \approx i_f - E_t \pi_f$
  3. Compare: Parity holds if $r_d = r_f$.

Final Revision Summary

UIP Uses $E_t(e_{t+1})$. Expected returns with exchange-rate risk. High $i \rightarrow$ Expected depreciation.
CIP Uses $F_t$. Covered riskless returns. Any deviation allows risk-free arbitrage.
Real Parity Adjusts for inflation. High nominal rates do not guarantee high real gains.
