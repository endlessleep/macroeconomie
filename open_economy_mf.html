Mundell-Fleming & Interest Rate Parity

IS*-LM* Framework, Policy Effectiveness, Impossible Trinity, UIP and CIP

1. The Mundell-Fleming Model: IS-LM goes Global

In the previous chapter (the classic SOE model), we studied the long run, where prices are perfectly flexible and the economy always operates at its full potential. But what happens in the short run? What happens when prices are "sticky" and factories can run empty?

To answer this, Robert Mundell and Marcus Fleming had a brilliant idea in the 1960s: take the good old IS-LM model and transpose it into an open economy. It is the ultimate tool to understand whether an economic policy (fiscal or monetary) will be effective or not depending on the exchange rate regime chosen by the country.

 The Architecture of the Model

IS* Curve (Goods Market) The equation is $Y = C + I + G + NX(e)$. The big change is $NX(e)$. Aggregate demand now depends on the exchange rate $e$. If $e$ rises (depreciation of our currency), our goods become cheaper for foreigners, $NX$ explodes, and IS* shifts to the right.
LM* Curve (Money Market) The equation is $M/P = L(r^*, Y)$. Notice that money demand depends on the global interest rate $r^*$ (which is externally fixed). The exchange rate $e$ appears nowhere! Consequently, in a graph with $e$ on the vertical axis and $Y$ on the horizontal axis, LM* is a vertical line.
Perfect Capital Mobility The same unforgiving assumption as in the SOE model: capital moves so fast that our interest rate can never deviate from the global rate. $r = r^*$.
Who absorbs the shocks? The vertical axis.

In the classic closed IS-LM model, the vertical axis represented the interest rate $i$. If there was an imbalance, $i$ would rise or fall to bring the economy back to normal.

But in Mundell-Fleming, the interest rate is locked at $r^*$! It can no longer move. Who will therefore act as the "shock absorber" for the economy? It is the Exchange Rate ($e$). This is why we place it on the vertical axis of the graph.

2. Policy Effectiveness under Floating Rates

Under "floating" exchange rates, the Central Bank lets financial markets dictate the value of the currency. The exchange rate $e$ rises and falls freely according to the supply and demand for foreign currencies.

Fiscal Policy — INEFFECTIVE

In a closed economy, the government could stimulate the economy (at the cost of some crowding out). In a floating open economy, the government stimulus is completely neutralized by the flight of exports. Let's trace the mechanism:

Mechanism: Total crowding out by the exchange rate
1
The spark: The government spends massively ($G \uparrow$). Mechanically, the IS* curve attempts to shift to the right, pushing $Y$ upward.
2
The monetary tension: With this frantic activity, local agents need more liquidity. The local interest rate attempts to rise (it starts to exceed $r^*$).
3
The foreign influx: Investors worldwide see that local rates are slightly higher. They rush to buy our currency to take advantage of these yields.
4
The fatal appreciation: This massive demand for our currency makes its value skyrocket. The exchange rate appreciates ($e \downarrow$). Suddenly, our exports cost a fortune for foreigners!
5
Back to square one: Exports collapse ($NX \downarrow$). The IS* curve reverses course until it returns exactly to its starting point.
Final result on $Y$: ZERO. The stimulus effect was killed by the loss of export competitiveness.
Monetary Policy — HIGHLY EFFECTIVE

While the government is powerless, the Central Bank becomes the master of the game. By flooding the market with money, it triggers a fierce competitive devaluation.

Mechanism: Stealing foreign demand
1
The shock: The Central Bank prints money ($M \uparrow$). The vertical LM* line shifts abruptly to the right.
2
The capital flight: With all this money in circulation, the local interest rate attempts to fall below the global rate $r^*$. Local investors, disappointed by these miserable yields, sell their currency to invest abroad.
3
The saving depreciation: This massive sale of our currency makes it crash on the foreign exchange markets. The currency depreciates strongly ($e \uparrow$).
4
The export boom: Our products become incredibly cheap for foreigners. Factory order books explode. $NX$ soars.
Final result on $Y$: STRONG INCREASE. Monetary policy worked by "stealing" demand from neighboring countries (a Beggar-thy-neighbor policy).

3. Policy Effectiveness under Fixed Rates

Forget everything we just said! Under "fixed" exchange rates, the Central Bank promises to keep the value of the currency constant ($e = \bar{e}$). This is a heavy commitment: it loses control of its monetary policy.

How to defend a Peg (fixed rate)?

Imagine the market attacks the currency and wants to push it down. How does the Central Bank maintain the value? It must play the role of buyer of last resort. It taps into its reserves of gold or dollars, and uses them to massively buy back its own currency on the market.

By buying back its own currency, it removes it from circulation. The money supply ($M$) mechanically falls. Conversely, if the currency is too strong, it prints fresh bills and buys foreign currencies, which increases $M$.

Crucial conclusion: Under fixed rates, the money supply $M$ is no longer decided by the Central Bank. It adjusts automatically (it becomes endogenous) solely to maintain the exchange rate.

Fiscal Policy — HIGHLY EFFECTIVE

The curse of crowding out by the exchange rate is lifted. If the government spends more ($G \uparrow$), this creates an upward pressure on interest rates, which attracts foreign capital. The currency wants to appreciate.

But the Central Bank opposes it! To prevent the appreciation, it prints money ($M \uparrow$). The LM* curve shifts to the right. Result: the government stimulated the economy, AND the central bank involuntarily helped it by injecting liquidity. GDP ($Y$) explodes.

Monetary Policy — IMPOSSIBLE

If the Central Bank nonetheless tries to stimulate the economy ($M \uparrow$), it creates a drop in rates, capital flees, and the currency begins to depreciate.

But, it has promised to maintain the fixed rate! It is therefore forced to immediately buy back all the bills it just printed by selling its reserves ($M \downarrow$). The policy cancels itself out within a second.

Synthesis Table (Most Important)

Memorize this perfect symmetry: what works in a floating regime fails in a fixed regime, and vice-versa.

🌊 Floating Rates (The market decides) ⚓ Fixed Rates (The CB intervenes)

Fiscal
$G \uparrow$ or $T \downarrow$		 INEFFECTIVE The stimulus appreciates the currency. Exports collapse and cancel the effect. VERY EFFECTIVE The stimulus creates tension, the CB prints bills to defend the peg. Double stimulus.

4. The Impossible Trinity (The Trilemma)

What we have just discovered hides a ruthless law of international economics. No country in the world can "have its cake and eat it too". A country can only achieve two of the three following goals simultaneously:

1. Free Capital Flow

Investors can move money in and out of the country without restrictions.

2. Fixed Exchange Rate

A stable currency, excellent for reassuring importers and exporters.

3. Independent Monetary Policy

The Central Bank can cut interest rates to fight unemployment.

Proof by example:

The US / European Choice: The Fed and the ECB want to control their interest rates to fight crises (Goal 3), and they want to let Wall Street trade freely (Goal 1). The mathematical consequence? They sacrifice the fixed rate. The Dollar and the Euro float.
The Danish / Bulgarian Choice: These countries desperately want to keep their currency pegged to the Euro (Goal 2) while allowing capital to flow freely (Goal 1). Consequence? They sacrifice their monetary sovereignty. If the ECB raises its rates, the Bulgarian central bank must do exactly the same, even if its economy is in recession.
The Historical Chinese Choice: China wanted an artificially weak currency (Goal 2) while controlling its internal economy with its own interest rates (Goal 3). How did they manage? By establishing fierce capital controls (Sacrificing Goal 1). Citizens were not allowed to move their millions out of the country.
