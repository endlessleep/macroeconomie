Mundell-Fleming & Interest Rate Parity

IS*-LM* Framework, Policy Effectiveness, Impossible Trinity, UIP and CIP

The Mundell-Fleming Model — What and Why

This is a Short-Run Model

Mundell-Fleming (MF) is the short-run open-economy extension of IS-LM. Unlike the SOE long-run model (where output is at full employment), MF assumes sticky prices — output is demand-determined. The key question: does fiscal or monetary policy work, and under which exchange rate regime?

 Model Setup

IS* Curve (Goods) $Y = C(Y-T) + I(r^*) + G + NX(e)$. Output depends on domestic demand AND net exports. The exchange rate $e$ is the new adjustment variable. Since $r = r^*$ is fixed, only $e$ (and thus $NX$) can clear the goods market.
LM* Curve (Money) $M/P = L(r^*, Y)$. Money supply is fixed by the CB; money demand depends on income $Y$ and the fixed world rate $r^*$. For a given $M/P$, LM* determines the equilibrium $Y$ — independently of $e$. LM* is therefore vertical in $(Y, e)$ space!
Perfect Capital Mobility Any incipient deviation of domestic $r$ from $r^*$ triggers instantaneous capital flows that force $r = r^*$. This is the key assumption — it means the exchange rate (not the interest rate) must do all the adjustment.
Exchange Rate Convention ($e$) $e$ = units of domestic currency per unit of foreign currency. $e \uparrow$ = depreciation (domestic goods cheaper → $NX \uparrow$). $e \downarrow$ = appreciation (domestic goods dearer → $NX \downarrow$).

Policies Under Floating Exchange Rates

Under floating rates, $e$ is determined by the market and adjusts freely. The CB does not intervene. The exchange rate is the shock absorber.

Fiscal Policy — INEFFECTIVE under floating rates
Full Chain Reaction — Fiscal Expansion ($G \uparrow$)
1
$G \uparrow$ → IS* shifts right → at initial $e$, output $Y$ tends to rise
2
Higher $Y$ → money demand $L^d \uparrow$ → domestic $r$ tends to rise above $r^*$
3
Capital inflows to arbitrage the higher return → domestic currency appreciates ($e \downarrow$)
4
Appreciation makes domestic goods expensive → $NX \downarrow$ → IS* shifts back to original position
Net effect on $Y$: zero. Complete crowding out through the exchange rate. $e \downarrow$ (appreciation). $NX \downarrow$.

Intuition: Every dollar of increased government demand is exactly offset by a dollar of lost net exports. Fiscal policy under a float merely changes the composition of demand (more G, less NX) — not the level of output.

Monetary Policy — HIGHLY EFFECTIVE under floating rates
Full Chain Reaction — Monetary Expansion ($M \uparrow$)
1
$M \uparrow$ → LM* shifts right → at old $e$, output $Y$ tends to rise
2
More liquidity → domestic $r$ tends to fall below $r^*$
3
Capital outflows seeking better returns abroad → domestic currency depreciates ($e \uparrow$)
4
Depreciation makes domestic goods cheaper → $NX \uparrow$ → IS* shifts right → $Y$ rises further
Net effect on $Y$: large positive. $e \uparrow$ (depreciation). $NX \uparrow$. Monetary expansion is doubly effective.

Intuition: Monetary expansion works by stealing demand from foreign producers (expenditure-switching). It is a "beggar-thy-neighbour" policy — domestic output rises at the expense of foreign exporters.

Trade Policy — INEFFECTIVE under floating rates
Full Chain Reaction — Tariff or Import Quota
1
Tariff reduces imports → direct $NX \uparrow$ → IS* shifts right
2
Same mechanism as fiscal expansion: $Y \uparrow$ → $r > r^*$ → capital inflows → currency appreciates ($e \downarrow$)
3
Appreciation destroys exports → $NX$ falls back to original level
Net effect on $Y$: zero. Net effect on $NX$: zero. Only the composition changes: fewer imports AND fewer exports.

Policy lesson: Protectionism in a floating-rate world does not reduce trade deficits. It creates sectoral unemployment (export workers lose jobs) while ostensibly protecting import-competing workers.

Policies Under Fixed Exchange Rates

Under a fixed regime, the CB commits to maintain $e = \bar{e}$. To do this, it buys or sells foreign currency using reserves. The cost: monetary policy ceases to be independent — $M$ must adjust endogenously to keep $e$ fixed.

How it Works

If $e$ tends to rise (depreciation pressure), the CB sells foreign currency (buys domestic) → $M$ shrinks → $e$ returns to $\bar{e}$.

If $e$ tends to fall (appreciation pressure), the CB buys foreign currency (sells domestic) → $M$ expands → $e$ returns to $\bar{e}$.

The Key Risk: Speculative Attack

If markets believe the peg will break, they massively sell domestic currency. The CB must intervene, draining reserves. A currency crisis occurs when reserves run out before the attack subsides.

See the Mexico 1994 Peso Crisis case study below.

Policy Effectiveness Under Fixed Rates

Fiscal Policy — HIGHLY EFFECTIVE under fixed rates
Chain Reaction — $G \uparrow$ under fixed $e$
1
$G \uparrow$ → IS* shifts right → tendency for $r > r^*$ and $e \downarrow$ (appreciation)
2
CB buys foreign currency to stop appreciation → $M \uparrow$ → LM* shifts right
3
Both IS* (right) and LM* (right) → $Y$ rises substantially. $e$ unchanged.
$Y$ rises strongly. No exchange-rate crowding out. Fiscal policy works!
Monetary Policy — TOTALLY INEFFECTIVE under fixed rates
Chain Reaction — $M \uparrow$ under fixed $e$
1
CB tries to expand: $M \uparrow$ → LM* shifts right → $r < r^*$ → capital outflows → depreciation pressure
2
To defend the peg, CB must sell foreign reserves and buy domestic currency back → $M \downarrow$
3
LM* reverts exactly back to its original position. Net effect: nothing changed.
$Y$ unchanged. The CB's monetary expansion is instantly sterilized by its own peg-defense obligation.
Trade Policy — EFFECTIVE under fixed rates
Chain Reaction — Tariff under fixed $e$
1
Tariff → $NX \uparrow$ → IS* right → appreciation pressure
2
CB buys foreign currency → $M \uparrow$ → LM* shifts right
3
Both curves shift right → $Y$ rises. $e$ unchanged.
$Y$ rises. $NX$ rises (tariff fully effective here — exchange rate cannot undo it).

Risk Premium and Currency Crises

Our baseline assumes domestic and foreign assets are perfect substitutes, so $r = r^*$ exactly. In practice, a country risk premium $\theta$ can drive a wedge:

$r = r^* + \theta$

A rise in $\theta$ means investors demand extra compensation to hold domestic assets (political risk, default risk, liquidity risk). The IS* and LM* equations both incorporate $\theta$.

Effect of a Risk Premium Shock ($\theta \uparrow$)
Chain Reaction — $\theta \uparrow$
1
Higher $\theta$ → effective rate $r = r^* + \theta \uparrow$ → investment $I(r) \downarrow$ → IS* shifts left
2
Simultaneously: higher $r$ → money demand $L^d(r) \downarrow$ → with fixed $M/P$, money market clears at lower $Y$ → LM* shifts right (down)
3
Capital flight: investors sell domestic assets → domestic currency depreciates sharply ($e \uparrow$)
Output falls (IS* left dominates). Exchange rate depreciates massively. Classic signs of an emerging-market crisis.

Case Study: Mexico Peso Crisis, December 1994

In 1994, the combination of political shocks (Chiapas uprising, presidential assassination) and rising U.S. interest rates pushed Mexico's risk premium $\theta$ sharply upward. Capital fled the country. Mexico tried to defend its fixed exchange rate (the peso-dollar peg) by selling dollar reserves — but not publicly, so markets didn't know how depleted they were.

On December 20, 1994, Mexico announced a devaluation. Markets saw that reserves had nearly run dry, triggering a full panic. The peso depreciated over 40% in weeks. This is the textbook speculative attack: once the peg looks indefensible, it becomes indefensible as a self-fulfilling prophecy.

Lesson: A fixed exchange rate provides stability in normal times but is catastrophically fragile when facing large risk-premium shocks and thin reserves.

The Impossible Trinity (Trilemma)

The Mundell-Fleming model embeds a fundamental constraint of international macroeconomics: a country cannot simultaneously achieve all three of the following goals.

Free Capital Flows

No restrictions on cross-border financial transactions.

Fixed Exchange Rate

Stable nominal peg to a foreign currency.

Independent Monetary Policy

CB sets interest rates to manage inflation and output.

Every country in practice must sacrifice one corner of the triangle:

Option 1 — U.S., Euro Area, UK: Free Capital Flows + Independent Monetary Policy → sacrifice the fixed rate → floating exchange rates.
Option 2 — Hong Kong, Bulgaria (Currency Board): Free Capital Flows + Fixed Exchange Rate → sacrifice monetary independence → must import foreign interest rates entirely.
Option 3 — China (historically): Fixed Exchange Rate + Independent Monetary Policy → sacrifice free flows → capital controls required.

Why the Trilemma is Not Really a Choice

The trilemma is not a political preference — it is a mathematical constraint embedded in the balance of payments accounting and the interest rate parity condition. Under perfect capital mobility and a fixed rate, the money supply is endogenous by necessity — the CB cannot independently target both $e$ and $M$.

Interest Rate Parity

When investors compare returns across borders, they care about the total return: the interest rate plus any expected change in the exchange rate. In equilibrium with perfect substitutes:

Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP)

The investor takes the currency risk — no hedging. For no-arbitrage:

$i_{domestic} - i_{foreign} \approx \dfrac{E_t(e_{t+1}) - e_t}{e_t}$

Intuition: If domestic rates are higher, the market must expect a future domestic depreciation to offset that advantage. Otherwise everyone would borrow abroad and invest domestically — until the rates equalize.

Empirical status: UIP often fails in the short run — the "forward premium puzzle." Currency carry trades (borrow low-rate currency, invest in high-rate) persistently profit, suggesting risk premia or irrationality.

Covered Interest Parity (CIP)

The investor hedges the exchange risk with a forward contract. For no-arbitrage:

$i_{domestic} - i_{foreign} \approx \dfrac{F_t - S_t}{S_t}$

Where $F_t$ = forward rate, $S_t$ = spot rate. The interest differential must exactly equal the forward premium.

Empirical status: CIP held tightly until the Global Financial Crisis. Post-2008, large CIP deviations emerged due to balance-sheet constraints at major banks (dollar funding stress). It is now seen as only holding approximately.

Condition Hedging Empirical Status Breaks Down When?
UIP None — full currency risk Often violated (carry trades profitable) Risk premia, irrationality, momentum

Key Takeaways — Full Recap

1. Mundell-Fleming Structure. IS* depends on $NX(e)$; LM* is vertical in $(Y,e)$ space. With perfect capital mobility, $r = r^*$ always. The exchange rate $e$ — not the interest rate — adjusts to clear markets.

2. Floating Rates. Monetary policy is powerful (depreciates currency, boosts NX). Fiscal policy is totally crowded out through exchange-rate appreciation. Trade policy also ineffective — appreciation offsets any NX gain.

3. Fixed Rates. Fiscal policy is powerful (CB accommodates, no exchange-rate offset). Monetary policy is impossible (any expansion is sterilized by peg defense). Trade policy works too.

4. Impossible Trinity. Free capital flows + fixed rate + independent monetary policy cannot coexist. Every country sacrifices one. This is a hard constraint of open-economy macro.

5. Parity Conditions. UIP says expected depreciation offsets interest differentials (frequently violated). CIP says the forward premium equals the interest differential (held well until post-2008 funding stress).
