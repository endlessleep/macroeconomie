The IS-LM Model

From the Keynesian Cross to Aggregate Demand — and the Bridge to IS-MP

Welcome to one of the founding models of modern macroeconomics: the IS-LM model. Before diving into the equations, let's step back. The goal of short-run macroeconomics is to understand how a country's output level ($Y$) is determined at a given time, when prices have not yet had time to adjust (we say prices are "rigid" or "sticky", meaning $P$ is fixed).

To understand this, the IS-LM model bridges two worlds that constantly interact: the goods and services market (where we produce, consume, and invest) and the money market (where the Central Bank injects liquidity and agents decide to hold cash or bonds). It is the intersection of these two markets that will give us the equilibrium of the economy.

 What do "IS" and "LM" stand for?

IS Curve (Investment-Saving) It represents the equilibrium in the goods market. "IS" means that Investment is equal to Saving. It gives us all the combinations of interest rate ($i$) and output ($Y$) for which the supply of goods is exactly equal to the demand. It is downward-sloping.
LM Curve (Liquidity-Money) It represents the equilibrium in the money market. "LM" means that the demand for liquidity (Liquidity) is equal to the money supply (Money). It traces the pairs $(Y, i)$ for which the monetary market is in equilibrium. It is upward-sloping.

Let's now build this model step by step. We will first build the goods market (which will lead to the IS curve), then the money market (the LM curve), before bringing them together.

1. The Keynesian Cross: The Foundation of the Goods Market

The Keynesian Cross is our starting point. It is a very simple model based on a fundamental idea by John Maynard Keynes: in the short run, demand creates supply. If consumers, firms, and the government decide to spend more, firms will draw from their inventories, realize they are selling a lot, and will therefore increase production to meet this demand.

Planned Expenditure vs. Actual Output

Let's imagine the economy produces a certain quantity of goods, let's call it $Y$ (actual output, our GDP). On the other hand, we have what everyone plans to spend, which is called Planned Expenditure ($PE$).

If agents plan to spend more than what is produced ($PE > Y$), store shelves empty out. To avoid stockouts, factories restart their machines: $Y$ increases. Conversely, if $PE < Y$, unsold goods accumulate and factories slow down: $Y$ falls. The economy is in equilibrium only when what is produced is exactly equal to what is demanded: $Y = PE$.

What makes up this demand ($PE$)?

Total demand in an economy is divided into four main blocks. For now, we will assume that investment and government spending are fixed (exogenous) numbers.

$C$
Household Consumption: Households do not consume randomly. Their consumption depends on their disposable income (Income $Y$ minus taxes $T$). The more you earn, the more you consume. The equation is $C = \bar{C} + c(Y - T)$. The parameter $c$ is crucial: it is the Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC). If $c=0.8$, it means that for every additional dollar earned, the household will spend 80 cents and save 20.
$I$
Investment: Purchases of machines or buildings by firms. For now, we assume it is constant: $I = \bar{I}$.
$G$
Government Spending: State purchases (roads, hospitals, civil servant salaries). It is a political choice, thus constant: $G = \bar{G}$.
$NX$
Net Exports: What we sell abroad minus what we buy. To simplify this first model, we will group them into autonomous spending.

The Magic of the Keynesian Multiplier

By substituting these elements into our equilibrium condition $Y = PE$, we obtain a magical equation. Let's look at the formula for equilibrium output:

$\boxed{Y^* = \alpha_g \cdot A}, \quad \text{with} \quad \alpha_g = \dfrac{1}{1 - c(1-t)}$

The term $A$ represents all "autonomous" spending (which does not depend on income, like $\bar{G}$ or $\bar{I}$). But the fascinating term is $\alpha_g$. Since $c$ is between 0 and 1, $\alpha_g$ is strictly greater than 1. It is called the government spending multiplier.

The economic intuition of the multiplier

Why does a 1 million dollar increase in government spending ($G$) cause GDP ($Y$) to increase by more than a million dollars? Imagine the government decides to build a new road for 1 million dollars. This sum is paid to a construction company, which uses it to pay its workers.

These workers now have additional income. What will they do with it? They will consume a fraction $c$ of it (say 80%, or $800,000) to buy cars, clothes, food. The sellers of these goods therefore see their income increase by $800,000, and will in turn spend 80% of it ($640,000), and so on.

It is a chain reaction of spending: the initial government spending creates a first income, which generates consumption, which creates a second income, etc. The sum of this infinite series gives the multiplier $\alpha_g$.

There is also a transfer multiplier (if the government increases welfare benefits or cuts pure taxes). This multiplier is smaller ($c \cdot \alpha_g$), because welfare is not directly injected into GDP: it is first received by households, who will save a small part before spending the rest. The first "round" of spending is therefore diluted.

2. Deriving the IS Curve

The Keynesian Cross is highly instructive, but it has a major flaw: it assumes that business investment is a fixed amount $\bar{I}$, fallen from the sky. In reality, investment is an economic decision highly sensitive to financing conditions.

If you are a CEO and you want to build a new factory, you will likely have to borrow from the bank. The interest rate ($i$) represents the cost of this loan. If rates are very low (e.g., 1%), many projects become profitable. If rates soar to 10%, only extremely profitable projects will be able to cover this financing cost, and many investments will be canceled.

We will therefore modify our investment equation to reflect this reality: investment depends negatively on the interest rate.

$I = \bar{I} - b \cdot i, \quad b > 0$

The parameter $b$ measures the sensitivity of investment to the interest rate. The larger $b$ is, the more a small change in rates will cause firms to react massively.

From the formula to the curve

Let's integrate this new reality into our goods market equilibrium ($Y = PE$). Autonomous spending $A$ now contains this term $-bi$. The equation for our output becomes:

$Y = \alpha_g (A - bi)$

This is the equation of the IS curve. It describes the goods market completely: for any interest rate level $i$ imposed by the markets, it tells us what the output level $Y$ will be that balances supply and demand. By isolating it with respect to $i$, we can plot it on a graph with $i$ on the vertical axis and $Y$ on the horizontal axis.

Causal intuition: Why is IS downward-sloping?
1
Initial shock: Imagine the Central Bank decides to raise interest rates ($i \uparrow$). Credit becomes more expensive.
2
Firms' reaction: Faced with these costly loans, firms cancel their construction and machinery purchase projects. Investment falls ($I \downarrow$).
3
Impact on aggregate demand: Since investment is a component of demand, total Planned Expenditure falls.
4
The multiplier kicks in: Construction companies lay off workers, workers lose purchasing power, they consume less... GDP falls by an amplified amount ($Y \downarrow$).

Logical conclusion: An increase in the interest rate depresses economic activity. The relationship between $i$ and $Y$ is inverse. This is why the IS curve slopes downward to the right.

Shifts in the IS curve

It is fundamental to distinguish a movement along the curve from a shift of the curve. If the interest rate changes, we move along the IS curve. But if a factor other than the interest rate modifies aggregate demand (for example, the government decides to build new schools, $G \uparrow$), then the entire relationship changes: for a given interest rate, demand will be stronger. The IS curve shifts to the right.

3. The LM Curve: Equilibrium in the Money Market

We have solved half the problem. We know how the real economy reacts to interest rates. But where does this interest rate come from? It is formed in another market: the money market. This is where the LM curve comes in.

To understand LM, we must understand the concept of Liquidity Preference. At any given time, you have a certain amount of wealth. You can choose to hold it in two forms:
1. Money (cash or checking account): It is highly liquid, you can buy a sandwich immediately with it. Disadvantage: it yields nothing (interest rate = 0%).
2. Bonds (financial securities): They are not liquid (you cannot pay your baker with a stock). But in exchange for this inconvenience, they pay you a return: the famous interest rate $i$.

Money Demand (Liquidity Demand, $L^d$)

Your choice to hold more or less cash will depend on two opposing forces:

The transaction motive (The income effect, $Y$): If your income increases, or if the economy is booming and GDP ($Y$) is very high, you make more purchases. You need more cash in your wallet to pay for your groceries. So, when $Y$ increases, money demand increases (parameter $k > 0$).

The speculative motive / Opportunity cost (The interest rate effect, $i$): Holding cash has an invisible price: it's the money you lose by not investing that cash. This forgone return is the interest rate. If savings accounts suddenly jump to 10%, you will rush to empty your checking account to buy bonds. The opportunity cost of money has become too high. So, when the interest rate $i$ rises, people flee cash: money demand falls (parameter $h > 0$).

This gives us our money demand equation: $\left(\frac{M}{P}\right)^d = kY - hi$. Facing this demand, we have the money supply ($\frac{\bar{M}}{\bar{P}}$), which is rigidly fixed (exogenous) by the Central Bank (the ECB or the Fed).

Monetary equilibrium and the LM curve

Equilibrium in the money market is reached when liquidity demand equals the supply fixed by the Central Bank. By setting them equal and isolating the interest rate, we get:

$\boxed{i = \dfrac{k}{h}Y - \dfrac{1}{h}\dfrac{M}{P}}$

This is the equation of the LM curve. Unlike IS, look at the sign in front of $Y$: it is positive. The LM curve is upward-sloping. Why? Let's deconstruct the mechanism.

Causal intuition: Why is LM upward-sloping?
1
Initial shock on the economy: Suppose GDP rises sharply ($Y \uparrow$). Firms are operating at full capacity, hiring multiplies.
2
Need for liquidity: To make all these new purchases and pay these new wages, agents massively need cash. Money demand surges ($L^d \uparrow$).
3
The conflict with fixed supply: The Central Bank, however, has not printed any new bills. There is therefore a shortage of money in the market (excess demand).
4
Liquidating assets: To obtain this missing cash, households and banks will sell their bonds en masse. What happens when everyone sells the same security? Its price collapses.
5
The interest rate adjustment: In finance, a bond's price moves inversely to its yield. If bond prices plummet, interest rates shoot up ($i \uparrow$). These new, very high rates will ultimately discourage agents from holding money, bringing the market back to equilibrium.

Logical conclusion: An increase in GDP mechanically causes tensions in the money market that drive up interest rates. The relationship between $Y$ and $i$ is therefore positive: the LM curve slopes upward to the right.

4. General IS-LM Equilibrium and Aggregate Demand

We now have our two scissor blades. On one side (IS), the interest rate dictates investment and therefore the level of economic activity. On the other (LM), the level of economic activity generates a need for liquidity that dictates the interest rate.

The economy finds its rest only at the intersection of these two curves. At this precise point, and nowhere else, the goods market clears perfectly without inventory accumulation, AND the money market is serene, with no agents desperately trying to sell bonds. This magical point gives us the short-run equilibrium pair $(Y^*, i^*)$ of our economy.

From IS-LM to the Aggregate Demand (AD) Curve

Remember our initial assumption: all this was calculated assuming the price level ($P$) was fixed. But what would happen if prices were suddenly higher?

If inflation strikes and $P$ rises, the quantity of physical bills in circulation does not change, but the purchasing power of these bills collapses. The real money supply ($\frac{M}{P}$) decreases.
Faced with this "scarcity" of real money, the LM curve will shift upward (rates rise because money becomes scarce).
These high rates will depress investment (a movement along IS), which will cause equilibrium GDP ($Y^*$) to fall.

The result? A higher price level generates a lower equilibrium GDP. If we plot this relationship on a graph with $P$ on the vertical axis and $Y$ on the horizontal axis, we get a downward-sloping curve: this is the famous Aggregate Demand (AD) curve you saw in the AS-AD model.

5. The Bridge to Modernity: From IS-LM to the IS-MP Model

The IS-LM model, as just presented to you, dominated macroeconomics for decades. It is a brilliant model, but it contains a flaw in realism regarding the LM curve.

In our LM story, the Central Bank rigidly controls the quantity of bills ($M$), and lets the interest rate ($i$) fluctuate wildly according to market demand shocks. However, in today's real world, no major Central Bank operates this way.

The classical view (IS-LM)

The Fed fixes $M$. If the government stimulates the economy, money demand explodes. The Fed does nothing, so interest rates skyrocket uncontrollably, destroying private investment.

The modern view (IS-MP)

The ECB or the Fed do not target the money supply. They announce a target interest rate (e.g., "The policy rate is 3%"). They will then print as much money as necessary every day to keep this rate exactly at 3%. The monetary curve becomes a horizontal line.

The MP (Monetary Policy) Curve

In contemporary macroeconomic models (like those studied at the beginning of the semester), the upward-sloping LM curve is therefore replaced by a horizontal MP curve.

$i = \bar{i} \quad \text{or via the Taylor rule: } \quad i_t = r^n_t + \pi_t + \phi_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*_t) + \phi_y(Y_t - \bar{Y}_t)$

This shift from IS-LM to IS-MP considerably changes how we analyze fiscal policies (as we will see in the applications), because a government stimulus no longer automatically causes an interest rate spike, since the Central Bank accommodates money demand to maintain its target rate.

However, the "real" block of the economy, the IS curve, remains exactly the same. Understanding IS-LM is therefore an indispensable step to mastering the plumbing of modern macroeconomics.
