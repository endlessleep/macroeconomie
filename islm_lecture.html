The IS-LM Model

From the Keynesian Cross to Aggregate Demand — and the Bridge to IS-MP

 What is IS-LM?

IS-LM Model A short-run framework that simultaneously determines equilibrium output $Y$ and interest rate $i$ by combining goods-market equilibrium (IS) and money-market equilibrium (LM), taking the price level $P$ as fixed.
IS Curve Investment–Saving: the set of $(Y, i)$ pairs such that the goods market clears, i.e., $Y = C + I + G + NX$. Slopes downward.
LM Curve Liquidity–Money: the set of $(Y, i)$ pairs such that the money market clears, i.e., $M/P = L(i,Y)$. Slopes upward.
Short Run Prices are sticky — $P$ is fixed. IS-LM determines $(Y^*, i^*)$ for a given $P$. In the long run, $P$ adjusts and output returns to its natural level.

The Keynesian Cross

The Keynesian Cross is the building block of the IS curve. It shows how, for a given interest rate, equilibrium output is determined by planned expenditure.

Planned vs. Actual Expenditure

Actual Output ($Y$)

Total goods and services produced — measured by GDP. In general, $Y \neq PE$ unless the economy is in equilibrium.

Planned Expenditure ($PE$)

What households, firms, the government, and foreigners intend to spend:

$PE = C + I + G + NX$
Goods-Market Equilibrium: $Y = PE$

When planned expenditure exceeds output, inventories run down and firms increase production. When output exceeds planned spending, inventories pile up and firms cut production. The economy self-corrects to $Y = PE$.

The Behavioural Equations

$C$
Consumption: $C = \bar{C} + c(Y - T)$, where $c \in (0,1)$ is the Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC). A $1 increase in disposable income raises consumption by $c.
$I$
Investment: Initially exogenous — $I = \bar{I}$. Later we make it interest-sensitive to derive the IS curve.
$G$
Government Purchases: Exogenous policy variable $G = \bar{G}$. Does not include transfers.
$T$
Taxes net of Transfers: $T = tY - TR$, where $t$ is the marginal income tax rate and $TR$ are government transfers. Higher income → higher tax revenue (automatic stabilizer).

Deriving Equilibrium Output

Substituting $T = tY - TR$ into the consumption function:

$PE = \underbrace{\bar{C} + c \cdot TR + \bar{I} + \bar{G} + \overline{NX}}_{A \; (\text{autonomous expenditure})} + c(1-t) \cdot Y$

Setting $Y = PE$ and solving:

$\boxed{Y^* = \alpha_g \cdot A}, \quad \alpha_g = \dfrac{1}{1 - c(1-t)}$

$\alpha_g$ is the government-expenditure multiplier. Since $c(1-t) < 1$, we have $\alpha_g > 1$: a $1 increase in autonomous spending raises equilibrium output by more than $1.

The Two Multipliers

Government-Purchases Multiplier

$\dfrac{dY}{dG} = \alpha_g = \dfrac{1}{1-c(1-t)}$

Each €1 of $G$ enters demand directly (1-for-1), then triggers rounds of induced consumption. The total effect is $\alpha_g > 1$.

Transfer / Tax-Cut Multiplier

$\dfrac{dY}{dTR} = \dfrac{c}{1-c(1-t)} = c \cdot \alpha_g$

Transfers first raise disposable income; households spend only fraction $c$. So the multiplier is smaller than $\alpha_g$ by a factor of $c$.

Keynesian Cross multiplier vs. IS-LM multiplier

In the Keynesian Cross, the interest rate is fixed and the full multiplier $\alpha_g$ applies. In IS-LM, fiscal expansion raises money demand which pushes up $i$, crowding out private investment. The IS-LM fiscal multiplier is therefore smaller than $\alpha_g$. See Applications page.

Deriving the IS Curve

We now relax the assumption of exogenous investment. In reality, higher interest rates make borrowing more expensive, reducing firms' incentive to invest:

$I = \bar{I} - b \cdot i, \quad b > 0$

The parameter $b$ is the interest sensitivity of investment. A larger $b$ makes the IS curve flatter.

The Derivation

Autonomous expenditure now becomes $A - bi$. Plugging into the goods-market equilibrium $Y = PE$:

$Y = \alpha_g (A - bi)$

Solving for $i$ (to read the IS curve in $(Y,i)$ space):

$\boxed{i = \dfrac{A}{b} - \dfrac{1}{\alpha_g b} \cdot Y}$
Why is the IS Curve Downward-Sloping?
When the interest rate rises ($i \uparrow$)…
1
Investment becomes more expensive → $I \downarrow$ (firms borrow less)
2
Planned expenditure $PE$ falls
3
Equilibrium output $Y^*$ falls (via the multiplier)

Thus: $i \uparrow \Rightarrow Y \downarrow$ — the IS curve is downward-sloping.

Slope and Shifts of the IS Curve

Factor Effect on Slope / Position Why?
$b$ large (sensitive investment) IS flatter A given rise in $i$ kills more investment → large drop in $Y$
$\alpha_g$ large (large multiplier) IS flatter Each drop in $I$ triggers larger multiplied drop in $Y$
$G \uparrow$ or $TR \uparrow$ or $T \downarrow$ IS shifts right Higher autonomous spending $A \uparrow$ → more output at every $i$
$G \downarrow$ or $TR \downarrow$ or $T \uparrow$ IS shifts left Lower $A$ → less output at every $i$

The LM Curve: Money Market Equilibrium

 Key Definitions — Money Market

Money Supply $\left(\frac{M}{P}\right)^s = \frac{\bar{M}}{\bar{P}}$ — fixed by the central bank in the short run. Represented by a vertical line in $(M/P, i)$ space.
Money Demand $\left(\frac{M}{P}\right)^d = kY - hi$ — increases with income (transactions motive, $k>0$) and falls with the interest rate (opportunity cost of holding money, $h>0$).
Opportunity Cost Holding money earns zero return. The interest rate $i$ is what you give up by preferring liquidity over bonds — this is why money demand falls when $i$ rises.

Deriving the LM Curve

Setting supply = demand, $\frac{M}{P} = kY - hi$, and solving for $i$:

$\boxed{i = \dfrac{k}{h}Y - \dfrac{1}{h}\dfrac{M}{P}}$
Why is the LM Curve Upward-Sloping?
When income rises ($Y \uparrow$)…
1
More transactions needed → money demand rises ($L^d \uparrow$)
2
With fixed money supply, there is excess demand for money
3
People sell bonds to get money → bond prices fall → $i \uparrow$
4
Higher $i$ discourages money holding → money market re-equilibrates

Thus: $Y \uparrow \Rightarrow i \uparrow$ — the LM curve is upward-sloping.

Slope and Shifts of the LM Curve

Factor Effect on Slope / Position Why?
$k$ large (money demand sensitive to $Y$) LM steeper A given rise in $Y$ creates large excess demand → needs a big $i$ rise
$h$ small (money demand insensitive to $i$) LM steeper Interest rate must rise a lot to reduce money demand by a given amount
$M \uparrow$ (monetary expansion) LM shifts right (down) More money available → lower $i$ needed to clear market at each $Y$
$M \downarrow$ (monetary contraction) LM shifts left (up) Less money → higher $i$ needed to clear market at each $Y$

IS-LM Equilibrium

Equilibrium occurs at the intersection of IS and LM, where both markets clear simultaneously. This jointly determines the short-run output level $Y^*$ and interest rate $i^*$ for a given price level $P$.

Goods Market (IS)

$Y = C(Y-T) + I(i) + G + NX$

Equilibrium in the market for goods and services.

Money Market (LM)

$\dfrac{M}{P} = L(i, Y) = kY - hi$

Equilibrium in the market for real money balances.

What moves the equilibrium?

  • Fiscal policy ($\Delta G$, $\Delta T$, $\Delta TR$): shifts the IS curve, changes both $Y^*$ and $i^*$.
  • Monetary policy ($\Delta M$): shifts the LM curve, changes both $Y^*$ and $i^*$.
  • Price level change ($\Delta P$): changes real money balances $M/P$ → shifts LM → traces out the Aggregate Demand curve.

From IS-LM to Aggregate Demand (AD)

For each price level $P$, IS and LM jointly determine equilibrium output. As $P$ rises:

Price Level rises ($P \uparrow$)…
1
Real money supply falls: $M/P \downarrow$
2
LM shifts left (up) → $i^*$ rises
3
Higher $i$ reduces investment → $Y^*$ falls

Plotting equilibrium $Y^*$ against $P$ traces the downward-sloping Aggregate Demand (AD) curve.

Short Run vs. Long Run

IS-LM describes the short run with fixed prices. In the long run, prices adjust. Output returns to potential $\bar{Y}$ — fiscal and monetary stimulus do not permanently raise output, only the price level.

A Caveat: One Rate in the Model, Many in Practice

IS-LM uses a single representative interest rate $i$. In reality, there is a whole spectrum of rates — from the overnight policy rate to 30-year mortgages. Other market rates depend on:

  • Expected future policy rates (expectations hypothesis of the term structure);
  • Term premia (compensation for holding long-dated bonds);
  • Credit and liquidity spreads (risk of default, difficulty selling).
Why This Matters for Monetary Policy

In the traditional LM story, the CB controls $M$, and $i$ is determined endogenously. But modern central banks target the policy rate directly, letting liquidity adjust endogenously. A change in money demand does not automatically change all market rates.

This is why modern macroeconomics replaces the LM curve with an MP (Monetary Policy) curve.

The Bridge to IS-MP

Feature IS-LM IS-MP
CB instrument Money supply $\bar{M}$ Policy interest rate $\bar{i}$
Interest rate Determined endogenously by money market Set exogenously by CB (MP curve is horizontal)
Monetary expansion Shift LM right → $i$ falls MP shifts down → $i$ falls directly
Fiscal expansion IS shifts right → $Y$ and $i$ both rise (crowding out) IS shifts right → $Y$ rises; $i$ unchanged → less crowding out
Realism Historical benchmark (pre-inflation targeting) Modern: matches how central banks actually operate

The MP Curve

In the simplest version, the CB just announces a rate:

$i = \bar{i}$

More realistically, the CB follows a Taylor Rule, adjusting $i$ in response to inflation and the output gap:

$i_t = r^n_t + \pi_t + \phi_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \phi_y(Y_t - \bar{Y}_t)$

Connecting to the Five-Equation Model

Combining IS + MP (Taylor Rule) + a Phillips Curve (inflation dynamics) gives the modern three-equation model (IS-PC-MP) — or the DAD-DAS dynamic framework studied in the first part of this course. The IS curve is the common thread running through all these frameworks.

What Have We Learned? — Full Recap

1. Keynesian Cross — For a fixed interest rate, equilibrium output is determined by $Y^* = \alpha_g \cdot A$. Multiplier $\alpha_g > 1$. Transfer multiplier is smaller by factor $c$.

2. IS Curve — Making investment interest-sensitive gives a downward-sloping IS. Fiscal expansions shift it right. Slope depends on $b$ (investment sensitivity) and $\alpha_g$.

3. LM Curve — Upward-sloping: higher income raises money demand, pushing up $i$. Monetary expansion shifts it right (lower $i$). Slope depends on $k/h$.

4. Equilibrium — IS ∩ LM determines $(Y^*, i^*)$. Tracing this for different $P$ levels gives the downward-sloping AD curve.

5. IS-MP Bridge — Modern CBs target $i$ directly → replace LM with a horizontal MP curve. Less crowding out from fiscal policy. Same IS logic, different monetary block.
