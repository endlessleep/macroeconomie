The IS-LM Model

General Equilibrium in the Short Run and the Transition to IS-MP

The IS-LM model formalizes short-run macroeconomic fluctuations under the assumption of price stickiness. It identifies the simultaneous equilibrium in two interdependent markets: the goods market (Investment-Saving) and the money market (Liquidity-Money).

 Market Equilibrium Conditions

IS Curve (Goods Market) Represents combinations of the interest rate ($i$) and aggregate output ($Y$) where planned expenditure equals actual production. It is downward-sloping due to the negative interest-elasticity of investment.
LM Curve (Money Market) Represents combinations of $(Y, i)$ where the demand for real money balances equals the exogenously fixed money supply. It is upward-sloping due to the transaction demand for money.

1. The Goods Market and the Keynesian Cross

The foundation of the IS curve is the Keynesian Cross, which determines equilibrium output holding the interest rate and price level constant. The core premise is that actual output ($Y$) adjusts to meet Planned Expenditure ($PE$).

Equilibrium Condition: $Y = PE$

Planned expenditure is the sum of consumption, investment, and government spending: $PE = C + I + G$.
Inventory adjustments drive the economy toward equilibrium. If $PE > Y$, firms experience unintended inventory depletion and increase production. If $PE < Y$, unintended inventory accumulation leads to production cuts. Equilibrium occurs strictly where $Y = PE$.

The components of $PE$ are formalized as follows, treating $I$ and $G$ as exogenous constants for the baseline model:

$C$
Consumption Function: $C = \bar{C} + c(Y - T)$. Consumption depends on autonomous spending ($\bar{C}$) and disposable income ($Y - T$). The parameter $c \in (0,1)$ is the Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC), denoting the fraction of an additional unit of disposable income devoted to consumption.
$I$ & $G$
Exogenous Variables: Investment ($I = \bar{I}$) and Government Spending ($G = \bar{G}$) are assumed constant.

The Expenditure Multiplier

Substituting the consumption function into the equilibrium condition yields the equilibrium output:

$\boxed{Y^* = \alpha_g \cdot A}, \quad \text{where} \quad \alpha_g = \dfrac{1}{1 - c(1-t)}$

Here, $A$ aggregates all autonomous spending components. The term $\alpha_g$ is the government spending multiplier. Because $c \in (0,1)$, $\alpha_g > 1$.

Mathematical Derivation of the Multiplier

An initial exogenous increase in government spending ($\Delta G$) immediately increases $Y$ by $\Delta G$. This income increase induces secondary consumption spending of $c \Delta G$, which further increases income, inducing tertiary spending of $c^2 \Delta G$, ad infinitum.

The total effect on output is the sum of an infinite geometric series: $\Delta Y = \Delta G \cdot (1 + c + c^2 + \dots) = \Delta G \cdot \frac{1}{1 - c}$. Therefore, $\frac{\Delta Y}{\Delta G} = \frac{1}{1 - c}$.

2. Deriving the IS Curve

The assumption of exogenous investment is relaxed to derive the IS curve. Investment is formalized as a decreasing function of the interest rate, representing the cost of borrowing and the opportunity cost of internal funds.

$I = \bar{I} - b \cdot i, \quad b > 0$

The parameter $b$ measures the interest elasticity of investment. Substituting this investment function into the goods market equilibrium condition yields the IS equation:

$Y = \alpha_g (A - bi)$

The IS curve maps the locus of $(Y, i)$ combinations ensuring goods market equilibrium. Its slope is negative: a higher interest rate reduces investment, which, via the multiplier effect, contracts equilibrium output.

Transmission Mechanism: Interest Rate to Output
1
An exogenous increase in the interest rate ($i \uparrow$) raises the cost of capital.
2
Firms reduce capital expenditure, leading to a decline in aggregate investment ($I \downarrow$).
3
The reduction in autonomous spending induces a multiplied contraction in aggregate income ($Y \downarrow$).

Changes in the interest rate result in movements along the IS curve. Conversely, changes in autonomous spending components (e.g., fiscal policy shocks such as $\Delta G$ or $\Delta T$) cause parallel shifts of the IS curve.

3. The Money Market and the LM Curve

The LM curve models equilibrium in the money market based on Keynes's theory of Liquidity Preference, which posits that the interest rate adjusts to balance the supply and demand for real money balances.

The Demand for Real Balances ($L^d$)

Money demand is determined by two primary motives:

1. The Transaction Motive ($kY$): Real balances are held to facilitate transactions. As aggregate income ($Y$) rises, the volume of transactions increases, proportionally increasing money demand ($k > 0$).

2. The Speculative Motive ($-hi$): The nominal interest rate represents the opportunity cost of holding non-interest-bearing money instead of bonds. As $i$ rises, agents substitute money for bonds, reducing money demand ($h > 0$).

The demand for real balances is thus formulated as $\left(\frac{M}{P}\right)^d = kY - hi$. Equilibrium requires this demand to equal the exogenous real money supply $\frac{\bar{M}}{\bar{P}}$, controlled by the central bank.

$\boxed{i = \dfrac{k}{h}Y - \dfrac{1}{h}\dfrac{M}{P}}$

The LM equation demonstrates a positive relationship between $Y$ and $i$.

Transmission Mechanism: Output to Interest Rate
1
An expansion in aggregate output ($Y \uparrow$) increases the transaction demand for money.
2
Assuming a fixed money supply, an excess demand for real balances emerges.
3
Agents attempt to liquidate bonds to acquire cash, driving bond prices down.
4
Given the inverse relationship between bond prices and yields, the interest rate rises ($i \uparrow$) until the speculative demand falls enough to restore equilibrium.

4. General Equilibrium and Aggregate Demand

General short-run equilibrium is defined uniquely at the intersection of the IS and LM curves. The resulting pair $(Y^*, i^*)$ ensures simultaneous market clearing in both the goods and money markets.

Derivation of the Aggregate Demand (AD) Curve

The IS-LM model assumes a fixed price level $P$. Relaxing this assumption reveals the Aggregate Demand (AD) curve.

An exogenous increase in the price level ($P \uparrow$) reduces the real money supply ($\frac{M}{P} \downarrow$). This leftward shift of the LM curve forces the equilibrium interest rate higher. The elevated interest rate subsequently depresses investment, causing equilibrium output to contract along the IS curve.

Consequently, the locus of equilibrium output levels associated with varying price levels forms a downward-sloping relationship in $(Y, P)$ space, defining the AD curve.

5. The Transition to the IS-MP Model

While the IS-LM framework assumes an exogenous money supply $M$, modern monetary policy implementation relies on targeting a short-term nominal interest rate rather than monetary aggregates. The IS-MP (Monetary Policy) model updates the LM component to reflect this institutional reality.

IS-LM Framework

The central bank fixes $M$. Demand shocks (shifts in IS) cause endogenous fluctuations in the interest rate as money demand varies along a fixed LM curve, leading to crowding out effects.

IS-MP Framework

The central bank sets a target interest rate $\bar{i}$ and endogenously accommodates any shifts in money demand via open market operations. The monetary equilibrium is represented by a horizontal MP curve.

The MP curve is formalized as an interest rate rule, often following a Taylor Rule specification:

$i_t = r^n_t + \pi_t + \phi_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*_t) + \phi_y(Y_t - \bar{Y}_t)$

The IS equation remains structurally identical in both models. The transition to IS-MP modifies solely the comparative statics of policy shocks, eliminating automatic crowding out associated with an upward-sloping LM curve unless the central bank actively adjusts its policy rate in response to output gaps.
