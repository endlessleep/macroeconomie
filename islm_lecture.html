The IS-LM Model and the Bridge to IS-MP

From the Keynesian Cross to Aggregate Demand — Janíčko, Macroeconomics II

The Keynesian Cross

The Keynesian cross is the building block for the IS curve. It establishes how planned spending by all agents determines the economy's short-run output level.

Planned vs. Actual Expenditure

Actual Output ($Y$)

The total amount of goods and services produced in the economy. Measured by GDP.

Planned Expenditure ($PE$)

The amount households, firms, government, and foreigners plan to spend:

$PE = C + I + G + NX$
Goods-Market Equilibrium
$Y = PE$

If firms sell less than they produce, inventories rise unexpectedly and firms reduce output. If firms sell more than they produce, inventories fall unexpectedly and firms increase output.

Basic Assumptions

We specify the components of planned expenditure with the following linear structure:

$C$
Consumption: $C = \bar{C} + c(Y - T)$, where $0 < c < 1$ is the marginal propensity to consume (MPC).
$I$
Investment: Initially taken as exogenous: $I = \bar{I}$.
$G$
Government Purchases: Exogenous: $G = \bar{G}$.
$NX$
Net Exports: Exogenous: $NX = \overline{NX}$.
$T$
Taxes and Transfers: $T = tY - TR$, where $t$ is the income tax rate and $TR$ are transfers.

Substituting these into the $PE$ equation gives:

$PE = \bar{C} + c\bigl[Y(1 - t) + TR\bigr] + \bar{I} + \bar{G} + \overline{NX}$

Autonomous Expenditure & Equilibrium Output

We define autonomous expenditure — the part of spending that does not depend on current income — as:

$A = \bar{C} + c \cdot TR + \bar{I} + \bar{G} + \overline{NX}$

Planned expenditure can then be written compactly as:

$PE = A + c(1 - t)Y$

Setting $Y = PE$ and solving for equilibrium output:

$Y^* = \dfrac{1}{1 - c(1 - t)} \cdot A = \alpha_g \cdot A$

The term $\alpha_g = \dfrac{1}{1 - c(1 - t)}$ is the government-expenditure multiplier.

Adjustment Mechanism

This economy self-corrects through unplanned inventory changes:

  • If $Y_1 > Y^*$: planned expenditure is below output. Firms accumulate unintended inventories and cut production until $Y$ falls back to $Y^*$.
  • If $Y_2 < Y^*$: planned expenditure exceeds output. Firms run down inventories and raise production until $Y$ rises to $Y^*$.

Multipliers

Government-Purchases Multiplier

$\dfrac{dY}{dG} = \dfrac{1}{1 - c(1 - t)} = \alpha_g$

Higher government purchases raise demand directly: each additional \$1 of $G$ enters demand one-for-one and then triggers rounds of induced consumption.

Transfer Multiplier

$\dfrac{dY}{dTR} = \dfrac{c}{1 - c(1 - t)}$

Higher transfers raise disposable income and therefore consumption. The multiplier is smaller than $\alpha_g$ because only a fraction $c$ of the transfer is spent immediately.

From the Keynesian Cross to the IS Curve

We now relax the assumption that investment is exogenous. Instead, we allow investment to depend negatively on the interest rate:

$I = \bar{I} - bi, \quad b > 0$

The parameter $b$ captures how sensitive investment is to changes in borrowing costs. Higher interest rates make projects less profitable, so firms invest less.

Aggregate Demand with Interest-Rate-Sensitive Investment

Aggregate demand now becomes:

$AD = A + c(1-t)Y - bi$

For a given interest rate, this is just the Keynesian cross with a lower intercept when $i$ is higher.

Deriving the IS Curve

Setting $Y = AD$ in goods-market equilibrium and solving:

$Y = \alpha_g(A - bi), \quad \alpha_g = \dfrac{1}{1 - c(1-t)}$

Equivalently, solving for $i$ to see the IS curve in $(Y, i)$ space:

$i = \dfrac{A}{b} - \dfrac{Y}{\alpha_g \cdot b}$
Why the IS Curve Slopes Downward

Higher interest rates reduce investment, which lowers planned expenditure, which lowers equilibrium output. Therefore, the IS curve is downward sloping in the $(Y, i)$ plane: each point on it represents a goods-market equilibrium for a different level of the interest rate.

Fiscal Policy and the IS Curve

Shifts of the IS Curve

The IS curve is drawn for a given fiscal policy. Changes in $G$, $T$, or $TR$ alter autonomous demand $A$ and therefore shift the curve:

  • Expansionary fiscal policy (higher $G$, higher $TR$, or lower taxes): raises $A$ → shifts IS to the right.
  • Contractionary fiscal policy (lower $G$, lower $TR$, or higher taxes): lowers $A$ → shifts IS to the left.

Liquidity Preference and the LM Curve

We now turn to the market for real money balances. The LM curve shows combinations of $i$ and $Y$ such that money demand equals money supply.

Supply and Demand for Money

Money Supply (Fixed)

In the traditional IS-LM model, the central bank fixes the supply of real money balances in the short run:

$\left(\dfrac{M}{P}\right)^s = \dfrac{\bar{M}}{\bar{P}}$

$M$ is an exogenous policy variable; $P$ is fixed in the short run. The supply curve is therefore vertical.

Money Demand (Downward)

The interest rate is the opportunity cost of holding money instead of interest-bearing assets. A simple linear specification:

$\left(\dfrac{M}{P}\right)^d = L(i, Y) = kY - hi$

$k > 0$ (higher income → more transactions → more money demand) and $h > 0$ (higher rate → higher cost of holding money → less demand).

Deriving the LM Curve

Money-market equilibrium requires supply = demand:

$\dfrac{M}{P} = kY - hi$

Solving for the interest rate:

$i = \dfrac{k}{h}Y - \dfrac{1}{h}\dfrac{M}{P}$
Why the LM Curve Slopes Upward

If income $Y$ rises, households and firms want to hold more money for transactions. But real money balances are fixed. The only way to restore equilibrium is for the interest rate to rise, discouraging money demand until it re-matches the fixed supply.

Slope Determinants

The slope of the LM curve is $k/h$:

  • The greater the response of money demand to income ($k$ large), the steeper the LM curve.
  • The smaller the response of money demand to the interest rate ($h$ small), the steeper the LM curve.

Monetary Policy and the LM Curve

Shifts of the LM Curve

  • Increase in $M$ (monetary expansion): higher real money balances → LM shifts downward/rightward.
  • Decrease in $M$ (monetary contraction): lower real money balances → LM shifts upward/leftward.

IS-LM Equilibrium

Simultaneous equilibrium in the goods market and the money market occurs at the intersection of the IS and LM curves. This determines the short-run equilibrium levels of output ($Y$) and the interest rate ($i$) for a given price level.

IS
$Y = C(Y - T) + I(i) + G + NX$ — equilibrium in the market for goods and services.
LM
$\dfrac{M}{P} = L(i, Y)$ — equilibrium in the market for real money balances.

The equilibrium pair $(Y^*, i^*)$ solves both equations simultaneously, taking $P$ as fixed in the short run.

Short Run vs. Long Run

The IS-LM equilibrium determines short-run output. In the long run, prices adjust and output returns to its natural level, so the short-run IS-LM equilibrium does not by itself determine long-run output.

  • Changes in fiscal policy ($G$, $T$, $TR$) shift the IS curve.
  • Changes in real money balances ($M/P$) shift the LM curve.

From IS-LM to Aggregate Demand

For a given price level $P$, IS and LM determine equilibrium output. If the price level changes, $M/P$ changes, which shifts the LM curve. Tracing the resulting equilibrium levels of output for different price levels gives the aggregate demand curve.

A Caveat: One Rate in the Model, Many in Practice

In IS-LM we use one representative interest rate $i$. In practice, the central bank controls a very short-term policy rate quite closely. Other market rates — mortgage rates, bank lending rates, corporate bond yields, long-term government yields — also depend on:

  • Expected future policy rates;
  • Term premia;
  • Credit and liquidity spreads.
Why This Matters

In the traditional LM story, higher money demand raises the equilibrium interest rate if real money balances are fixed. In modern practice, however, the central bank often keeps the short-term policy rate near its target by supplying the liquidity the system needs.

A rise in money demand does not mechanically imply that all market interest rates must rise. Central banks nowadays give up on controlling money supply as money is largely endogenous, and concentrate more on the key nominal interest rates.

These are the key reasons modern macroeconomics often replaces the LM curve with an MP curve.

The Bridge to IS-MP

The IS curve remains central in modern macroeconomics: higher real interest rates reduce investment and aggregate demand. What changes is the monetary-policy block.

IS-LM Framework

The central bank controls the money supply. The interest rate is determined endogenously through money-market equilibrium (the LM curve).

IS-MP Framework

The central bank sets the policy interest rate directly. Liquidity adjusts endogenously to money demand. There is no "money market equilibrium" to solve.

Why Replace LM with MP?

The LM curve is based on $\frac{M}{P} = L(i, Y)$. Analytically elegant, but it treats monetary policy as if the central bank primarily fixed the money supply. In practice, modern central banks announce a policy interest rate, not a money target. Once that rate is chosen, liquidity adjusts endogenously.

The MP Curve

In the simplest short-run version:

$i = \bar{i}$

More realistically, the central bank follows a Taylor Rule:

$i_t = r^n_t + \pi_t + \phi_\pi(\pi_t - \pi^*) + \phi_y(Y_t - \bar{Y}_t)$

Connecting it All Together

Combining IS and MP gives a more modern short-run demand framework. Adding inflation dynamics then leads to the dynamic AD-AS model, or the three-equation model (IS-PC-MP) studied in the first part of this course.

Thus, modern macroeconomics largely keeps IS but replaces LM with MP.

The MP Curve: Static Interpretation

In the static setup, inflation is taken as given. Key implications:

  • The central bank chooses the policy rate, so the MP curve is horizontal.
  • Liquidity adjusts endogenously to keep the market rate near the target.
  • The IS curve is downward sloping: lower rates increase demand and output.
  • Monetary easing shifts MP downward → raises equilibrium output.
  • Fiscal expansion shifts IS right; with a fixed policy rate, crowding out is smaller than in the IS-LM model.
What Have We Learned?

The traditional IS-LM model has two main blocks:

  1. IS: equilibrium in the market for goods and services.
  2. LM: equilibrium in the market for real money balances.

Their intersection determines short-run equilibrium output and the interest rate for a given price level. As the price level changes, the LM curve shifts, tracing out the aggregate demand curve.

IS-LM remains useful as a simple benchmark and as a historical interpretation of Keynesian short-run macroeconomics.

Modern macroeconomics, however, usually keeps the IS logic and replaces the LM curve with an MP curve that better reflects interest-rate targeting by central banks.
