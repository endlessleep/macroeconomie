Seminar 3: Shocks' Analysis

DMM — Supply Shocks, Dynamics & Central Bank Trade-Off

Exercise 1

Supply Shock Examples

For each of the following, determine whether the shock is a positive supply shock, a negative supply shock, or a demand shock.

  1. A sharp increase in global oil prices due to an embargo of the Strait of Hormuz.
  2. A major improvement in manufacturing technology that raises productivity.
  3. Increase in output due to a large fiscal stimulus.
  4. A substantial increase in the legally mandated minimum wage.
  5. A severe natural disaster that destroys factories and infrastructure.
  6. A global supply-chain breakdown that raises input delays and costs.
  7. A decline in output due to a drop in consumer and business confidence.
Show Solution
1 — Oil Price Embargo

Higher oil prices increase firms' production costs → prices rise, output falls.

→ Negative supply shock ($\nu_t > 0$: DAS shifts upward)

2 — Manufacturing Technology Improvement

Better technology increases productivity. This translates to an increase in potential output ($\bar{y} \uparrow$).

Because $\bar{y}$ appears in both DAD and DAS equations, both curves shift simultaneously:

  • DAD shifts right ("hausse"): higher output demanded at every inflation level.
  • DAS shifts downward/right ("hausse"): firms can produce more at lower inflation levels.
3 — Fiscal Stimulus

Government spending or tax cuts increase aggregate demand → output rises.

→ Positive demand shock ($\epsilon_t > 0$: DAD shifts right)

4 — Minimum Wage Increase

This policy creates a dual-channel shock:

  • Firms' side: Higher wages increase labor costs $\implies$ Negative supply shock ($\nu_t > 0$, DAS shifts upward).
  • Households' side: Higher minimum wages boost aggregate consumption $\implies$ Positive demand shock ($\epsilon_t > 0$, DAD shifts right/upward).

Result: Both DAD and DAS shift upward overall.

5 — Natural Disaster

Destruction of factories and infrastructure causes a permanent decrease in potential output ($\bar{y} \downarrow$).

Because $\bar{y}$ decreases, the economy structurally shrinks (opposite of Q2):

  • DAD shifts left ("baisse"): less output demanded at every inflation level.
  • DAS shifts upward/left ("baisse"): lower supply capacity pushing inflation up for any given output.
6 — Supply-Chain Breakdown

Delays and higher input costs reduce firms' ability to produce efficiently → costs rise.

→ Negative supply shock ($\nu_t > 0$: DAS shifts upward)

7 — Drop in Confidence

Lower consumer and business confidence reduces consumption and investment → aggregate demand decreases.

→ Negative demand shock ($\epsilon_t < 0$: DAD shifts left)

Quick Rule

Supply shocks affect production costs (oil, wages, technology) → shift DAS
Demand shocks affect spending (confidence, fiscal policy) → shift DAD

Exercise 2

One-Period Supply Shock

Starting Point

Long-run equilibrium: $y = 100$, $\pi = 2$, $r = 2$, $i = 4$
Shock: $\nu_0 = -1$ (positive supply shock that lowers inflation)

  1. What happens to inflation as an immediate result of the shock?
  2. What happens to nominal interest rates as an immediate result of the change in inflation?
  3. What happens to expected inflation as an immediate result of the shock?
  4. What happens to the real interest rate? Explain relating to your previous two answers.
  5. Why does the central bank decrease interest rates? What is it trying to achieve?
  6. What happens to output as an immediate result of the shock?
  7. Capture the previous sequence in the IS–MPR diagram.
  8. What does the resulting change in output imply for inflation? (Simultaneous equations + DAD–DAS)
  9. Capture the immediate effect in the DAD–DAS diagram.
  10. Explain how inflation changes by less than the size of the shock.
Show Solution
Q1 — Immediate Effect on Inflation

The shock enters the Phillips curve directly. However, the result is not a simple 1 pp drop. Because the model is simultaneous:

$\pi_0 = \pi_{-1} + \phi(y_0 - \bar{y}) + \nu_0$

Initially, $\nu_0 = -1$ pushes inflation down. But as we will see, the CB reacts immediately by lowering rates, which pushes output $y_0$ above potential. This positive output gap creates upward pressure that partially counteracts the shock.

Result: Inflation falls, but by less than 1 percentage point ($|\Delta\pi| < 1$).

Q2 — Nominal Interest Rates

The Taylor rule says the CB reacts to inflation below target:

$\pi_0 < \pi^* \implies i_0 \downarrow$

The central bank reduces the nominal interest rate.

Q3 — Expected Inflation

With adaptive expectations: $E_0\pi_1 = \pi_0 = 1$.

Expected inflation falls because current inflation has fallen.

Q4 — Real Interest Rate

From Fisher: $r_0 = i_0 - E_0\pi_1$

Both $i_0$ and $E_0\pi_1$ decrease. However, because the CB reduces nominal rates strongly (Taylor principle), the real interest rate falls:

$r_0 < \rho=2 \implies \text{borrowing becomes cheaper}$
Q5 — Why the CB Lowers Rates

Inflation has fallen below target. The CB lowers rates to stimulate aggregate demand and push inflation back toward the target.

Lower rates → more consumption & investment → higher output → positive output gap → inflation increases through the Phillips curve.

Q6 — Immediate Effect on Output

Because the real interest rate falls:

$r_0 \downarrow \implies c \uparrow, \; I \uparrow \implies y_0 > \bar{y} = 100$

Output rises above potential.

Q7 — IS–MPR Diagram

The sequence in the IS–MPR space:

  1. Supply shock lowers inflation.
  2. Taylor rule → CB lowers nominal rate → movement down along the MPR curve.
  3. Lower real rate → movement along the IS curve toward higher output.

IS-MPR Dynamics after a Positive Supply Shock

Q8 — Simultaneous Equations

The increase in output creates a positive output gap. From the Phillips curve:

$\pi_t = \pi_{t-1} + \phi(\underbrace{y_t - \bar{y}}_{> 0}) + \nu_t$

The positive output gap pushes inflation back up, partially offsetting the initial fall caused by the supply shock.

This shows that inflation and output are determined simultaneously by the DAD and DAS equations — they cannot be solved separately.

Q9 — DAD–DAS Diagram

The supply shock $\nu_0 = -1$ shifts the DAS curve downward (lower inflation at every output level).

The CB response (lower rates) moves the economy along the DAD curve toward higher output.

New equilibrium: lower inflation + higher output.

DAD-DAS Equilibrium Shift

Q10 — Inflation Falls Less Than the Shock

The shock alone would reduce inflation by 1 pp. But the CB's response increases output above potential, which generates upward pressure on inflation through the Phillips curve.

These two effects partially cancel out:

$\underbrace{\nu_0 = -1}_{\text{pushes } \pi \downarrow} + \underbrace{\phi(y_0 - \bar{y}) > 0}_{\text{pushes } \pi \uparrow} \implies |\Delta\pi| < 1$

Graphically: DAS shifts down, but the economy moves along DAD, so the equilibrium inflation change is smaller than the vertical shift of DAS.

Exercise 3

Supply Shock Dynamics

Continue from Exercise 2. Assume $\nu_t = 0$ for $t \geq 1$ (the shock disappears).

  1. Trace out the path of $y_t$, $\pi_t$, $r_t$, and $i_t$ for periods 1, 2, and 3.
  2. Explain the mechanism that brings inflation back toward target.
  3. Why must the central bank create an expansion to stabilise inflation?
  4. Sketch the time paths of all four variables from period 0 through convergence.
Show Solution
(a) — Dynamic Path

Period 0 (shock period)

The negative supply shock lowers inflation immediately. The CB cuts rates → real rate falls → output rises above potential.

$\pi_0 \downarrow \qquad i_0 \downarrow \qquad r_0 \downarrow \qquad y_0 \uparrow$

Period 1

The shock disappears ($\nu_1 = 0$), but inflation was low in period 0, so with adaptive expectations $\pi_1$ is still below target. The CB keeps rates relatively low. Output remains above potential but less than in period 0.

The positive output gap pushes inflation back up through the Phillips curve.

Period 2

Inflation continues moving toward target. As $\pi_t$ rises, the Taylor rule raises $i_t$ and $r_t$ gradually. Output moves closer to potential.

Period 3+

Adjustment continues. All variables converge back to long-run equilibrium:

$y \to 100 \qquad \pi \to 2 \qquad r \to 2 \qquad i \to 4$
(b) — Mechanism Bringing Inflation Back

After the shock, inflation falls below target. The CB lowers rates, which creates a positive output gap. Through the Phillips curve:

$\pi_t = \pi_{t-1} + \phi(\underbrace{y_t - \bar{y}}_{> 0})$

The positive output gap gradually pushes inflation upward each period until it returns to the target.

(c) — Why an Expansion Is Necessary

If the CB did nothing (kept $y_t = \bar{y}$), the Phillips curve would become:

$\pi_t = \pi_{t-1} + \phi(0) = \pi_{t-1}$

Inflation would stay permanently stuck below target.

To bring inflation back up, the CB must create a temporary expansion ($y_t > \bar{y}$). The positive output gap is the only mechanism that pushes inflation upward when there are no supply shocks.

(d) — Time Paths

Inflation $\pi_t$

Sharp drop in period 0 → then gradual rise back to $\pi^* = 2$ (V-shaped recovery).

Output $y_t$

Jumps above 100 in period 0 → then slowly returns to $\bar{y} = 100$ (temporary boom).

Real Rate $r_t$

Falls below $\rho = 2$ in period 0 → gradually rises back to 2.

Nominal Rate $i_t$

Falls below 4 in period 0 → gradually rises back to 4.

Exercise 4

Central Bank Trade-Off

Following a positive supply shock, the central bank faces a trade-off between inflation stability and output stability.

  1. Could the central bank completely offset the impact of a supply shock on inflation? If so, how? If not, why not?
  2. What would be the cost (in terms of output) of preventing any increase in inflation from a 1 pp supply shock? (Use $\phi = 0.25$)
  3. Compare a weak reaction ($\theta_\pi = 0.5$) vs. a strong reaction ($\theta_\pi = 1.5$), keeping $\theta_y = 0.5$. Capture both in DAD–DAS diagrams.
Show Solution
Q1 — Can the CB Fully Offset?

Yes, in theory. From the Phillips curve:

$\pi_t = \pi_{t-1} + \phi(y_t - \bar{y}) + \nu_t$

To keep inflation constant ($\pi_t = \pi_{t-1}$), we need:

$\phi(y_t - \bar{y}) + \nu_t = 0 \implies y_t - \bar{y} = -\frac{\nu_t}{\phi}$

The CB must create an output gap that exactly offsets the supply shock. However, this may require very large changes in output, which can be extremely costly. In practice, CBs usually allow some temporary change in inflation.

Q2 — Output Cost of Fully Stabilising Inflation

For a 1 pp positive supply shock ($\nu_t = 1$) with $\phi = 0.25$:

$y_t - \bar{y} = -\frac{1}{0.25} = \boxed{-4}$

Output must fall 4 units below potential — a severe recession — just to prevent inflation from rising by 1 pp.

This is the sacrifice ratio: to eliminate 1 pp of inflation, the economy must give up 4 units of output.

Q3 — Weak vs. Strong Reaction
DAD Slope

$\text{slope} = -\frac{\alpha\theta_\pi}{1 + \alpha\theta_y}$

Weak: $\theta_\pi = 0.5$

Slope $= -\frac{0.5}{1.5} \approx -0.33$

DAD is steep. After a supply shock:

  • Inflation changes more
  • Output changes less

→ CB tolerates more inflation variation to protect output.

Strong: $\theta_\pi = 1.5$

Slope $= -\frac{1.5}{1.5} = -1$

DAD is flat. After a supply shock:

  • Inflation changes less
  • Output fluctuates more

→ CB aggressively fights inflation at the cost of output volatility.

[À INSÉRER] Two DAD–DAS diagrams: flat DAD (weak) vs steep DAD (strong)

The Trade-Off

After a supply shock, the CB cannot stabilise both inflation and output at the same time.
Flat DAD → more inflation variation, less output variation
Steep DAD → less inflation variation, more output variation
